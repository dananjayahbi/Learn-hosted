Marketing Channels

Introduction

You've been growing through word-of-mouth and direct sales, but to scale beyond product-market fit, you need diversified customer acquisition channels. This chapter covers how to identify, test, and scale marketing channels that drive predictable, profitable growth.

Key Concepts

1. The Marketing Channel Lifecycle

Stage 1: Experimental (Month 1-3)

  • Small budget ($500-$2,000/month)
  • Test channel viability
  • Learn mechanics
  • Measure basic metrics

Stage 2: Validation (Month 4-6)

  • Increase budget (2-3x)
  • Optimize for efficiency
  • Establish CAC baseline
  • Prove ROI

Stage 3: Scaling (Month 7-12)

  • Significant budget increase
  • Build processes and systems
  • Hire specialists
  • Drive meaningful volume

Stage 4: Optimization (Ongoing)

  • Continuous improvement
  • Test variations
  • Expand within channel
  • Maximize ROI

Don't Skip Stages! Many companies scale too fast and burn money.

2. The Bullseye Framework (from "Traction")

Outer Ring: All possible channels (19 total) Middle Ring: Promising channels (3-5 to test) Bullseye: Your #1 channel (focus here) The Process: 1. Brainstorm: List all possible channels 2. Rank: Score each on potential 3. Prioritize: Pick top 3-5 to test 4. Test: Run small experiments 5. Focus: Double down on what works Common Mistake: Trying too many channels at once. Pick 1-2 to master first.

3. The 19 Traction Channels

We'll cover the most relevant for B2B SaaS: 1. Content Marketing 2. Search Engine Marketing (SEM/PPC) 3. Social & Display Ads 4. Sales (Outbound) 5. SEO 6. Partnerships (covered in previous chapter) 7. Community Building 8. Events & Trade Shows 9. Email Marketing 10. PR & Media 11. Viral/Referral Programs 12. Account-Based Marketing (ABM) Others (less common for B2B SaaS):

  • Offline ads
  • Affiliate marketing
  • Existing platforms
  • Business development
  • Engineering as marketing
  • Unconventional PR

4. Channel-Product Fit

Not all channels work for all products. Questions to Ask:

  • Where does our target customer spend time?
  • What's our price point? (High = outbound sales, Low = self-serve)
  • How complex is our sale? (Complex = content + sales, Simple = ads)
  • What's our LTV? (High LTV = higher CAC acceptable)
  • How urgent is the need? (High urgency = search ads work well)

Example: CoodLoom (Restaurant POS)

  • Good Channels: Outbound sales, trade shows, content, partnerships
  • Maybe Channels: Google Ads, SEO, email, events
  • Poor Channels: Facebook/Instagram ads, PR, viral

Why?

  • Restaurants aren't urgently searching for POS online
  • Decision is considered, not impulsive
  • High LTV supports outbound sales
  • Trust matters (trade shows, content, referrals work)

Channel Deep Dives

Channel 1: Content Marketing

What It Is: Creating valuable content that attracts and educates your target audience. Types of Content:

  • Blog posts: SEO-optimized articles
  • Guides & eBooks: Lead magnets
  • Case studies: Proof of value
  • Webinars: Educational sessions
  • Videos: Product demos, tutorials
  • Podcasts: Industry discussions
  • Tools & calculators: Interactive content

For CoodLoom:

  • "Ultimate Guide to Choosing a Restaurant POS"
  • "10 Ways to Reduce Food Waste in Your Restaurant"
  • "Restaurant Profit Margin Calculator"
  • Case Study: "How [Restaurant] Increased Sales 30% with CoodLoom"
  • Webinar: "Modern Restaurant Technology Stack"

Pros:

  • Builds authority and trust
  • Compounds over time
  • Supports SEO
  • Educates buyers
  • Relatively low cost

Cons:

  • Takes time to see results (6-12 months)
  • Requires consistent effort
  • Hard to directly attribute revenue
  • Need content creation skills

Getting Started: 1. Define buyer personas 2. Map content to buyer journey 3. Create editorial calendar 4. Produce 2-4 pieces per month 5. Promote via other channels 6. Measure and optimize Key Metrics:

  • Organic traffic
  • Content-influenced deals
  • Lead generation
  • SEO rankings
  • Engagement metrics

Budget: $2K-10K/month (writer + designer or in-house team)

Channel 2: Search Engine Marketing (Google Ads)

What It Is: Paying for ads on search engines when people search for relevant keywords. Types:

  • Search Ads: Text ads on Google search results
  • Display Ads: Banner ads across websites
  • Remarketing: Ads to people who visited your site

For CoodLoom:

  • Keywords: "restaurant pos system", "best pos for restaurants", "cloud pos"
  • Landing pages specific to keywords
  • Remarketing to website visitors

Pros:

  • High intent traffic (actively searching)
  • Fast results
  • Scalable
  • Measurable ROI
  • Good for competitive markets

Cons:

  • Can be expensive
  • Requires ongoing optimization
  • Competitive keywords costly
  • Stops when you stop paying

Getting Started: 1. Keyword research (use Google Keyword Planner) 2. Create targeted landing pages 3. Write compelling ad copy 4. Set daily budget ($50-100/day to start) 5. Track conversions 6. Optimize based on data Key Metrics:

  • Click-through rate (CTR): >2% is good
  • Cost per click (CPC)
  • Conversion rate: >3% is good for B2B
  • Cost per lead (CPL)
  • Cost per acquisition (CPA)
  • Return on ad spend (ROAS): >3:1 minimum

Budget: $2K-20K/month depending on market competitiveness Pro Tip: Start with branded keywords (cheap, high conversion) and expand to competitors and generic terms.

Channel 3: Sales (Outbound)

What It Is: Proactively reaching out to potential customers via email, phone, or LinkedIn. Types:

  • Cold email: Outreach to prospects via email
  • Cold calling: Phone outreach
  • LinkedIn outreach: Connect and message
  • Account-based: Target specific high-value accounts

For CoodLoom:

  • Build list of restaurants in target city
  • Personalized email sequences
  • Call restaurants directly
  • LinkedIn to owner/manager
  • Visit restaurants in person

Pros:

  • High control
  • Fast feedback
  • Direct conversations
  • Scalable with team
  • Works for high-ticket products

Cons:

  • Time-intensive
  • Rejection heavy
  • Requires thick skin
  • Scales linearly with team

Getting Started: 1. Build target list (use databases, scrapers, manual research) 2. Craft outreach sequence (email + phone + LinkedIn) 3. Use tools (Outreach.io, SalesLoft, Apollo) 4. A/B test messaging 5. Track response and conversion rates 6. Hire SDRs as you scale Outbound Email Sequence Example: Email 1 (Day 0): Problem + value prop

Channel 4: SEO (Search Engine Optimization)

What It Is: Optimizing your website to rank higher in organic search results. Types:

  • On-page SEO: Optimizing your website
  • Off-page SEO: Building backlinks
  • Technical SEO: Site speed, structure
  • Local SEO: Google My Business, local directories

For CoodLoom:

  • Target keywords: "restaurant pos", "best pos for small restaurants"
  • Blog content for long-tail keywords
  • Local pages for each city
  • Get mentioned by restaurant blogs/directories
  • Customer reviews on Google

Pros:

  • Free traffic once ranked
  • Compounds over time
  • High-intent visitors
  • Credibility boost

Cons:

  • Takes 6-12 months to see results
  • Competitive keywords hard to rank
  • Requires ongoing effort
  • Google algorithm changes

Getting Started: 1. Keyword research (target low-competition keywords first) 2. Optimize website structure 3. Create high-quality content 4. Build backlinks (guest posts, partnerships) 5. Get customer reviews 6. Monitor rankings (use Ahrefs, SEMrush) Key Metrics:

  • Organic traffic
  • Keyword rankings
  • Domain authority
  • Backlinks acquired
  • Pages indexed
  • Conversion rate from organic

Budget: $3K-10K/month (SEO agency or in-house specialist) Quick Wins:

  • Optimize title tags and meta descriptions
  • Speed up your website
  • Get listed in industry directories
  • Ask happy customers for Google reviews
  • Create comparison pages ("CoodLoom vs. [Competitor]")

Channel 5: Social & Display Ads

What It Is: Paid advertising on social platforms and across the web. Platforms:

  • LinkedIn Ads: B2B targeting (best for CoodLoom)
  • Facebook/Instagram Ads: B2C or B2B with creative
  • Twitter Ads: Tech-savvy audience
  • YouTube Ads: Video content
  • Display Networks: Banner ads across websites

For CoodLoom:

  • LinkedIn ads targeting "restaurant owner", "restaurant manager"
  • Facebook ads for restaurant groups/pages
  • Retargeting website visitors
  • Video testimonials from restaurant owners

Pros:

  • Precise targeting
  • Visual storytelling
  • Retargeting capabilities
  • Good for brand awareness

Cons:

  • Lower intent than search
  • Can be expensive (esp. LinkedIn)
  • Requires creative assets
  • Ad fatigue over time

Getting Started: 1. Choose platform (LinkedIn for B2B restaurant) 2. Define audience (job title, interests, behaviors) 3. Create ad creative (images, video, copy) 4. Set budget ($30-50/day minimum) 5. A/B test ads 6. Retarget engaged visitors Key Metrics:

  • Impressions & reach
  • Click-through rate (CTR)
  • Cost per click (CPC)
  • Cost per lead (CPL)
  • Conversion rate
  • Return on ad spend (ROAS)

Budget: $2K-10K/month LinkedIn Ads for B2B:

  • More expensive ($5-15 CPC)
  • But higher quality leads
  • Target specific job titles
  • Use lead gen forms

Channel 6: Community Building

What It Is: Creating or participating in communities where your customers gather. Types:

  • Online forums: Reddit, Facebook Groups
  • Your own community: Slack, Circle, Discord
  • User groups: Monthly meetups
  • Industry associations: Sponsorships, memberships

For CoodLoom:

  • Join restaurant owner Facebook groups
  • Create "CoodLoom Restaurant Owners" community
  • Sponsor local restaurant association
  • Host monthly "Restaurant Tech Meetup"
  • Reddit r/restaurateur participation

Pros:

  • Deep customer relationships
  • Feedback and insights
  • Word-of-mouth amplification
  • Customer retention tool

Cons:

  • Time-intensive
  • Hard to measure ROI
  • Slow to build
  • Can't be overly promotional

Getting Started: 1. Identify where customers already gather 2. Join and participate (don't sell immediately) 3. Provide value first 4. Gradually introduce your product 5. Consider building your own community Key Metrics:

  • Community size
  • Engagement rate
  • Leads from community
  • Customer retention (community members vs. non-members)
  • Net Promoter Score (NPS)

Budget: $1K-5K/month (community manager time + tools)

Channel 7: Events & Trade Shows

What It Is: In-person events where you can meet customers face-to-face. Types:

  • Trade shows: Industry events (restaurant industry shows)
  • Conferences: Speaking opportunities
  • Hosted events: Your own events
  • Workshops: Educational sessions
  • Dinners: Intimate gatherings

For CoodLoom:

  • National Restaurant Association Show
  • Regional restaurant trade shows
  • Host "Restaurant Technology Workshop"
  • Local restaurant owner dinners
  • Speaking at hospitality conferences

Pros:

  • Face-to-face relationship building
  • Build trust quickly
  • Multiple conversations in short time
  • Industry visibility
  • Pipeline acceleration

Cons:

  • Expensive ($5K-50K per event)
  • Time-intensive
  • Limited reach
  • Hard to measure ROI
  • Travel required

Getting Started: 1. Research relevant industry events 2. Attend first as visitor (scout) 3. Exhibit at smaller regional shows 4. Track leads and conversions 5. Scale to national shows if ROI positive Trade Show Booth Strategy:

  • Clear, simple messaging
  • Live product demo
  • Engaging booth design
  • Lead capture system (iPad with form)
  • Follow-up plan
  • Giveaways/incentives

Key Metrics:

  • Leads collected
  • Cost per lead
  • Meeting scheduled rate
  • Opportunity rate
  • Close rate
  • ROI per event

Budget: $5K-50K per major event (booth + travel + materials)

Channel 8: Email Marketing

What It Is: Sending targeted emails to prospects and customers. Types:

  • Lead nurture: Educational email sequences
  • Newsletter: Regular updates
  • Product announcements: New features
  • Event invitations: Webinars, workshops
  • Re-engagement: Win-back campaigns

For CoodLoom:

  • Weekly "Restaurant Growth Tips" newsletter
  • Lead nurture sequence for demo signups
  • Customer onboarding emails
  • Feature announcement emails
  • Case study spotlights

Pros:

  • Direct to inbox
  • High ROI ($42 for every $1 spent)
  • Segmentation and personalization
  • Owned channel (not platform-dependent)

Cons:

  • Need email list
  • Deliverability challenges
  • Can feel spammy if done wrong
  • List decay over time

Getting Started: 1. Choose email platform (Mailchimp, SendGrid, HubSpot) 2. Build email list (lead magnets, website forms) 3. Create welcome sequence 4. Send regular newsletter 5. Segment based on behavior 6. A/B test subject lines and content Email Sequence for Demo Requests: Day 0: Welcome + confirm demo time Day 1: What to expect in demo Day 3: Case study Day 7: Pre-demo questionnaire Demo Day: Join link + agenda Day After: Thank you + next steps Day 3 After: Proposal Day 7 After: Follow-up Key Metrics:

  • List growth rate
  • Open rate: 20-30% is good
  • Click rate: 2-5% is good
  • Unsubscribe rate: <0.5%
  • Conversion rate
  • Revenue per email

Budget: $100-$1K/month (email platform + copywriting)

Channel 9: PR & Media

What It Is: Getting featured in publications, podcasts, and media outlets. Types:

  • Press releases: Company announcements
  • Media coverage: Articles about your company
  • Podcast appearances: Industry podcasts
  • Awards: Industry recognition
  • Thought leadership: Op-eds, expert quotes

For CoodLoom:

  • Local business press (new funding, major customer)
  • Restaurant industry publications
  • Tech news (TechCrunch, VentureBeat)
  • Restaurant podcasts
  • "Best Restaurant POS" awards

Pros:

  • Credibility and trust
  • Reach large audience
  • SEO benefits (backlinks)
  • Often free

Cons:

  • Hard to control message
  • Unpredictable results
  • Time-intensive
  • Indirect impact on sales

Getting Started: 1. Create press kit (company info, photos, bios) 2. Build media list (relevant journalists, podcasters) 3. Pitch newsworthy stories 4. Be responsive and helpful 5. Build relationships with journalists Pitch-Worthy Stories:

  • Funding announcements
  • Major customer wins
  • Product launches
  • Industry trends/data
  • Contrarian viewpoints
  • Company milestones

Key Metrics:

  • Media mentions
  • Reach/impressions
  • Website traffic from media
  • Backlinks acquired
  • Brand awareness lift

Budget: $2K-10K/month (PR agency) or in-house effort

Channel 10: Referral Programs

What It Is: Incentivizing existing customers to refer new customers. Types:

  • Customer referrals: Customers refer peers
  • Partner referrals: Partners refer customers
  • Affiliate programs: Commission-based referrals
  • Influencer programs: Industry influencers recommend you

For CoodLoom:

  • "Refer a Restaurant, Get $500"
  • Give referee first month free
  • Partner referral program (consultants, suppliers)
  • Restaurant influencer partnerships

Pros:

  • High-quality leads (warm intro)
  • Lower CAC
  • Leverages existing customers
  • Scalable

Cons:

  • Requires happy customers
  • Fraud risk
  • May cannibalize organic referrals
  • Requires incentive budget

Getting Started: 1. Ensure product is loved (NPS >50) 2. Define referral incentive 3. Build referral mechanism (link, code) 4. Promote to customers 5. Track and reward referrals Referral Program Structure: For Referrer:

  • $500 credit or cash per successful referral
  • Unlimited referrals
  • Paid after referee pays for 3 months

For Referee:

  • First month free ($99 value)
  • Expedited onboarding
  • Special support

Key Metrics:

  • Referral rate (% of customers who refer)
  • Referred customers per referrer
  • Referral conversion rate
  • CAC for referred customers
  • LTV of referred customers
  • Viral coefficient (K-factor)

Budget: Cost of incentives + platform ($100-500/month for referral software)

Channel 11: Account-Based Marketing (ABM)

What It Is: Targeting specific high-value accounts with personalized marketing. Best For: Enterprise sales, high LTV customers, specific target accounts. For CoodLoom:

  • Target restaurant chains (50+ locations)
  • Research and personalize outreach
  • Multi-touch campaigns (email, ads, direct mail, events)
  • Coordinate sales and marketing

Tactics:

  • LinkedIn ads to specific companies
  • Personalized direct mail
  • Custom landing pages per account
  • Executive dinners
  • Custom demos/proposals

Pros:

  • Higher conversion for enterprise
  • Better resource allocation
  • Personalized approach
  • Sales and marketing alignment

Cons:

  • Resource-intensive
  • Requires coordination
  • Longer sales cycles
  • Not suitable for all businesses

Getting Started: 1. Define ideal customer profile (ICP) 2. Build target account list (50-100) 3. Research each account 4. Create personalized campaigns 5. Coordinate with sales 6. Multi-touch outreach Key Metrics:

  • Account engagement
  • Pipeline from target accounts
  • Deal size
  • Win rate
  • Sales cycle length

Budget: $5K-20K/month (tools + campaigns)

Building Your Marketing Mix

The 70-20-10 Rule

70% of budget: Proven channels that work 20% of budget: Promising channels to scale 10% of budget: Experimental channels Example for CoodLoom at $50K/month marketing budget:

  • Proven ($35K): Outbound sales ($20K), Trade shows ($15K)
  • Scaling ($10K): Content marketing ($5K), Google Ads ($5K)
  • Experimental ($5K): LinkedIn ads ($2.5K), Community ($2.5K)

Testing New Channels

The 90-Day Channel Test: Month 1: Setup

  • Learn channel mechanics
  • Set up tools/accounts
  • Create initial campaigns
  • Establish baseline metrics

Month 2: Optimization

  • Analyze initial results
  • A/B test variations
  • Increase budget slightly
  • Refine targeting

Month 3: Evaluation

  • Calculate true CAC
  • Assess scalability
  • Compare to other channels
  • Decide: Scale, Optimize, or Kill

Go/No-Go Criteria:

  • CAC < 1/3 of LTV
  • Positive ROI within 12 months
  • Scalable (can 10x spend?)
  • Repeatable (not one-time fluke?)

Measuring Marketing Performance

Key Marketing Metrics

Traffic Metrics:

  • Website visitors
  • Traffic sources
  • Bounce rate
  • Time on site

Lead Metrics:

  • Leads generated
  • Lead quality (MQL vs. SQL)
  • Lead-to-customer conversion rate
  • Cost per lead (CPL)

Customer Acquisition:

  • Customer acquisition cost (CAC)
  • CAC by channel
  • CAC payback period
  • LTV:CAC ratio

Revenue Metrics:

  • Marketing-influenced revenue
  • Marketing-sourced revenue
  • Return on marketing investment (ROMI)
  • Revenue per channel

Channel Efficiency:

  • Cost per channel
  • Conversions per channel
  • ROI per channel
  • Contribution margin

Marketing Attribution

Models: First-Touch: Credit to first interaction

  • Simple but ignores nurture

Last-Touch: Credit to last interaction

  • Simple but ignores awareness

Linear: Equal credit to all touches

  • Fair but not realistic

Time-Decay: More credit to recent touches

  • Better for long sales cycles

Position-Based: 40% first, 40% last, 20% middle

  • Balanced approach

Custom: Your own model

  • Most accurate but complex

Recommendation for Most SaaS: Start with First-Touch and Last-Touch, then evolve to multi-touch.

Common Marketing Mistakes

1. Trying Too Many Channels

Mistake: Spreading budget across 10 channels Problem: Nothing gets enough attention to work Solution: Focus on 2-3 channels, master them

2. Scaling Before Validation

Mistake: Throwing $50K at Google Ads immediately Problem: Burning money on unoptimized campaigns Solution: Test with small budget, optimize, then scale

3. Ignoring CAC

Mistake: Celebrating customer wins without knowing cost Problem: Unsustainable growth, running out of money Solution: Track CAC religiously, compare to LTV

4. Short-Term Thinking

Mistake: Expecting immediate results from SEO or content Problem: Killing channels too early Solution: Match expectations to channel (SEO = 6-12 months)

5. No Attribution

Mistake: Not tracking where customers come from Problem: Can't optimize, don't know what works Solution: Implement attribution from day one

6. Set and Forget

Mistake: Launching campaigns and not optimizing Problem: Declining performance over time Solution: Weekly optimization, monthly reviews

7. Neglecting Existing Customers

Mistake: All budget on acquisition, none on retention Problem: Leaky bucket - can't grow if churning Solution: Balance acquisition with customer marketing

Case Studies

Case Study 1: HubSpot - Inbound Marketing Pioneer

Channels: Content, SEO, Free Tools Strategy:

  • Created massive content library (1000s of blog posts)
  • Free tools (Website Grader, Marketing Grader)
  • Free CRM to drive upgrades
  • Educational approach

Results:

  • Millions of organic visitors/month
  • 150K+ customers
  • $2B+ revenue
  • Defined "inbound marketing" category

Key Lessons:

  • Content and SEO compound over time
  • Free tools drive acquisition
  • Education-first approach builds trust
  • Long-term investment pays off

Case Study 2: Drift - Conversational Marketing

Channels: ABM, Community, Events Strategy:

  • Created conversational marketing category
  • Built community (Drift Insider)
  • Major events (Hypergrowth conference)
  • ABM for enterprise

Results:

  • 5,000+ customers
  • $150M+ revenue
  • Industry thought leader
  • Acquired for $1B

Key Lessons:

  • Create category, don't compete
  • Community drives loyalty
  • Events for brand building
  • ABM works for enterprise

Case Study 3: Gong - Content + Outbound

Channels: Content, Outbound, Community Strategy:

  • Data-driven content (analyzing millions of sales calls)
  • Aggressive outbound sales
  • Gong Labs community
  • Thought leadership

Results:

  • 3,000+ customers
  • $200M+ ARR
  • $7.25B valuation
  • Market leader

Key Lessons:

  • Data makes content unique
  • Outbound + content = powerful
  • Community builds moat
  • Focus on enterprise

Key Takeaways

  • Diversify marketing channels for predictable growth
  • Use Bullseye Framework: test 3-5, focus on 1-2 winners
  • Match channels to your product and customer
  • Test channels with 90-day experiments before scaling
  • Track CAC and ROI for every channel
  • 70-20-10 rule: proven, scaling, experimental
  • Content and SEO compound over time
  • Outbound sales works well for B2B high-ticket
  • Events and community build deep relationships
  • Don't try too many channels - focus is key
