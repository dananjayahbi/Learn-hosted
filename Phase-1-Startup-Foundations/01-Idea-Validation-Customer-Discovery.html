Idea Validation & Customer Discovery

Introduction

You've built a POS system. But is it a viable business? This chapter teaches you the systematic process of validating that CoodLoom solves real problems people will pay for. You'll learn customer discovery techniques used by successful startups to avoid building something nobody wants. Why This Matters: 42% of startups fail because there's no market need for their product. Let's make sure CoodLoom isn't one of them.

Key Concepts

1. Problem-Solution Fit vs Product-Market Fit

Problem-Solution Fit (You are here):

  • Does your solution actually solve the problem?
  • Do customers recognize they have this problem?
  • Is the problem painful enough that they'll pay to solve it?

Product-Market Fit (Later stage):

  • Customers are actively using and loving your product
  • Word-of-mouth growth happening
  • Hard to keep up with demand

Your Current State:

  • ✓ You have existing customers using your POS
  • ? Have you validated they'd pay market rates?
  • ? Do they actively recommend it to others?
  • ? Would they be upset if you shut it down?

2. The Mom Test

Bad Question: "Do you think my POS system is a good idea?"

  • People will lie to be nice
  • Hypothetical answers are worthless
  • Compliments aren't commitments

Good Questions:

  • "Tell me about the last time you had issues with your current POS?"
  • "How much did that problem cost you?"
  • "What have you tried to solve this?"
  • "If I could solve this, what would you be willing to pay?"

The Rule: Talk about their life, not your idea.

3. Customer Discovery Framework

Problem → Solution → Value → Willingness to Pay → Commitment

Stage 1: Problem Discovery

  • What problems do restaurants/retailers face with POS systems?
  • How painful are these problems? (Scale 1-10)
  • What's the cost of the problem? (Time/Money/Frustration)
  • What are they doing now to solve it?

Stage 2: Solution Discovery

  • Does your solution actually solve their problem?
  • Is it better than current alternatives?
  • What's the learning curve?
  • What features matter most?

Stage 3: Value Discovery

  • How much value does your solution create?
  • Can you quantify it? (Save $X, Make $X more, Save X hours)
  • Is this value immediate or long-term?

Stage 4: Willingness to Pay

  • What would they pay for this value?
  • How do they currently budget for POS?
  • What price seems too cheap? Too expensive? Just right?

Stage 5: Commitment

  • Will they sign up for early access?
  • Will they provide a testimonial?
  • Will they refer others?
  • Will they pre-pay or commit to a contract?

Your Current Situation Analysis

What You Know

  • ✓ You have a working POS system
  • ✓ You have existing customers using it
  • ✓ You have a co-founder with capital

What You Need to Validate

  • ? Are customers paying market rates or "friend prices"?
  • ? Would they still use CoodLoom if you raised prices?
  • ? What specific features make them use your system?
  • ? What features are they asking for?
  • ? Would they recommend CoodLoom to others?
  • ? What would make them switch to a competitor?
  • ? What's their biggest pain point your system solves?

Step 2: Create Interview Guide

Goal: Understand their problems, not pitch your solution. Introduction (1 minute):

"Hi [Name], thanks for taking time. I'm doing research to understand
                        
challenges with POS systems. I'm NOT trying to sell you anything today. I just want to learn about your experience. Should take about 20-30 minutes. Sound good?"
Background Questions (5 minutes): 1. Tell me about your business. How long have you been operating? 2. Walk me through a typical transaction at your business. 3. What systems do you use daily? (POS, inventory, accounting, etc.) 4. How many staff members use the POS? Problem Discovery (10 minutes): 5. What's the most frustrating thing about your current POS? 6. Tell me about the last time your POS caused a problem.

  • What happened?
  • How much did it cost you? (time, money, customers)
  • How did you resolve it?

7. What features do you wish your POS had? 8. What do you spend the most time doing with your POS? 9. If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing, what would it be? Current Solution (5 minutes): 10. What POS system are you using now? 11. How did you choose it? 12. What do you love about it? 13. What do you hate about it? 14. How much does it cost you monthly? (subscription + processing fees) 15. Have you considered switching? Why/why not? Value & Pricing (5 minutes): 16. If a POS could [solve their #1 problem], what would that be worth to you? 17. What's your current monthly POS budget? 18. What price would seem too cheap (suspicious)? 19. What price would be too expensive (not even consider)? 20. What price would be expensive but you'd consider it? Commitment & Next Steps (3 minutes): 21. If I built something that solved [their problem], would you be interested in trying it? 22. Would you be willing to be a beta customer? 23. Do you know other [business type] owners with similar problems? 24. Can I follow up with you in a few weeks? Wrap Up:

"Thank you so much. This has been incredibly helpful. Can I send you
                        
a summary of what I learned? And if I build something, would you be open to seeing it?"

Step 7: Validate Willingness to Pay

Pricing Validation Strategies: Strategy 1: Direct Ask In interviews, after establishing value:

"If this solution could [solve their problem] and [deliver specific value],
                        
what would you expect to pay monthly?"
Strategy 2: Price Anchoring Show three options:

  • "Would you pay $50/month?"
  • "How about $150/month?"
  • "What about $300/month?"

Watch their reaction. Where do they hesitate? Where do they nod? Strategy 3: Comparison

"Square charges $60/month + processing fees.
                        
Toast is around $165/month for their plan. Given what we discussed, where would you expect CoodLoom to fall?"
Strategy 4: Pre-Orders/Deposits The ultimate validation:

"We're launching in 2 months. For early adopters, we're offering
                        
[discount/bonus]. Would you be interested in reserving your spot with a $[amount] deposit?"
Money talks. If people won't commit money, they don't see value.

Validation Criteria

Minimum Validation Thresholds

Before proceeding to build a business, you should have: ✓ Problem Validation:

  • [ ] 70%+ of interviewees have the problem you're solving
  • [ ] Average pain level is 7+ out of 10
  • [ ] Problem occurs frequently (weekly or more)
  • [ ] Current solutions are inadequate

✓ Solution Validation:

  • [ ] 60%+ of interviewees say your solution would solve their problem
  • [ ] 40%+ prefer your approach over current solution
  • [ ] No major objections you can't overcome

✓ Willingness to Pay:

  • [ ] 50%+ would pay your target price
  • [ ] Target price > your costs (with margin)
  • [ ] LTV (Lifetime Value) > 3x CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)

✓ Commitment:

  • [ ] 30%+ willing to be beta users
  • [ ] 10%+ willing to pre-pay or commit
  • [ ] 20%+ willing to refer others

If You Don't Hit These Numbers:

  • Red Flag: Your idea may not be viable
  • Yellow Flag: You may be targeting the wrong customer segment
  • Green Flag: Pivot your positioning, pricing, or target market

Common Validation Mistakes

Mistake 1: Confirmation Bias

Problem: Only talking to people who will say nice things Solution: Actively seek critics. Talk to competitor customers.

Mistake 2: Pitching Instead of Listening

Problem: Spending the interview explaining your solution Solution: Follow the 80/20 rule - They talk 80%, you listen 20%

Mistake 3: Asking Hypothetical Questions

Problem: "Would you use..." "Would you pay..." are worthless Solution: Ask about past behavior and actual commitments

Mistake 4: Talking Only to Friends

Problem: Friends will lie to be supportive Solution: 80%+ of interviews should be strangers

Mistake 5: Ignoring Negative Feedback

Problem: Dismissing criticism as "they don't get it" Solution: If 5+ people say the same thing, they're probably right

Mistake 6: Stopping Too Early

Problem: Talking to only 3-5 people Solution: Minimum 20 interviews. More is better.

Mistake 7: Not Documenting

Problem: Relying on memory, losing insights Solution: Take notes during, summarize after every interview

Mistake 8: Analysis Paralysis

Problem: Endlessly researching, never launching Solution: Set a deadline. After 20 interviews, make a decision.

Validation Frameworks

The Pivot or Persevere Framework

After Your 20 Interviews: PERSEVERE if:

  • ✓ Clear, frequent, painful problem identified
  • ✓ Your solution resonates with target customers
  • ✓ Willingness to pay at viable price point
  • ✓ Identifiable, reachable customer segment
  • ✓ You're excited and passionate about solving this

PIVOT if:

  • ✗ Problem isn't that painful
  • ✗ Your solution doesn't excite people
  • ✗ Can't get to profitable unit economics
  • ✗ Can't find enough customers
  • ✗ You're forcing yourself to care

POSSIBLE PIVOTS: Customer Pivot: Different customer segment

  • Example: From restaurants to retail stores

Problem Pivot: Different problem for same customer

  • Example: From inventory to customer loyalty

Feature Pivot: Different main feature

  • Example: From POS to online ordering

Value Capture Pivot: Different pricing model

  • Example: From subscription to transaction fees

Channel Pivot: Different sales approach

  • Example: From direct sales to partnerships

The Traction Validation Framework

Weak Signals (Interesting but not enough):

  • People say they like your idea
  • People say they would use it
  • People give you feature suggestions
  • People connect you to others

Strong Signals (This is real):

  • People offer to pay you
  • People sign LOIs (Letter of Intent)
  • People agree to pilot/beta with commitment
  • People actively refer specific contacts
  • People ask "when can I start?"

Very Strong Signals (You've got something):

  • People pre-pay
  • People sign contracts
  • People use your product regularly
  • People refer others without being asked
  • You have a waitlist

Measuring Success

Validation Scorecard

After completing your 20 interviews, score yourself: Problem Validation (0-25 points):

  • Problem frequency: _____/10 (70%+ = 10 pts)
  • Pain level: _____/10 (7+ avg = 10 pts)
  • Current solutions inadequate: _____/5 (Yes = 5 pts)

Solution Validation (0-25 points):

  • Solution resonance: _____/10 (60%+ = 10 pts)
  • Preference over current: _____/10 (40%+ = 10 pts)
  • Objection handling: _____/5 (Can overcome = 5 pts)

Market Validation (0-25 points):

  • Willingness to pay: _____/10 (50%+ = 10 pts)
  • Target price viable: _____/10 (Yes = 10 pts)
  • Unit economics work: _____/5 (LTV > 3x CAC = 5 pts)

Commitment Validation (0-25 points):

  • Beta users: _____/10 (30%+ = 10 pts)
  • Pre-pay/commit: _____/10 (10%+ = 10 pts)
  • Referral willingness: _____/5 (20%+ = 5 pts)

TOTAL SCORE: _____/100 Scoring Guide:

  • 80-100: Strong validation - Build it!
  • 60-79: Moderate validation - Needs refinement
  • 40-59: Weak validation - Consider pivot
  • 0-39: Not validated - Major pivot or abandon

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Successful Validation

Company: Toast (Restaurant POS)

  • Discovery: Restaurants hated outdated, complex POS systems
  • Validation: Talked to 100+ restaurants, 80% complained about same issues
  • Commitment: Got 10 restaurants to pilot at full price
  • Result: Now worth $5B+

What They Did Right:

  • Extensive customer discovery
  • Found severe, frequent pain
  • Validated willingness to pay
  • Got real commitments

Example 2: Failed Validation (Learned Early)

Company: Anonymous POS Startup

  • Discovery: Thought mobile POS for events would be huge
  • Validation: Event organizers said "nice to have" not "must have"
  • Pivot: Switched to festivals (higher volume, more pain)
  • Result: Found product-market fit in new segment

What They Did Right:

  • Recognized weak signals
  • Pivoted before wasting resources
  • Found better segment

Company: CoodLoom (You)

  • Validation Needed:
    • Will customers pay market rates?
    • Can you find 100+ similar customers?

Key Takeaways

✓ Don't assume - Validate: Your existing customers don't prove market viability ✓ Talk to Strangers: Friends and current customers will be too nice ✓ Listen More, Talk Less: This is research, not sales ✓ Look for Patterns: One customer's problem might be unique, 10 customers' problem is a market ✓ Measure Commitment: Words are cheap, money talks ✓ Be Willing to Pivot: Better to pivot early than fail slowly ✓ Document Everything: Your memory will fail you ✓ Set a Deadline: Don't validate forever, make a decision
