M&A Strategy (Mergers & Acquisitions)

Introduction

You've built a growing company. Now you're considering acquisitions to accelerate growth, or maybe someone wants to acquire you. M&A can be a powerful growth strategy or a lucrative exit. This chapter covers both sides: buying companies and being acquired.

Key Concepts

1. Why Companies Do M&A

Strategic Reasons:

  • Acquire Technology: Get product/tech faster than building
  • Acquire Talent: Hire a whole team at once ("acqui-hire")
  • Acquire Customers: Buy customer base
  • Enter New Markets: Geographic or vertical expansion
  • Eliminate Competition: Remove competitor
  • Consolidate Market: Roll up fragmented industry
  • Vertical Integration: Own more of value chain

Financial Reasons:

  • Accelerate Growth: Faster than organic
  • Economies of Scale: Lower per-unit costs
  • Increase Valuation: 1+1=3 synergies
  • Tax Benefits: Utilize losses
  • Return on Cash: Deploy excess capital

For CoodLoom Examples:

  • Acquire competitor in new city (customers + market entry)
  • Acquire complementary product (inventory management software)
  • Acquire implementation agency (services revenue + customers)
  • Get acquired by payment processor (exit for founders)

2. Types of Acquisitions

Horizontal Acquisition:

  • Buy competitor or similar company
  • Example: CoodLoom acquires another restaurant POS
  • Rationale: Market share, eliminate competition

Vertical Acquisition:

  • Buy supplier or distributor
  • Example: CoodLoom acquires payment processor
  • Rationale: Own more value chain, better margins

Acqui-hire:

  • Buy for team, not product
  • Example: Acquire agency with POS implementation expertise
  • Rationale: Hard to hire, faster team building

Product Acquisition:

  • Buy for technology/product
  • Example: Acquire inventory management software
  • Rationale: Faster than building, proven solution

Customer Acquisition:

  • Buy for customer base
  • Example: Acquire legacy POS with 500 customers
  • Rationale: Cheaper than organic customer acquisition

Roll-up:

  • Buy multiple small companies, consolidate
  • Example: Acquire 10 regional POS companies
  • Rationale: Fragmented market, economies of scale

3. Acquisition Success Factors

Successful Acquisitions Have:

  • ✓ Clear strategic rationale
  • ✓ Cultural fit
  • ✓ Reasonable price
  • ✓ Integration plan
  • ✓ Key talent retention
  • ✓ Complementary strengths

Failed Acquisitions Often:

  • ✗ Overpay significantly
  • ✗ Cultural clash
  • ✗ No integration plan
  • ✗ Key people leave
  • ✗ Distraction from core business
  • ✗ Hidden problems emerge

Statistics: 70-90% of acquisitions fail to create value. Be careful!

4. Valuation Basics

How to Value an Acquisition Target: For Revenue-Generating SaaS:

Valuation = ARR × Multiple
                        
Multiples vary by:

                        
    
                        
  • Growth rate (faster = higher)

    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Retention (higher = higher)
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Market (hot market = higher)

Typical Range: 3-10x ARR

  • Growth: 50% YoY
  • Profitable: No, burning $200K/year
  • Retention: 85%

  • Asset-based (value of assets minus liabilities)
  • DCF (discounted cash flow - future earnings)
  • Comparable transactions (what similar companies sold for)

Acquiring Companies (You as Buyer)

When to Make Acquisitions

Good Time to Acquire:

  • ✓ You have strong core business
  • ✓ Clear strategic need
  • ✓ Capital available (raised or profitable)
  • ✓ Management bandwidth to integrate
  • ✓ Found compelling target

Bad Time to Acquire:

  • ✗ Core business struggling
  • ✗ "Because we can" (no strategy)
  • ✗ No capital or would strain finances
  • ✗ Team is already underwater
  • ✗ Just window shopping

Rule of Thumb: Core business must be healthy before doing M&A.

Finding Acquisition Targets

Sources: 1. Direct Outreach

  • Identify targets (competitors, complementary products)
  • Reach out to founders
  • "Not for sale" today, but plant seed

2. Investment Bankers

  • They represent sellers
  • Will bring you deals
  • Take 3-10% commission (seller pays)

3. Business Brokers

  • For smaller deals (<$5M)
  • Platforms: BizBuySell, Flippa

4. Your Network

  • Other founders
  • Investors
  • Advisors
  • Industry contacts

5. Distressed Assets

  • Companies running out of cash
  • Can negotiate better price
  • But higher risk (reason for distress?)

Building Your Target List: Criteria:

  • Strategic fit (product, market, customers)
  • Size (can you afford it?)
  • Technology (compatible?)
  • Team (want to retain?)
  • Customers (would they stay?)
  • Culture (compatible?)

Example for CoodLoom: | Company | Type | ARR | Rationale | Fit | |---|---|---|---|---| | QuickPOS | Competitor | $2M | 300 customers in Texas | High | | InventoryPro | Product | $500K | Add-on product | Medium | | Resto-Tech Agency | Services | $1M | Implementation services | Medium |

  • Gauge interest in acquisition
  • High-level discussion
  • Sign NDA

  • Review basic financials

  • Non-binding offer
  • Price range
  • Key terms
  • Exclusivity period (30-60 days)
  • Contingent on diligence

Stage 4: Due Diligence (Week 7-12) Financial Diligence:

  • Verify revenue and expenses
  • Customer contracts review
  • Accounts receivable/payable
  • Tax returns
  • Liabilities and debts
  • Working capital needs

Legal Diligence:

  • Corporate documents
  • Material contracts
  • IP ownership
  • Employment agreements
  • Litigation history
  • Compliance with laws

Technical Diligence:

  • Code review
  • Architecture assessment
  • Security audit
  • Technical debt
  • Scalability
  • Integration complexity

Customer Diligence:

  • Retention analysis
  • Customer concentration
  • NPS/satisfaction
  • Support tickets
  • Churn reasons

Team Diligence:

  • Key person risk
  • Retention plan
  • Culture assessment
  • Compensation review

Red Flags:

  • Revenue declining
  • Major customer concentration (>20% from one customer)
  • Legal issues (lawsuits, IP disputes)
  • Technical debt (code is mess)
  • Key people planning to leave
  • Hidden liabilities
  • Overstated financials

Stage 5: Definitive Agreement (Week 13-16)

  • Final purchase price
  • Detailed terms
  • Representations and warranties
  • Indemnification
  • Closing conditions
  • Legal documentation (100+ pages!)

Use Lawyer! M&A deals are complex legally. Stage 6: Closing (Week 17+)

  • All conditions satisfied
  • Board approval
  • Regulatory approval (if needed)
  • Wire transfer
  • Announce deal
  • Integration begins

Total Timeline: 3-6 months (can be faster for small deals)

Structuring the Deal

Purchase Price Components: 1. Cash (Upfront)

  • Paid at closing
  • Simplest, cleanest
  • Requires capital

2. Stock (Equity)

  • Buyer's company stock
  • Aligns interests long-term
  • Tax-efficient for seller
  • Vests over time (typically 2-4 years)

3. Earnout (Contingent)

  • Additional payment based on future performance
  • Example: $1M upfront + $500K if hit revenue target
  • Bridges valuation gap
  • Can cause conflict (seller wants to hit target, buyer wants conservative)

4. Seller Financing

  • Seller receives payments over time
  • Like loan from seller
  • Good if buyer lacks capital
  • Risk for seller

Typical Mix for CoodLoom Acquiring $2M ARR Company:

  • Cash: $2M (50%)
  • Stock: $1M (25%, vests over 2 years)
  • Earnout: $1M (25%, if 90% customer retention over 2 years)
  • Total: $4M

Asset Purchase vs. Stock Purchase: Asset Purchase (Common for smaller deals):

  • Buyer purchases specific assets (customer list, IP, contracts)
  • Doesn't assume liabilities
  • More tax-efficient for buyer
  • Cleaner

Stock Purchase (Common for larger deals):

  • Buyer purchases company stock
  • Assumes all liabilities
  • Tax-efficient for seller
  • Simpler legally

For most SaaS acquisitions: Asset purchase is preferred.

Integration Planning

Pre-Close (Before deal closes):

  • Form integration team
  • Identify quick wins
  • Retention plan for key people
  • Communication plan
  • Technical integration plan

Day 1 (Closing day):

  • Announce to teams
  • Announce to customers
  • FAQ ready
  • IT access granted
  • Welcome package

Week 1:

  • Team meetings
  • Customer communication
  • Immediate integration steps
  • Address urgent issues

Month 1:

  • Product integration plan
  • Customer migration plan
  • Team integration
  • Cultural integration

Month 3:

  • Majority of integration complete
  • Synergies being realized
  • Team stabilized
  • Customers transitioned

Month 6:

  • Fully integrated
  • Hitting synergy targets
  • Post-mortem: what went well/poorly

Common Integration Mistakes:

  • Moving too fast (confusion)
  • Moving too slow (uncertainty)
  • Ignoring culture
  • Not communicating enough
  • Losing key people
  • Alienating customers

Retaining Key People

Golden Handcuffs: Retention Bonuses:

  • Pay key people to stay
  • Example: $50K bonus if stay 1 year
  • Vests over time

Equity Grants:

  • New stock options in acquiring company
  • Aligns with new company's success
  • Typically 2-4 year vesting

Employment Contracts:

  • Minimum employment period
  • Non-compete clauses
  • Clear role and compensation

Example Retention Package:

  • Founder: 2-year employment, $150K salary, $200K stock vesting over 2 years
  • CTO: 1-year employment, $130K salary, $100K stock vesting
  • Key Engineers: $25K retention bonus each after 1 year

But: Best retention is making them excited about the future!

Being Acquired (You as Seller)

Why Founders Sell

Financial Reasons:

  • Cash out (liquidity)
  • Valuation is attractive
  • Risk reduction
  • Can't raise more capital

Strategic Reasons:

  • Larger platform to grow on
  • Access to resources (capital, team, distribution)
  • Better for customers
  • Tired of running company

Personal Reasons:

  • Want to work on something else
  • Health or family reasons
  • Burnout
  • Partner conflict

Market Reasons:

  • Competitor is consolidating market
  • Industry is declining
  • Better to sell now than later

No "Right" Reason: It's your company, your decision.

When to Sell

Good Time to Sell:

  • ✓ Strong growth trajectory
  • ✓ Profitable or clear path to profitability
  • ✓ Market is hot (high valuations)
  • ✓ You have leverage (multiple bidders)
  • ✓ Personal readiness

Bad Time to Sell:

  • ✗ Desperate (out of cash)
  • ✗ Declining business
  • ✗ No alternatives
  • ✗ Market is cold

Rule: Sell from position of strength, not desperation.

Preparing for Sale

6-12 Months Before: Clean Up Financials:

  • Proper accounting (GAAP)
  • Audited financials (if possible)
  • Clean up personal expenses
  • Maximize EBITDA
  • Clear financials documentation

Clean Up Legal:

  • Incorporate properly
  • Clean cap table
  • All IP owned by company
  • Contracts in order
  • No pending litigation

Optimize Business:

  • Reduce customer concentration
  • Improve retention
  • Document processes
  • Reduce key person dependency
  • Achieve key milestones

Build Data Room:

  • Financial statements (3 years)
  • Customer data and contracts
  • Employee agreements
  • Legal documents
  • Product documentation
  • Metrics and KPIs

Position for Exit:

  • Hit growth targets
  • Sign marquee customers
  • Close strategic partnerships
  • Build strong team
  • Generate acquisition interest

Finding Buyers

Types of Buyers: 1. Strategic Acquirers

  • Companies in your industry
  • Want your product, customers, or market
  • Pay highest prices (strategic value)
  • Examples: Salesforce, Intuit, Oracle

2. Financial Buyers (Private Equity)

  • PE firms, search funds
  • Buy to hold and grow, then resell
  • Pay moderate prices (financial model)
  • Examples: Vista Equity, Thoma Bravo

3. Competitors

  • Direct competitors
  • Want to eliminate competition or consolidate
  • Pay varies
  • Antitrust concerns possible

4. Adjacent Companies

  • Complementary products
  • Want to expand offering
  • Pay moderate to high

Building Buyer Interest: Inbound:

  • Grow fast (attracts attention)
  • PR and industry presence
  • Relationships with corporate dev teams
  • Investors can introduce
  • "Not for sale" often generates interest

Outbound:

  • Hire investment banker
  • Identify 50-100 potential acquirers
  • Confidential outreach
  • Competitive process
  • Multiple bidders = higher price

Investment Bankers:

  • Specialized M&A advisors
  • Find buyers, manage process, negotiate
  • Fee: 3-10% of deal (higher % for smaller deals)
  • Worth it for deals >$5M

The Sale Process (Seller Side)

Stage 1: Preparation (1-2 months)

  • Hire banker (optional but recommended)
  • Prepare materials (CIM, data room)
  • Identify target buyers
  • Set expectations

CIM (Confidential Information Memorandum):

  • 30-50 page document
  • Company overview
  • Market opportunity
  • Financial performance
  • Growth strategy
  • Management team
  • Investment highlights

Stage 2: Buyer Outreach (1 month)

  • Teaser sent to 50-100 potential buyers
  • Interested parties sign NDA
  • Send CIM
  • Initial calls

Stage 3: First Round Bids (1 month)

  • Interested buyers submit IOIs (Indications of Interest)
  • Non-binding offers
  • Typical: Start with 50 → 20 interested → 5-10 IOIs

Stage 4: Management Presentations (1 month)

  • Top 5-10 bidders
  • In-person presentations
  • Q&A sessions
  • Site visits

Stage 5: LOIs (2-4 weeks)

  • Top 2-3 bidders submit binding LOIs
  • Negotiate terms
  • Choose winner (or walk away)
  • Grant exclusivity

Stage 6: Due Diligence (2-3 months)

  • Buyer investigates everything
  • Open data room
  • Answer questions
  • Negotiate adjustments

Stage 7: Definitive Agreement (1 month)

  • Final terms negotiated
  • Legal documents finalized
  • Board approval
  • Shareholder approval

Stage 8: Closing (1-2 weeks)

  • Final conditions satisfied
  • Wire transfer
  • Announce publicly
  • Pop champagne! 🍾

Total Timeline: 6-9 months

Sale Price Negotiation

Understanding Your Worth: For SaaS Companies:

Valuation = ARR × Multiple × Adjustment Factors
                        
Base Multiple:

                        
    
                        
  • $0-$1M ARR: 3-5x
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • $1M-$5M ARR: 4-7x
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • $5M-$20M ARR: 6-10x
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • $20M+ ARR: 8-15x
    • 
                        
                        

                        
Adjustment Factors: + Fast growth (>50% YoY): +20-50% + Profitable: +10-30% + High retention (>95%): +10-20% + Large market: +10-20% + Strategic buyer: +20-100%

                        
    
                        
  • Slow growth (<20% YoY): -20-40%
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Unprofitable with high burn: -10-30%
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • High churn (>10% annually): -20-40%
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Customer concentration: -10-30%

Example for CoodLoom:

  • ARR: $5M
  • Growth: 60% YoY
  • Churn: 8% annually (okay, not great)
  • Burn: $100K/month
  • Base Multiple: 6-7x
  • Growth adjustment: +30%
  • Churn adjustment: -10%
  • Effective Multiple: 7x
  • Valuation: $35M

Negotiating Tips: Have Leverage:

  • Multiple bidders (best leverage)
  • Not desperate (can walk away)
  • Business is growing
  • Other options (continue building, raise capital)

Focus on Total Deal Value:

  • Not just headline number
  • Consider: Cash, stock, earnouts, employment
  • Employment terms (salary, duration, role)

Understand Earnouts:

  • Earnouts are risky (you may not get them)
  • Prefer cash and stock over earnouts
  • If earnout, make targets achievable
  • Get control over earnout metrics

Representations and Warranties:

  • You guarantee things about the business
  • Breaches can reduce purchase price
  • Indemnification cap (limit liability)
  • Escrow (10-20% held back 12-18 months)

Tax Optimization:

  • Asset sale vs. stock sale (different tax treatment)
  • QSBS exemption (can exclude capital gains)
  • Installment sale (spread taxes over years)
  • Consult tax advisor!

Life After Acquisition

What to Expect: First 3 Months:

  • Integration chaos
  • Lots of meetings
  • Culture shock
  • Second-guessing decision (normal!)

First Year:

  • Learning new company
  • Less autonomy than before
  • Politics and bureaucracy
  • But: More resources, bigger platform

Common Emotional Journey: 1. Excitement (sold!) 2. Honeymoon (first month) 3. Reality (this is different) 4. Frustration (I miss my company) 5. Adaptation (finding new role) 6. Departure or acceptance Earnout Period:

  • Hitting earnout targets
  • Conflict over decisions
  • Pressure to perform
  • Different priorities

Post-Earnout:

  • Many founders leave after earnout
  • Some stay if loving new role
  • Some fired if not working out
  • Plan for next chapter

Financial Freedom:

  • You have money now
  • Tax considerations
  • Investment strategy
  • What's next?

Alternatives to Selling

Before you sell, consider: 1. Raise Capital

  • Grow faster with funding
  • Retain control
  • Build to larger exit

2. Recapitalization

  • PE buys majority, you keep some
  • Take some money off table
  • Continue building

3. Stay Independent

  • Build lifestyle business
  • Control your destiny
  • Dividends for income

4. Hire CEO

  • Step back from operations
  • Stay on board
  • Work on what you love

5. Merge with Competitor

  • Equal merger
  • Combine forces
  • Larger company together

Not selling is okay! Don't sell just because you can.

M&A Mistakes to Avoid

For Buyers

1. Overpaying

  • Emotional bidding war
  • Overestimating synergies
  • Solution: Walk away if price too high

2. Skipping Due Diligence

  • Rushing to close
  • Missing problems
  • Solution: Thorough diligence always

3. No Integration Plan

  • "We'll figure it out"
  • Chaos after close
  • Solution: Plan before closing

4. Losing Key People

  • No retention plan
  • Team leaves
  • Solution: Golden handcuffs

5. Alienating Customers

  • Poor communication
  • Forced migrations
  • Solution: Customer-centric integration

For Sellers

1. Selling Too Early

  • Leaving money on table
  • Could have grown more
  • Solution: Sell from strength

2. Selling Too Late

  • Business declining
  • No leverage
  • Solution: Know when to exit

3. Taking First Offer

  • No competitive process
  • Lower price
  • Solution: Create competition

4. Not Understanding Terms

  • Focus only on headline price
  • Bad earnout terms
  • Solution: Read everything, use lawyer

5. Poor Earnout Planning

  • Unrealistic targets
  • Loss of control
  • Solution: Negotiate carefully or avoid

Case Studies

Case Study 1: Instagram → Facebook ($1B)

Context: 2012, Instagram 13 employees, 30M users, no revenue Deal:

  • Acquisition: $1B ($300M cash, $700M stock)
  • Founders stayed 4+ years
  • Operated independently

Outcome:

  • Instagram now worth $100B+
  • Founders made billions
  • Validated mobile social betting

Lessons:

  • Strategic value >> financial value
  • Independence helped retention
  • Stock value appreciated massively

Case Study 2: Zapier Stays Independent

Context: Growing automation platform, $100M+ ARR Decision:

  • Turned down acquisition offers
  • Raised no VC capital
  • Bootstrapped to profitability
  • Founder retains control

Outcome:

  • $100M+ ARR
  • Profitable
  • 700+ employees
  • Founders wealthy from dividends

Lessons:

  • Selling is not mandatory
  • Independence is valuable
  • Profitability > growth at all costs
  • Alternative path to success

Case Study 3: WhatsApp → Facebook ($19B)

Context: 2014, 55 employees, 450M users, $20M revenue Deal:

  • Acquisition: $19B ($4B cash, $12B stock, $3B RSUs)
  • Founders stayed (then left 4 years later)
  • Operated independently

Outcome:

  • One of largest tech acquisitions ever
  • 2B+ users now
  • Founder made $9B personally

Lessons:

  • User base valued > revenue
  • Stock component can be huge
  • Even big deals have earnouts (RSUs)
  • Founders often leave after earnout

Case Study 4: Mailchimp Stays Independent, Then Sells ($12B)

Context: Bootstrapped email marketing, $800M revenue, profitable Journey:

  • 2001: Founded, bootstrapped
  • 2019: $700M revenue, no outside capital
  • 2021: Sold to Intuit for $12B
  • Founders owned 100% (no investors!)

Outcome:

  • Founders made $12B (no dilution!)
  • 20-year journey
  • Largest bootstrapped exit ever

Lessons:

  • Patience pays off
  • Profitability = optionality
  • Don't need investors to build big
  • Sold on own terms at peak

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ M&A can accelerate growth or provide exit
  • ✓ 70-90% of acquisitions fail - be selective
  • ✓ As buyer: Buy for strategic reasons, not just growth
  • ✓ As buyer: Cultural fit and integration plan are critical
  • ✓ As seller: Sell from position of strength, not desperation
  • ✓ As seller: Create competitive process for higher price
  • ✓ Valuation is art + science, mostly negotiation
  • ✓ Focus on total deal value, not just headline
  • ✓ Use experienced lawyers and bankers
  • ✓ Alternatives to M&A exist - consider them
