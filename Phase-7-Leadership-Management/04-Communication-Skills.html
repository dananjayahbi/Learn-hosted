Communication Skills

Introduction

As CEO, communication is your most-used skill. You'll communicate vision to investors, strategy to the team, value to customers, and expectations to stakeholders. Poor communication creates confusion, misalignment, and wasted effort. Great communication multiplies your impact and accelerates CoodLoom's success.

The Communication Pyramid

▲
                        
/ \ /   \ /     \ /  WHY  \           (Vision, Purpose) /---------\ /           \ /    WHAT     \        (Strategy, Goals) /---------------\ /                 \ /       HOW         \     (Plans, Tactics) /---------------------\ Start with WHY, work down to HOW
Most people communicate backwards (starting with HOW). Great leaders start with WHY.

Example: Launching New Feature

Bad (HOW first): "We're going to spend the next 6 weeks building a new dashboard with real-time analytics, user segmentation, and export functionality using React and our new API architecture." Good (WHY first): "Our customers are making decisions blind because they can't see their content performance in real-time. We're going to change that by building a dashboard that shows them exactly what's working, so they can create better content and get better results. This will take 6 weeks and use our new API architecture. Questions?"

The Core Communication Principles

1. Clarity Above All

The Curse of Knowledge: You know more than your audience. What's obvious to you is confusing to them. How to Be Clear:

Use Simple Words:
                        
❌ "We're leveraging synergistic paradigms to optimize our go-to-market strategy" ✓ "We're testing what works and doubling down on it" Use Concrete Examples: ❌ "We need to improve customer satisfaction" ✓ "We need to respond to support tickets in under 4 hours instead of 24" Use Numbers: ❌ "We're growing fast" ✓ "We're growing 15% month-over-month" Use Stories: ❌ "CoodLoom helps businesses" ✓ "Sarah saved 20 hours last week using CoodLoom to automate her content planning"
The 5th Grader Test: Can a smart 5th grader understand you? If not, simplify.

2. Consistency Is King

The Rule of 7: People need to hear something 7 times before it sticks. What This Means:

Week 1: Share the vision in all-hands
                        
Week 2: Reference it in 1-on-1s Week 3: Write about it in company update Week 4: Use it in customer call Week 5: Post about it on social media Week 6: Include it in investor update Week 7: Bring it up in team lunch By Week 7, people are finally internalizing it.
Consistency Across Channels:

  • Same message to team, investors, customers
  • Same core values in every decision
  • Same priorities repeated constantly
  • Same vision reinforced everywhere

CoodLoom Example: If your vision is "Make AI content creation effortless," every communication should tie back:

  • Product decisions: "This makes AI content easier"
  • Hiring: "Looking for people who obsess over user experience"
  • Marketing: "See how easy it is..."
  • Customer success: "Let's make this even easier for you"

3. Transparency Builds Trust

What to Share:

✓ Progress toward goals (good and bad)
                        
✓ Major decisions and reasoning ✓ Challenges facing the company ✓ Financial situation (high level) ✓ Strategic changes ✓ Mistakes you made
What to Keep Private:

✗ Individual performance issues
                        
✗ Personal information without consent ✗ Confidential customer/partner info ✗ Sensitive legal matters ✗ Information that could cause panic without context
The Transparency Test: "Would I be upset if this was shared publicly?" If yes, think carefully about sharing internally.

4. Adapt to Your Audience

Different Stakeholders Need Different Communication: Team:

  • What: Day-to-day operations, specific tasks
  • How Often: Daily/weekly
  • Style: Collaborative, detailed, two-way
  • Medium: Slack, meetings, 1-on-1s

Investors:

  • What: Metrics, strategy, risks/opportunities
  • How Often: Monthly/quarterly
  • Style: Professional, data-driven, confident
  • Medium: Email updates, board meetings

Customers:

  • What: Value, benefits, how-to, support
  • How Often: As needed
  • Style: Helpful, simple, customer-centric
  • Medium: Email, chat, documentation

Partners:

  • What: Mutual benefits, collaboration opportunities
  • How Often: Quarterly
  • Style: Win-win, strategic, professional
  • Medium: Email, calls, meetings

5. Listen More Than You Talk

The 70/30 Rule: Listen 70%, talk 30%. Active Listening:

Do:
                        
✓ Make eye contact ✓ Nod and show you're engaged ✓ Ask clarifying questions ✓ Paraphrase to confirm understanding ✓ Take notes ✓ Wait for them to finish before responding ✓ Put away phone/laptop Don't: ✗ Interrupt ✗ Think about your response while they're talking ✗ Check phone ✗ Finish their sentences ✗ Dismiss their concerns ✗ Change the subject
Powerful Listening Questions:

"Tell me more about that."
                        
"What else?" "How did that make you feel?" "What do you think we should do?" "What am I missing?" "Help me understand..."

Written Communication

The Email Framework

The BLUF Method (Bottom Line Up Front - Military technique):

Structure:
                        
1. Subject: Clear, specific (not "Update") 2. BLUF: Main point in first sentence 3. Context: Why this matters 4. Details: Supporting information 5. Action: What you need Example: Subject: Decision Needed: EU Market Entry by Friday [BLUF] We need to decide by Friday whether to enter the EU market in Q3. [Context] We have 50+ inbound requests from EU customers, but it requires localization work and EU support hours. [Details]

                        
    
                        
  • Revenue potential: $200k ARR in first year
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Cost: $80k (translation + 1 EU support hire)
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Timeline: 12 weeks to launch
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Risk: Spreads team thin during US growth
    • 
                        
                        

                        
[Action] Please review attached analysis and share your vote by Friday 3pm. We'll discuss in Friday's meeting. Thanks, [Your name]
Email Best Practices:

✓ Subject lines that tell the story
                        
✓ First sentence with main point ✓ Keep it under 5 paragraphs ✓ Use bullet points ✓ Bold key information ✓ One clear call-to-action ✓ Respond within 24 hours (even if just "Got it, will respond fully by Thursday") ✗ Vague subjects ("Quick question") ✗ Burying the lead ✗ Wall of text ✗ Multiple unrelated topics ✗ Passive-aggressive tone ✗ Unclear what you need ✗ Going radio silent

Difficult Conversations: Giving Critical Feedback:

1. Request permission:
                        
"Can I share some feedback on the presentation?" 2. Be specific (SBI Model): Situation: "In today's client call..." Behavior: "...you interrupted the client 3 times..." Impact: "...which made them defensive and we lost momentum." 3. Make it about behavior, not person: ✓ "The interruptions were problematic" ✗ "You're disrespectful" 4. Future-focused: "Next time, try pausing and asking questions instead. What do you think?" 5. End positively: "I know you can nail this. Let's practice before the next call."
Receiving Feedback:

1. Don't defend or justify
                        
2. Say "Thank you for telling me" 3. Ask clarifying questions 4. Reflect: "Let me think about this" 5. Follow up: Show you took it seriously

Presentations

The Steve Jobs Presentation Formula:

1. The Hook (30 seconds):
                        
"Today, we're going to solve a problem that's costing businesses $10 billion per year." 2. The Problem (2 minutes): Paint the pain. Make them feel it. "Every marketer spends 15 hours per week on content planning. That's 40% of their time on repetitive work instead of strategy." 3. The Solution (5 minutes): Your product/idea. Show, don't tell. [Demo the product] "See how CoodLoom reduced that 15 hours to 2 hours?" 4. The Benefits (2 minutes): What changes for them. "This means: More time for strategy, 50% more content output, better results." 5. The Proof (3 minutes): Social proof, data, testimonials. "50 companies are already using this. Here's what they're saying..." 6. The Call to Action (1 minute): What you want them to do. "Start your free trial today at CoodLoom.com" Total: 13-14 minutes Leave time for questions
Presentation Tips:

Content:
                        
✓ One main idea per slide ✓ Big, clear headlines ✓ Minimal text (the slide is not your notes) ✓ High-quality images ✓ Simple, clean design ✓ Dark text on light background (or vice versa) ✓ Practice 10+ times Delivery: ✓ Make eye contact ✓ Move with purpose (not pacing) ✓ Vary your tone and pace ✓ Pause for effect ✓ Tell stories ✓ Show genuine enthusiasm ✓ Handle questions confidently Avoid: ✗ Reading from slides ✗ Turning your back to audience ✗ Monotone delivery ✗ Saying "um" constantly (pause instead) ✗ Apologizing for being nervous ✗ Going over time ✗ Complicated slides with tiny text

Storytelling

Why Stories Matter

Facts vs Stories:

Fact: "CoodLoom increased productivity by 86%"
                        
→ Forgettable Story: "Meet Sarah. She's a marketing director at a 50-person SaaS company. Every Monday, she spent 4 hours planning social media content for the week. She was stressed, frustrated, and falling behind. Then she tried CoodLoom. The AI understood her brand voice, suggested content ideas based on her goals, and created a week's worth of posts in 30 minutes. Now, she spends those 4 hours on strategy instead. She got a promotion last month." → Memorable
Stories are:

  • Easier to remember
  • More persuasive
  • Emotionally engaging
  • Shareable

The Story Structure

The Hero's Journey (Simplified):

1. Status Quo
                        
"Sarah was a marketing director, drowning in content work" 2. Problem/Challenge "She spent 4 hours every Monday on social planning, falling behind" 3. The Encounter (Your Product) "Then she discovered CoodLoom" 4. The Transformation "AI helped her create a week's content in 30 minutes" 5. The New Reality "Now she focuses on strategy. Got promoted." 6. The Lesson "That's the power of the right tools"
Types of Stories to Tell: Customer Success Stories:

  • How a customer achieved results
  • Specific numbers and outcomes
  • Their words, not yours
  • Use in: Sales, marketing, all-hands, fundraising

Founder Story:

  • Why you started CoodLoom
  • Personal connection to problem
  • The "aha" moment
  • Use in: Pitches, recruiting, press

Company Origin Story:

  • How CoodLoom came to be
  • Early struggles and wins
  • The team's journey
  • Use in: Culture building, recruiting

Failure Stories:

  • What went wrong and what you learned
  • Show vulnerability and growth
  • Build psychological safety
  • Use in: All-hands, 1-on-1s, retrospectives

1. Acknowledge the issue
                        
"We're aware of the outage affecting login" 2. Say what you're doing "Team is investigating and will update every 30 minutes" 3. Set expectations "We expect resolution within 2 hours" 4. Apologize if appropriate "We apologize for the disruption"

  • ✓ Practice makes better: Communication is a skill, not a talent
