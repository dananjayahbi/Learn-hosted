Exit Strategy

Introduction

"Exit strategy" doesn't mean quitting—it means understanding your options for realizing the value you've created. Whether through IPO, acquisition, or building a generational company, having clarity on your end game shapes your journey. This chapter covers all exit paths and how to maximize value.

Key Concepts

1. What is an Exit Strategy?

Definition: A plan for how founders and investors will eventually realize financial returns from the business. Common Misconception: Exit = Failure or giving up Reality: Exit = Strategic milestone and value realization The Three Main Paths: 1. IPO (Initial Public Offering) - Go public 2. Acquisition - Sell to larger company 3. No Exit - Build generational/lifestyle business Your Choice Depends On:

  • Personal goals and vision
  • Company trajectory and potential
  • Market conditions
  • Investor expectations (if you raise VC)
  • Competitive landscape

2. Why Think About Exit Now?

"Begin with the end in mind" - Stephen Covey Exit Strategy Shapes Everything: Fundraising Decisions:

  • VC money = Pressure for big exit ($100M+)
  • Bootstrapped = Full control of destiny
  • Strategic investors = May acquire you

Product Decisions:

  • Build to sell = Focus on acquirer's needs
  • Build to IPO = Focus on growth and scale
  • Build to hold = Focus on profitability and sustainability

Team Decisions:

  • Build for scale = Hire professional managers
  • Build for acquisition = Keep lean and nimble
  • Build to hold = Build culture for long-term

Financial Decisions:

  • Exit path = Maximize growth (burn OK)
  • Hold path = Maximize profit (no burn)

You Don't Have to Decide Today

  • But understand options
  • Make decisions that don't close doors
  • Maintain optionality

3. Exit Timeline Expectations

Typical Timeline by Path: VC-Backed IPO:

  • Timeline: 7-10 years
  • Size: $100M+ revenue typically required
  • Path: Seed → Series A → B → C → IPO
  • Example: Toast (POS company) - Founded 2011, IPO 2021 (10 years)

Strategic Acquisition:

  • Timeline: 3-10 years
  • Size: $5M+ revenue (varies widely)
  • Path: Build valuable asset, attract acquirers
  • Example: Countless POS companies acquired at various stages

Bootstrapped to Generational:

  • Timeline: Indefinite
  • Size: Any profitable size
  • Path: Build sustainably profitable business
  • Example: Mailchimp (built over 20 years, sold for $12B in 2021)

Your CoodLoom Timeline:

  • Year 3-5: Too early (unless amazing acquisition offer)
  • Year 5-7: Potential acquisition window
  • Year 7-10: IPO possible if massive scale
  • Year 10+: Generational company option

Exit Option 1: Initial Public Offering (IPO)

What is an IPO?

Definition: Selling shares of your private company to public investors on stock exchange. The Process: 1. Company files to go public (S-1 filing) 2. Roadshow (pitch to institutional investors) 3. Price offering 4. Shares trade publicly on NYSE or NASDAQ 5. Ongoing reporting requirements (quarterly, annually) Requirements for IPO: Minimum Thresholds:

  • Revenue: Typically $100M+ (ideally $200M+)
  • Growth: 30-50%+ year-over-year
  • Profitability: Not required but helps (SaaS companies often unprofitable at IPO)
  • Market size: Multi-billion dollar TAM
  • Corporate governance: Board, audit committee, compliance

Pros of IPO: ✓ Massive Capital: Raise hundreds of millions ✓ Liquidity: Shares tradable, founders can eventually sell ✓ Valuation: Often highest valuation path ✓ Prestige: Public company status ✓ Employee Wealth: Stock options become real money ✓ Acquisition Currency: Use stock for acquisitions ✓ Brand: Legitimacy and trust Cons of IPO: ✗ Pressure: Quarterly earnings pressure ✗ Scrutiny: Public disclosure and analyst coverage ✗ Short-term Focus: Market rewards quarterly results ✗ Costs: $5-10M+ to go public, $2-5M+ annually ✗ Regulations: SOX compliance, SEC reporting ✗ Loss of Privacy: Everything disclosed ✗ Volatility: Stock price fluctuations

IPO Preparation Timeline

3-5 Years Before IPO:

  • Achieve $30-50M+ revenue
  • Strong growth trajectory (50%+)
  • Build experienced management team
  • Implement enterprise systems
  • Clean financials and accounting

2-3 Years Before:

  • $50-100M+ revenue
  • Hire CFO with public company experience
  • Implement SOX controls
  • Board with public company directors
  • Start analyst relationships

1-2 Years Before:

  • $100M+ revenue
  • Pre-IPO funding round (establish valuation)
  • Financial audit and cleanup
  • S-1 preparation
  • Investment bank selection

IPO Year:

  • File S-1 with SEC
  • Roadshow and marketing
  • Price and launch
  • Trade publicly
  • Quarterly reporting begins

IPO Success Factors

1. Revenue Scale and Growth

  • Minimum $100M, ideally $200M+ revenue
  • 40%+ Rule: Growth % + Profit % > 40%
  • Predictable recurring revenue (SaaS is good)

2. Market Opportunity

  • Large TAM ($10B+)
  • Clear path to $1B+ company
  • Growing market, not declining

3. Unit Economics

  • Strong gross margins (70%+ for SaaS)
  • Efficient customer acquisition (CAC payback <18 months)
  • High retention and expansion

4. Management Team

  • Experienced executive team
  • Public company experience
  • Credible with investors

5. Market Timing

  • IPO window open
  • Comparable companies trading well
  • Investor appetite for growth stocks

CoodLoom IPO Scenario: Requirements to Achieve:

  • $150-200M+ revenue
  • 1,000+ customers (enterprise) or 10,000+ (SMB)
  • Dominant in restaurant POS market
  • Expanding into adjacent markets
  • Strong growth and margins

Timeline: Year 8-10 at earliest Probability: Low (<5% of startups IPO)

Exit Option 2: Acquisition

Types of Acquisitions

Strategic Acquisition

  • Buyer: Larger company in your industry
  • Motivation: Product, customers, talent, or eliminate competition
  • Valuation: Typically 3-10x revenue
  • Example: Square acquires Weebly, Toast acquires smaller POS companies

Financial Acquisition

  • Buyer: Private equity firm
  • Motivation: Cash flow and growth potential
  • Valuation: Typically 5-8x EBITDA
  • Example: Vista Equity Partners buys enterprise software companies

Acqui-hire

  • Buyer: Company wanting your team
  • Motivation: Talent acquisition
  • Valuation: Low (<2x revenue, often just enough to make investors whole)
  • Example: Google/Facebook buying small startups for teams

Reverse Merger / SPAC

  • Buyer: Special Purpose Acquisition Company
  • Motivation: Take you public faster
  • Valuation: Varies (2020-2021 SPAC boom, now cooled)
  • Example: Many tech companies went public via SPAC 2020-2021

Acquisition Timeline and Process

Phase 1: Getting on the Radar (12-24 months before) Activities:

  • Build relationships with potential acquirers
  • Partnerships and integrations
  • Industry presence and visibility
  • Strong financial performance

Phase 2: Initial Interest (6-12 months before) Trigger Events:

  • Potential acquirer reaches out
  • Banker introduces buyers
  • Competitive pressure (other acquisitions)
  • Your growth inflection

Initial Steps:

  • Sign NDA
  • Preliminary discussions
  • High-level financials shared
  • Mutual fit assessment

Phase 3: Serious Courting (3-6 months) Due Diligence Begins:

  • Detailed financial review
  • Customer interviews
  • Technology audit
  • Legal review
  • Team assessment

Negotiation:

  • Valuation range
  • Deal structure (cash, stock, earnout)
  • Employment terms for founders
  • Transition plan

Phase 4: Closing (2-4 months) Final Steps:

  • Letter of Intent (LOI) signed
  • Deep due diligence
  • Final purchase agreement
  • Financing secured (if needed)
  • Regulatory approval
  • Closing and wire transfer

Total Timeline: 12-18 months from first contact to closing (typical)

Acquisition Valuation

Valuation Multiples: Revenue Multiple (Common for SaaS):

  • Early stage: 2-4x revenue
  • Growth stage: 4-8x revenue
  • Late stage / IPO: 8-15x revenue
  • Hot market: 15-25x revenue (rare)

EBITDA Multiple (Common for profitable businesses):

  • Small business: 3-5x EBITDA
  • Mid-market: 5-8x EBITDA
  • Large / strategic: 8-12x EBITDA

Factors That Increase Valuation: ✓ High growth rate (50%+) ✓ Strong retention (>90%) ✓ Efficient customer acquisition ✓ Strategic fit with buyer ✓ Multiple interested buyers ✓ Recurring revenue model ✓ Proprietary technology ✓ Large addressable market Factors That Decrease Valuation: ✗ Slowing growth ✗ High churn ✗ Customer concentration risk ✗ Outdated technology ✗ Key person dependency ✗ Legal or financial issues CoodLoom Acquisition Scenarios: Early Exit (Year 3-5):

  • Scenario: $5-10M revenue, growing 50%+
  • Buyer: Square, Toast, or regional player
  • Valuation: $20-50M (4-5x revenue)
  • Your take: Depends on ownership %

Mid-Stage Exit (Year 5-7):

  • Scenario: $20-50M revenue, market leader in niche
  • Buyer: Public POS company or private equity
  • Valuation: $100-250M (5-8x revenue)
  • Your take: Life-changing money

Late Stage Exit (Year 7-10):

  • Scenario: $100M+ revenue, industry leader
  • Buyer: Major tech company or PE firm
  • Valuation: $500M-1B+ (5-10x revenue)
  • Your take: Generational wealth

Acquisition Deal Structures

All Cash Deal:

  • Cleanest and simplest
  • Get paid at closing
  • No ongoing risk
  • Preferred by sellers

Cash + Stock Deal:

  • Partial cash, partial buyer's stock
  • Upside if buyer's stock appreciates
  • Risk if stock declines
  • Common in public company acquisitions

Cash + Earnout:

  • Base payment at close
  • Additional payment if hit milestones
  • Aligns incentives
  • Risk of not hitting targets
  • Common in growth-stage acquisitions

Example Structure:

  • $50M at close
  • $25M earnout over 3 years (if revenue targets hit)
  • Total potential: $75M

Stock + Earnout:

  • Most complex
  • Stock risk + performance risk
  • Can be very lucrative or disappointing
  • Watch out for this structure

Golden Handcuffs:

  • Employment requirement (2-4 years)
  • Retention bonuses
  • Vesting of earnout payments
  • Typical in most acquisitions

Preparing for Acquisition

Make Your Company Acquirable: 1. Clean House

  • Organized financials (audited if possible)
  • Clean cap table (ownership clear)
  • Legal compliance (contracts, IP, etc.)
  • No skeletons (lawsuits, disputes, etc.)

2. Build Strategic Value

  • Unique technology or IP
  • Large customer base
  • Strategic partnerships
  • Strong team (that will stay)

3. Create Scarcity

  • Multiple potential acquirers
  • Not desperate to sell
  • Strong performance
  • Competitive tension

4. Hire Advisors

  • M&A attorney
  • Investment banker (if large deal)
  • Accountant
  • They pay for themselves

Acquisition Negotiation Tips

Know Your Number:

  • Minimum acceptable price
  • Walk-away point
  • Ideal outcome
  • Don't share this with buyer!

Create Competition:

  • Talk to multiple buyers
  • Create time pressure
  • Auction process (if you have leverage)

Negotiate Everything:

  • Not just price
  • Employment terms
  • Earnout structure
  • Retention for team
  • Product roadmap
  • Brand usage

Understand Buyer Motivation:

  • What do they really want?
  • Is it defensive (eliminate competitor)?
  • Is it offensive (enter new market)?
  • Use this to negotiate

Watch Out For:

  • "Trailing" provisions (they come back later)
  • Earnout impossible to hit
  • Stock in illiquid company
  • Non-competes that are too broad
  • Retention requirements too long

Exit Option 3: No Exit (Generational Company)

Building to Hold Forever

The Berkshire Hathaway Model:

  • Build profitable, sustainable business
  • Don't sell (or wait decades)
  • Compound value over time
  • Enjoy cash flow and control

Why Consider This Path? Pros: ✓ Full Control: Your company, your rules ✓ Long-term Thinking: No quarterly pressure ✓ Cash Flow: Dividends and owner distributions ✓ Legacy: Build something lasting ✓ Flexibility: Change direction anytime ✓ Lifestyle: Design business around your life Cons: ✗ Liquidity: Wealth tied up in business ✗ Risk: All eggs in one basket ✗ Scale Limits: May not reach full potential ✗ Stress: Ongoing responsibility ✗ Opportunity Cost: Money locked up Famous Examples:

  • Mailchimp (bootstrapped 20 years, sold for $12B in 2021 - but could have held forever)
  • Basecamp (still independent after 20+ years)
  • Atlassian (bootstrapped to $10B+ valuation, IPO but founder control)

The Lifestyle Business Path

What is a Lifestyle Business?

  • Profitable and sustainable
  • Funds desired lifestyle
  • Not growth-at-all-costs
  • Balanced life and work

CoodLoom Lifestyle Business Scenario: Year 5 Achievement:

  • 200 customers
  • $2.5M revenue
  • $1M profit (40% margin)
  • 10 person team
  • Founders each take $300K+ salary
  • Plus distributions

Maintain This:

  • Keep growing moderately (10-20%/year)
  • Stay profitable and healthy
  • Don't overextend
  • Enjoy the business and lifestyle

By Year 10:

  • 500 customers
  • $6-8M revenue
  • $2-3M profit
  • Founders making $500K-1M/year each
  • Nice lifestyle, low stress
  • Option to sell for $15-30M if you want

This is a perfectly valid path!

Succession Planning

If You Want to Step Back (But Not Sell): Option 1: Hire CEO

  • Bring in professional CEO
  • You become Chairman
  • Stay involved strategically
  • Keep ownership
  • Get time back

Option 2: Promote from Within

  • Develop internal leader
  • Transition over time
  • Maintain culture
  • Lower risk than external hire

Option 3: Sell to Management

  • Management buyout (MBO)
  • Team acquires company
  • You get liquidity
  • Business continues

Option 4: Next Generation

  • Family business succession
  • Train kids or family
  • Multi-generational company
  • Legacy and tradition

Dividend Strategy

Creating Cash Flow: Once Profitable:

  • Retain capital for growth (first 3-5 years)
  • Start taking dividends (year 5+)
  • Balance reinvestment and distributions

Distribution Strategy:

  • Keep 6-12 months operating cash
  • Invest in strategic initiatives
  • Distribute excess to owners

Example:

  • $2M annual profit
  • Keep $500K for operations
  • Invest $500K in growth
  • Distribute $1M to owners

Over 10 years:

  • $10M+ in distributions
  • Plus company value appreciation
  • Total wealth created: $30-50M+

Making the Decision

Decision Framework

Evaluate Your Options: | Factor | IPO | Acquisition | Hold Forever | |--------|-----|-------------|--------------| | Potential Payout | Highest | Medium | Lowest | | Timeline | 8-10+ years | 5-10 years | Indefinite | | Control | Lose control | Lose control | Keep control | | Liquidity | Eventually liquid | Immediate | Over time | | Stress | Very high | Medium | Lower | | Flexibility | Low | Low | High | | Legacy | Public company | Part of bigger company | Your company | Your Personal Factors: Consider: 1. Age and Energy: How long do you want to run a company? 2. Financial Needs: Do you need liquidity soon? 3. Personal Goals: What do you want from life? 4. Family: What do they need and want? 5. Passion: Do you love the business? 6. Market: What's possible in your market? 7. Competition: Can you win long-term?

Maintaining Optionality

Keep All Doors Open: Build for Options:

  • Strong financials (appeals to all buyers)
  • Scalable systems (ready for IPO)
  • Sustainable profitability (can hold)
  • Clean cap table (easy to transact)

Don't Close Doors:

  • Taking VC = Pressure for big exit
  • Being unprofitable = Can't hold forever
  • Messy financials = Can't sell
  • Bad reputation = Limits options

Reassess Regularly:

  • Annual strategy review
  • Market conditions assessment
  • Personal goals check-in
  • Decide year-by-year

Maximizing Exit Value

Value Drivers (Regardless of Exit Path)

1. Revenue Growth

  • Faster growth = Higher multiple
  • Target: 40-50%+ annually
  • Predictable and sustainable

2. Recurring Revenue

  • SaaS model preferred
  • High visibility into future
  • Lower risk = Higher value

3. Gross Margins

  • 70-80%+ ideal for SaaS
  • Shows leverage and scalability
  • More profit to buyer

4. Customer Retention

  • >90% annual retention
  • Proves product value
  • Predictable revenue

5. Market Leadership

  • #1 or #2 in niche
  • Clear differentiation
  • Defensible position

6. Team Quality

  • Strong leadership team
  • Low key person dependency
  • Will stay after acquisition

7. Technology/IP

  • Proprietary technology
  • Patents or unique algorithms
  • Competitive moat

8. Financial Performance

  • Clean financials
  • Audited statements (if possible)
  • Efficient operations
  • Path to profitability

9. Strategic Fit

  • Right buyer at right time
  • Solves buyer's problem
  • Synergy potential

10. Deal Timing

  • Strong performance trend
  • Market momentum
  • Multiple interested parties
  • No desperation

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Three main paths: IPO, acquisition, or build to hold forever
  • ✓ IPO requires scale: $100M+ revenue, 10+ year journey
  • ✓ Acquisition most common: Wide range of buyers and valuations
  • ✓ Holding forever is valid: Can create tremendous wealth and freedom
  • ✓ Exit strategy shapes decisions: From fundraising to product to hiring
  • ✓ Maintain optionality: Don't close doors prematurely
  • ✓ Value drivers universal: Growth, margins, retention, team, market position
  • ✓ Timing matters: Right buyer at right time = Higher valuation
  • ✓ Know your number: Minimum acceptable outcome
  • ✓ It's a marathon: Building to exit takes 5-10+ years
