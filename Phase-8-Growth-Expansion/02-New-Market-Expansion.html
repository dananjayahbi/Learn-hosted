New Market Expansion

Introduction

You've proven your POS system works in your initial market. Now it's time to expand - but expansion is more than just "going to new places." This chapter covers how to systematically enter new geographic markets and vertical segments without losing focus or burning through cash.

Key Concepts

1. Geographic vs. Vertical Expansion

Geographic Expansion: Same product, new locations

  • Selling your restaurant POS to restaurants in new cities/countries
  • Similar customers, different locations
  • Lower product risk, higher operational complexity

Vertical Expansion: Same location, new customer types

  • Selling to retail stores, salons, or other business types
  • Same location, different use cases
  • Lower operational risk, higher product risk

Which First?

  • Geographic: If your product is strong and market is similar
  • Vertical: If local market is large and product is adaptable
  • Most companies: Geographic first (easier to replicate success)

2. The Beachhead Strategy

Don't try to be everywhere at once. Establish dominance in one market before expanding. Beachhead Approach: 1. Choose one initial market (your current city) 2. Dominate it completely (>10% market share) 3. Build playbook (document what works) 4. Clone to similar markets (nearby cities) 5. Iterate and improve (refine with each expansion) 6. Accelerate (faster expansion as you learn) Example: CoodLoom's Path

  • Year 1: Dominate your city (50+ restaurants)
  • Year 2: Clone to 3 similar-sized cities nearby
  • Year 3: Expand to 10 more cities using proven playbook
  • Year 4: Enter different region or country
  • Year 5: National or multi-country presence

3. Market Selection Criteria

Not all markets are created equal. Choose wisely. Evaluation Framework: | Criterion | Weight | Questions to Ask | |---|---|---| | Market Size | 25% | How many potential customers? | | Market Accessibility | 20% | Can we reach them cost-effectively? | | Competition | 20% | Who's already there? How strong? | | Cultural Fit | 15% | Do they operate similarly? | | Economic Conditions | 10% | Can they afford our product? | | Regulatory Environment | 10% | Any legal barriers? | Scoring System: Rate each 1-10, multiply by weight, sum for total score. Example: Evaluating New Cities for CoodLoom City A:

  • Size: 8 (5,000 restaurants)
  • Accessibility: 7 (3-hour drive)
  • Competition: 9 (fragmented market)
  • Cultural Fit: 9 (similar restaurant culture)
  • Economics: 8 (strong restaurant scene)
  • Regulatory: 10 (no issues)
  • Total: 8.3 ✓ Good target

City B:

  • Size: 9 (8,000 restaurants)
  • Accessibility: 4 (different country, far)
  • Competition: 5 (strong incumbent)
  • Cultural Fit: 6 (very different dining culture)
  • Economics: 7 (affordable but tight margins)
  • Regulatory: 6 (complex regulations)
  • Total: 6.3 ✗ Wait until stronger

4. Expansion Timing

Too Early Signs:

  • Core market not yet saturated
  • Product still has major issues
  • High churn in existing market
  • No repeatable sales process
  • Burning cash in current market

Too Late Signs:

  • Competitors entering "your" cities
  • Growth stalling in core market
  • Customer asking "when are you coming to X?"
  • Team capacity available
  • Strong cash position

Right Time Indicators:

  • Strong product-market fit (>80% retention)
  • Repeatable sales playbook
  • 3-6 months cash buffer
  • Proven customer success process
  • Clear expansion target identified

Geographic Expansion Strategies

Strategy 1: Concentric Circles (Ripple Effect)

Expand to adjacent, similar markets first. How It Works: 1. Start in your home city 2. Move to nearby cities (2-3 hour radius) 3. Gradually expand radius 4. Fill in gaps as you grow Advantages:

  • Similar culture and market dynamics
  • Easier to support customers (travel, time zones)
  • Can share resources across nearby markets
  • Build regional reputation

Disadvantages:

  • Slower to reach large distant markets
  • May miss strategic opportunities
  • Can feel limiting

Best For: Early-stage companies, service-heavy products, limited resources Example: CoodLoom's Concentric Expansion

  • Year 1: Your city (Portland)
  • Year 2: Salem, Eugene, Bend (2-3 hour drive)
  • Year 3: Seattle, Boise (4-5 hour drive)
  • Year 4: San Francisco, Vancouver (further)
  • Year 5: Los Angeles, Denver (major metros)

Strategy 2: Cherry Picking (Opportunistic)

Target the best markets regardless of location. How It Works: 1. Evaluate all potential markets 2. Pick top 5-10 by attractiveness 3. Enter in priority order 4. Skip less attractive markets Advantages:

  • Focus on highest-value markets
  • Faster revenue growth
  • Attract better customers and talent

Disadvantages:

  • Complex operations (scattered locations)
  • Hard to support customers
  • Expensive (travel, local presence)

Best For: High-value enterprise sales, capital-rich companies, scalable products Example: CoodLoom Cherry Picking

  • Year 1: Your city (Portland)
  • Year 2: San Francisco, New York, Chicago (major food cities)
  • Year 3: Los Angeles, Miami, Austin
  • Year 4: Fill in gaps with remote sales

Strategy 3: Regional Domination

Own an entire region before moving to the next. How It Works: 1. Define a region (e.g., Pacific Northwest) 2. Enter all cities in that region 3. Build regional brand and presence 4. Achieve #1 position in region 5. Move to next region Advantages:

  • Strong regional brand
  • Efficient operations within region
  • Economies of scale in marketing/support
  • Hard for competitors to enter

Disadvantages:

  • Requires entering some weak markets
  • Slower path to national scale
  • Resource-intensive

Best For: Companies with regional strength, brick-and-mortar elements, network effects Example: CoodLoom Regional Domination

  • Year 1-2: Pacific Northwest (OR, WA, ID)
  • Year 3-4: California
  • Year 5-6: Southwest (AZ, NM, NV, CO)
  • Year 7-8: Texas and Mountain States

Strategy 4: City-by-City Blitz

Launch intensively in one city at a time. How It Works: 1. Choose target city 2. Concentrate all resources on it 3. Launch with big marketing push 4. Sign 20-50 customers in 3-6 months 5. Move to next city Advantages:

  • Builds momentum quickly
  • Creates "buzz" in each market
  • Allows testing and iteration
  • Clear milestones

Disadvantages:

  • Requires intense focus and resources
  • All-or-nothing approach per city
  • Can be expensive

Best For: Well-funded companies, proven products, competitive markets Example: CoodLoom City Blitz

  • Q1: Target Seattle
    • Hire 2 local sales reps
    • Run local ad campaign
    • Sponsor restaurant association event
    • Goal: 30 customers
  • Q2: Target San Diego
    • Apply same playbook
    • Adjust based on Seattle learnings
    • Goal: 40 customers
  • Q3: Target Austin
    • Refined playbook
    • Goal: 50 customers

International Expansion

Taking your POS system to other countries - bigger opportunity, bigger challenges.

When to Go International

Go International If:

  • Dominated domestic market (or it's small)
  • Clear opportunity in target country
  • Product needs minimal localization
  • Resources available (capital, team)
  • Strong unit economics domestically

Wait If:

  • Still growing strongly domestically
  • Product needs significant changes
  • Limited resources
  • Complex regulatory environment
  • Negative or marginal unit economics

International Market Selection

Key Factors: 1. Market Size and Growth

  • How many potential customers?
  • Is market growing or mature?
  • Can you capture meaningful share?

2. Market Maturity

  • Do they use POS systems?
  • What's adoption rate?
  • Ahead or behind your home market?

3. Language and Culture

  • Can you operate in local language?
  • Similar business practices?
  • Cultural barriers to adoption?

4. Regulatory Environment

  • Business registration complexity
  • Data privacy laws (GDPR, etc.)
  • Payment regulations
  • Tax requirements

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Who are the incumbents?
  • Any local champions hard to displace?
  • Market share distribution

6. Economic Factors

  • GDP per capita
  • Exchange rate stability
  • Payment processing availability
  • Can customers afford your product?

7. Operational Feasibility

  • Time zone compatibility
  • Travel distance
  • Local talent availability
  • Infrastructure quality

International Expansion Models

Model 1: Remote/Export Model

  • Sell from home country
  • No local presence
  • Remote support only

Pros: Low cost, low risk Cons: Hard to build trust, limited scale Best For: Adjacent markets, initial testing Model 2: Partner/Distributor Model

  • Partner with local company
  • They sell and support
  • You provide product and brand

Pros: Local expertise, faster entry Cons: Less control, share economics Best For: Unfamiliar markets, resource constraints Model 3: Local Office Model

  • Establish local entity
  • Hire local team
  • Full presence in market

Pros: Full control, better service Cons: Expensive, complex, slower Best For: Large markets, long-term commitment Model 4: Hybrid Model

  • Start remote
  • Add local sales rep
  • Expand to full office as you grow

Pros: Balanced risk/reward Cons: Requires careful staging Best For: Most SaaS companies

Localization Requirements

Essential Localization:

  • Language translation (UI, docs, support)
  • Currency and payment processing
  • Local regulations compliance
  • Tax calculation and reporting
  • Date/time formats

Advanced Localization:

  • Local payment methods (WeChat Pay, etc.)
  • Cultural customization
  • Local integrations
  • Regional marketing
  • Local partnerships

Don't Over-Localize Early:

  • Start with essential only
  • Add based on customer feedback
  • English often sufficient for B2B SaaS
  • Technical users comfortable with English

International Expansion Playbook

Phase 1: Market Research (1-2 months)

  • Size the opportunity
  • Analyze competition
  • Identify regulatory requirements
  • Assess localization needs
  • Calculate unit economics

Phase 2: Soft Launch (2-3 months)

  • Get first 5-10 customers
  • Remote sales and support
  • Learn market dynamics
  • Test pricing
  • Validate demand

Phase 3: Committed Entry (6-12 months)

  • Hire local sales rep or country manager
  • Essential localization
  • Local marketing campaigns
  • Establish partnerships
  • Set up legal entity if needed

Phase 4: Scale (12+ months)

  • Build local team
  • Full localization
  • Regional marketing
  • Expand product offerings
  • Achieve profitability

Vertical Expansion

Selling to different types of businesses in your existing geography.

Understanding Vertical Markets

Your Core Vertical: Restaurants (table-service, QSR, cafes) Adjacent Verticals:

  • Tier 1 (Very Similar): Bars, food trucks, catering
  • Tier 2 (Somewhat Similar): Retail stores, salons, services
  • Tier 3 (Different): Manufacturing, healthcare, real estate

Start with Tier 1: Most similar needs, easiest adaptation.

Vertical Market Assessment

Questions for Each Vertical: 1. Market Size

  • How many businesses in this vertical?
  • What's the average revenue per customer?
  • Total addressable market value?

2. Product Fit

  • What % of our features are relevant?
  • What new features are required?
  • How much engineering effort?

3. Sales Process

  • Same buyer persona?
  • Similar sales cycle?
  • Can we use existing sales team?

4. Competition

  • Who are the vertical-specific competitors?
  • Any dominant incumbent?
  • What's our differentiation?

5. Economics

  • Similar pricing tolerance?
  • Comparable LTV?
  • Acceptable CAC?

Vertical Expansion Approaches

Approach 1: Horizontal First (Recommended)

  • Perfect product for core vertical
  • Expand geographically
  • Only then consider new verticals
  • Example: Dominate restaurants nationwide before entering retail

Approach 2: Multi-Vertical from Start

  • Build for multiple verticals simultaneously
  • More complex product and go-to-market
  • Higher risk but broader opportunity
  • Example: General-purpose POS for any business

Approach 3: Vertical Hopping

  • Master one vertical, then move to next
  • Build new features for each vertical
  • Multiple specialized products
  • Example: Restaurant POS, then retail POS, then salon POS

Most SaaS Success: Horizontal first, vertical later

Entering a New Vertical: Step-by-Step

Step 1: Customer Discovery (4-6 weeks)

  • Interview 20-30 businesses in target vertical
  • Understand their needs and pain points
  • Map features to requirements
  • Identify gaps in your product

Step 2: Pilot Program (2-3 months)

  • Recruit 5-10 pilot customers
  • Offer discounted pricing
  • Build minimal required features
  • Gather intensive feedback

Step 3: Product Adaptation (3-6 months)

  • Build vertical-specific features
  • Customize UI/UX for vertical
  • Create vertical-specific content
  • Train team on vertical knowledge

Step 4: Go-to-Market (6-12 months)

  • Hire vertical-specific sales rep
  • Create vertical marketing materials
  • Build case studies
  • Attend vertical-specific events
  • Launch formal offering

Step 5: Scale (12+ months)

  • Dedicated vertical team
  • Vertical-specific pricing
  • Industry partnerships
  • Become vertical leader

Building the Expansion Team

First Hire: Sales Rep

  • Local to the market
  • Industry experience
  • Hunter mentality
  • Self-sufficient

Second Hire: Customer Success Manager

  • Once you have 10-15 customers
  • Ensures retention
  • Drives expansion
  • Gathers product feedback

Third Hire: Marketing Coordinator

  • Local events and partnerships
  • Content creation
  • Community building
  • Lead generation

Later Hires:

  • Additional sales reps
  • Implementation specialists
  • Regional manager
  • Office admin

Hiring Local vs. Remote Team

Hire Locally If:

  • In-person sales important
  • Local presence matters
  • Market is large enough
  • Cultural fit is critical

Hire Remotely If:

  • Product is fully remote
  • Talent scarce in market
  • Cost savings significant
  • Company culture is remote-first

Hybrid Approach (Recommended):

  • Sales: Local (face-to-face matters)
  • Customer Success: Can be remote
  • Marketing: Hybrid (some local presence)
  • Engineering: Remote (global talent pool)

Measuring Expansion Success

Key Metrics for New Markets

Market Entry Metrics (First 6 Months):

  • Customers signed
  • Pipeline generated
  • Cost per lead
  • Sales cycle length
  • Win rate

Target Benchmarks:

  • 10 customers in 3 months (aggressive)
  • 5 customers in 3 months (conservative)
  • Sales cycle within 1.5x of core market
  • Win rate within 75% of core market

Scale Metrics (6-18 Months):

  • MRR/ARR from market
  • Customer count
  • Market share
  • Churn rate
  • Net Revenue Retention

Target Benchmarks:

  • Break even on CAC within 12 months
  • Churn rate similar to core market
  • 50+ customers by month 18
  • 5% market share in target segment

Long-Term Metrics (18+ Months):

  • Contribution margin
  • Market leadership position
  • Team productivity vs. core market
  • Brand recognition

Expansion ROI Framework

Calculate Expected ROI: Investment:

  • Team costs (salaries, recruiting)
  • Marketing spend
  • Travel and events
  • Legal and setup costs
  • Infrastructure

Returns:

  • New customer ARR
  • Expansion from existing customers
  • Market position value
  • Team and knowledge gained

Formula:

ROI = (Gross Profit from Market - Investment) / Investment

Example:

  • Investment Year 1: $300K
  • Revenue Year 1: $200K (20 customers @ $10K each)
  • Gross Profit (60% margin): $120K
  • Year 1 ROI: -60% (expected - investing phase)
  • Investment Year 2: $400K
  • Revenue Year 2: $600K (60 customers @ $10K each)
  • Gross Profit: $360K
  • Cumulative Investment: $700K
  • Cumulative Gross Profit: $480K
  • Cumulative ROI: -31% (improving)
  • Investment Year 3: $500K
  • Revenue Year 3: $1.2M (120 customers)
  • Gross Profit: $720K
  • Cumulative Investment: $1.2M
  • Cumulative Gross Profit: $1.56M
  • Cumulative ROI: +30% (positive!)

Realistic Expectations:

  • Payback period: 18-36 months
  • Initial losses are normal
  • Scale drives profitability

When to Double Down vs. Pull Back

Double Down (Accelerate) If:

  • Hitting or exceeding customer targets
  • Strong product-market fit signals
  • Positive word-of-mouth
  • Competition entering market
  • Clear path to profitability

Actions: Hire faster, increase marketing, raise prices Hold Steady If:

  • Meeting targets but not exceeding
  • Learning and improving
  • Need more time to assess
  • Cash flow is adequate

Actions: Continue plan, optimize, wait for data Pull Back (Reduce Investment) If:

  • Missing targets significantly (>50%)
  • High churn in new market
  • Sales cycle much longer than expected
  • Product doesn't fit market needs
  • Burning cash without progress

Actions: Reduce team, cut marketing, focus on core Exit Market If:

  • Fundamentally wrong market choice
  • Insurmountable competitive disadvantage
  • Regulatory or legal blockers
  • Can't achieve unit economics
  • Better opportunities elsewhere

Actions: Wind down gracefully, serve existing customers, reallocate resources

Common Expansion Mistakes

1. Expanding Too Early

Mistake: Moving to new markets before mastering current one Signs: Unstable product, high churn, no playbook Solution: Dominate core market first, build repeatability

2. Expanding Too Many Markets at Once

Mistake: Entering 5 cities simultaneously Signs: Spread too thin, can't support customers, high burn Solution: Focus on 1-2 markets at a time

3. Insufficient Localization

Mistake: Assuming product/marketing works as-is Signs: Poor conversion, confused customers Solution: Research market needs, adapt appropriately

4. Over-Localization

Mistake: Rebuilding product for each market Signs: Long delays, high costs, product sprawl Solution: Start minimal, add based on actual feedback

5. Wrong Hiring

Mistake: Hiring expensive senior people too early Signs: High burn, low activity, misaligned culture Solution: Start with hungry junior folks who hustle

6. Neglecting Core Market

Mistake: All focus on new market, existing customers suffer Signs: Rising churn in core market Solution: Balance resources, protect core business

7. Ignoring Unit Economics

Mistake: Expanding despite negative economics Signs: CAC never recovers, losing money per customer Solution: Fix economics before expanding

Case Studies

Case Study 1: Zoom - Geographic Expansion

Challenge: Growing from US to global presence Approach:

  • Started US-only (2013-2015)
  • Expanded to English-speaking markets first (UK, Australia, Canada)
  • Then to Europe and Asia (localization)
  • Built data centers regionally
  • Hired local teams in major markets

Results:

  • International revenue 33% by 2019
  • 50%+ by 2023
  • Global brand recognition

Key Lessons:

  • Start with similar markets (language, culture)
  • Invest in infrastructure for global scale
  • Localize thoughtfully, not everywhere at once
  • Build strong brand before expanding

Case Study 2: Spotify - Country-by-Country Blitz

Challenge: Music licensing requires country-by-country deals Approach:

  • Launched Sweden only (2008)
  • Added European countries one-by-one (2009-2011)
  • Finally entered US (2011)
  • Continued strategic country launches
  • Heavy localization per market

Results:

  • 180+ countries by 2023
  • Localized content for each market
  • Global music platform

Key Lessons:

  • Sometimes you must go market-by-market
  • Patience in negotiating local requirements
  • Each market requires unique approach
  • Global scale enables better content deals

Case Study 3: Toast - Vertical Focus

Challenge: Should they expand beyond restaurants? Approach:

  • Committed to restaurants only (2013-present)
  • Went deep: POS → online ordering → loyalty → payroll → lending
  • Geographic expansion while maintaining vertical focus
  • Built restaurant-specific features no generalist could match

Results:

  • #1 POS for restaurants
  • 85K+ restaurant customers
  • $3B+ revenue
  • IPO 2021

Key Lessons:

  • Vertical focus can be a competitive advantage
  • Go deep before going wide
  • Build features competitors can't justify
  • Become the industry standard

Case Study 4: Pipedrive - Regional Domination

Challenge: Competing against US giants (Salesforce, HubSpot) Approach:

  • Started in Estonia (2010)
  • Dominated Europe first (2010-2015)
  • Built strong European brand
  • Only then entered US (2016+)
  • Continued European focus while expanding globally

Results:

  • 100K+ customers globally
  • Strong European position
  • Growing US presence
  • Survived against giants

Key Lessons:

  • Don't have to start in US
  • Regional dominance provides foundation
  • Can compete globally from any base
  • Focus on underserved markets first

Recommended Reading

"Obviously Awesome" by April Dunford - Positioning for new segments 4. "Scaling Up" by Verne Harnish - Operational scaling across geographies 5. "The Culture Map" by Erin Meyer - Working across cultures 6. "Entering Foreign Markets" by Franklin Root - International expansion guide

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Geographic expansion usually comes before vertical expansion
  • ✓ Use beachhead strategy - dominate one market before expanding
  • ✓ Systematically evaluate and prioritize markets
  • ✓ Start with adjacent, similar markets (concentric circles)
  • ✓ Build a repeatable playbook before scaling expansion
  • ✓ International expansion requires careful localization and market research
  • ✓ Hire local talent who understand the market
  • ✓ Measure expansion ROI - expect 18-36 month payback
  • ✓ Avoid expanding too early or to too many markets at once
  • ✓ Balance investment in new markets with protecting core business

Next Steps

Move to Chapter 3: Product Expansion to learn how to add new products and features to drive growth within existing markets.

Remember: Expansion is about replication, not reinvention. Perfect your model in one market, then clone it to others. Resist the urge to be everywhere at once - focus wins.
