Incorporation Process: Step-by-Step Guide

Introduction

You've chosen your business structure (hopefully Delaware C-Corp if you're serious about building CoodLoom into a real company). Now it's time to actually incorporate. This chapter provides a detailed, step-by-step guide to legally forming your company. Timeline: 2-4 weeks from start to fully operational Cost: $500-3,000 depending on whether you DIY, use a service, or hire a lawyer

Before You Start: Prerequisites

1. Make These Decisions First

  • [ ] Structure decided: LLC, S-Corp, or C-Corp (see Chapter 1)
  • [ ] State chosen: Delaware, home state, or other
  • [ ] Company name finalized: "CoodLoom, Inc." or "CoodLoom, LLC"
  • [ ] Co-founder agreement discussed: Equity split, roles (see Chapter 3)
  • [ ] Initial capital: Who's investing how much?
  • [ ] Registered agent: Who will receive legal mail?

Additional Costs:

  • Bank account: $0-100 (some require minimum balance)

Total Budget: $500-3,000

Three Paths to Incorporation

Choose based on complexity, budget, and risk tolerance:

Path A: Hire a Lawyer ($1,500-3,000)

Best For:

  • C-Corps with complex structures
  • Multiple founders with IP to transfer
  • High-value businesses
  • If you value expertise and customization

Pros:

  • ✅ Everything done correctly
  • ✅ Customized for your situation
  • ✅ Legal advice included
  • ✅ Build relationship with startup lawyer

Cons:

  • ❌ Most expensive
  • ❌ Slower (lawyer's timeline)

Path B: Use Formation Service ($300-1,000)

Services:

  • Stripe Atlas: $500 (best for tech startups)
  • Clerky: $800+ (YC-recommended)
  • Gust Launch: $500+
  • LegalZoom: $300+ (basic)
  • IncFile: $0 + state fees

Best For:

  • Standard Delaware C-Corp
  • Tech startups following standard patterns
  • Balance of cost and quality

Pros:

  • ✅ Reasonable cost
  • ✅ Faster (1-2 weeks)
  • ✅ Templates designed by lawyers
  • ✅ Includes most necessary documents

Cons:

  • ❌ Template-based (less customization)
  • ❌ Limited legal advice
  • ❌ May need lawyer review anyway

Path C: DIY ($50-300)

Best For:

  • Simple LLCs
  • Solo founders or very small operations
  • Tight budgets
  • Comfortable with legal paperwork

Pros:

  • ✅ Cheapest
  • ✅ You control timeline
  • ✅ Learn the process

Cons:

  • ❌ Easy to make mistakes
  • ❌ Time-consuming
  • ❌ No legal advice
  • ❌ May cost more to fix mistakes

Recommended for CoodLoom: Path B (Stripe Atlas) or Path A (lawyer) for C-Corp

Detailed Process: Delaware C-Corporation

This is the most common path for tech startups. Adjust for your structure/state.

Phase 1: Pre-Incorporation (Week 1)

Step 1: Choose and Check Company Name

Name Requirements:

  • Must include "Corporation", "Incorporated", "Company", or abbreviation (Inc., Corp., Co., Ltd.)
  • Cannot be confusingly similar to existing company
  • Cannot include restricted words without approval (Bank, Insurance, etc.)

Check Name Availability: 1. Delaware: Search at corp.delaware.gov/directweb.shtml 2. Other states: Search Secretary of State website 3. Federal trademark: Search at uspto.gov 4. Domain availability: Check if coodloom.com is available (see Chapter 7) Pro Tip: Reserve name for 120 days ($75 in Delaware) while preparing CoodLoom Options:

  • CoodLoom, Inc. ✓ (recommended)
  • CoodLoom Corporation
  • CoodLoom Technologies, Inc.
  • CoodLoom Software, Inc.

Step 2: Choose Registered Agent

What is it?: Entity that receives legal documents on behalf of your company. Requirements:

  • Must have physical address in state of incorporation
  • Must be available during business hours
  • Can be yourself, co-founder, or service

Options: A. Yourself/Co-founder ($0):

  • Pros: Free
  • Cons: Public address, must be available, get legal mail at home

B. Registered Agent Service ($50-300/year):

  • Services: Harvard Business Services ($50/year), Northwest ($125/year), CT Corporation ($300/year)
  • Pros: Privacy, professional, mail scanning
  • Cons: Annual cost

Recommendation: Use service for privacy and professionalism ($50-125/year worth it)

Step 3: Prepare Certificate of Incorporation

Required Information:

Certificate of Incorporation of CoodLoom, Inc.
                        
FIRST: The name of the corporation is CoodLoom, Inc. SECOND: The registered office of the corporation in the State of Delaware is located at [Registered Agent Address], County of [County]. The registered agent at such address is [Registered Agent Name]. THIRD: The purpose of the corporation is to engage in any lawful act or activity for which corporations may be organized under the General Corporation Law of Delaware. FOURTH: The total number of shares of stock which the corporation shall have authority to issue is 10,000,000 shares of Common Stock, par value $0.00001 per share. FIFTH: The name and mailing address of the incorporator is: [Your Name] [Your Address] SIXTH: [Optional: Indemnification, Director Liability Limitation clauses]
Key Decisions: Authorized Shares: How many shares can you issue?

  • Typical for startups: 10,000,000 shares
  • Why so many: Allows for future fundraising, employee options, flexibility
  • Initially issue: 8,000,000 shares to founders (leave 2,000,000 for option pool)
  • Delaware franchise tax: Based on authorized shares, but low for startups (~$89/year)

Par Value: Minimum price per share

  • Standard: $0.00001 (one hundred-thousandth of a penny)
  • Why so low: Reduces franchise tax, gives pricing flexibility

Stock Classes:

  • Initially: Just Common Stock
  • Later: Add Preferred Stock when raising VC (requires amendment)

Step 6: Obtain EIN (Employer Identification Number)

What is it: Federal tax ID number (like SSN for your business) When: Immediately after incorporation How to Get EIN: 1. Go to IRS.gov/EIN 2. Click "Apply Online Now" 3. Select entity type: Corporation 4. Fill in:

  • Legal name: CoodLoom, Inc.
  • Trade name: CoodLoom (if different)
  • State of incorporation: Delaware
  • Date of incorporation: [Date from certificate]
  • Business address: [Your address]
  • Responsible party: You (CEO)
  • SSN: [Your SSN]
  • Reason: Started new business

5. Submit 6. Receive EIN immediately: 12-3456789 (format) Cost: Free Time: 15 minutes Save This: You'll need EIN for everything (bank account, taxes, contracts)

Phase 3: Create Corporate Documents (Week 2)

Step 7: Draft Bylaws

What are Bylaws: Internal rules governing how corporation operates. Key Provisions: Article I: Offices

  • Principal office location
  • Registered office (Delaware)

Article II: Shareholders Meetings

  • Annual meeting requirement
  • Special meetings: how called
  • Quorum: Majority of outstanding shares
  • Voting: One share = one vote
  • Notice: 10-60 days advance

Article III: Board of Directors

  • Number of directors: Initial = number of founders (2 for you)
  • Term: 1 year
  • Meetings: How often, notice requirements
  • Quorum: Majority
  • Committees: Can establish Audit, Compensation, etc.

Article IV: Officers

  • CEO: Manages day-to-day, reports to Board
  • CFO: Manages finances
  • Secretary: Keeps records, minutes
  • Same person can hold multiple offices

Article V: Stock

  • Stock certificates
  • Transfer restrictions
  • Lost certificates

Article VI: Indemnification

  • Protection for directors/officers
  • Standard in Delaware

Article VII: Amendments

  • How to amend bylaws
  • Typically: Board or shareholder vote

Getting Bylaws: Option A: Lawyer drafts ($500-1,000) Option B: Formation service includes (Stripe Atlas, Clerky) Option C: Download template and customize

  • Delaware.gov - Official forms
  • Cooley GO - Free startup templates

Length: Typically 15-30 pages Adoption: Board of Directors adopts bylaws at first meeting

What: Physical or electronic certificates representing ownership Information on Certificate:

  • Certificate number: 001, 002, etc.
  • Shareholder name
  • Number of shares
  • Date issued
  • Signatures: CEO and Secretary
  • Corporate seal (optional)
  • Legend: Transfer restrictions

Example:

CoodLoom, Inc. Incorporated under the laws of Delaware This certifies that [YOUR NAME] is the owner of FOUR MILLION (4,000,000) shares of Common Stock of CoodLoom, Inc., transferable only on the books of the Corporation by the holder hereof in person or by attorney upon surrender of this Certificate properly endorsed. Dated: [Date] [CEO Signature]        [Secretary Signature] CEO                    Secretary [Corporate Seal] LEGEND: The shares represented by this certificate are subject to restrictions on transfer as set forth in the Bylaws and any stockholders agreement of the Corporation.
Options: A. Buy Stock Certificate Kit ($50-150):

  • Includes: Blank certificates, corporate seal, stock ledger
  • Professional look
  • Available on Amazon

B. Print Your Own ($0):

  • Use template (Cooley GO, Stripe Atlas)
  • Print on quality paper
  • Looks less professional but legally valid

C. Electronic ($0):

  • Modern approach
  • Store in secure system (Carta, Pulley)
  • No physical certificates

Recommendation: Electronic certificates via Carta or similar (free for early-stage)

Phase 4: Post-Incorporation Setup (Week 3-4)

Step 12: Open Business Bank Account

Why Separate Account:

  • Maintains corporate veil (limited liability)
  • Cleaner accounting
  • Professional image
  • Legal requirement

What You'll Need:

  • Certificate of Incorporation
  • EIN letter
  • Bylaws
  • Board resolution authorizing account
  • Driver's license
  • Initial deposit ($100-1,000 depending on bank)

Bank Options: Traditional Banks:

  • Chase Business Banking
  • Bank of America
  • Wells Fargo
  • Local credit unions

Startup-Friendly Banks:

  • Silicon Valley Bank (if VC-funded)
  • Mercury (online, popular with startups)
  • Brex (includes credit card)
  • Novo (simple, free)

Features to Consider:

  • Monthly fees ($0-25)
  • Minimum balance requirements
  • Online/mobile banking
  • Integration with accounting software
  • Credit card options
  • Remote deposit
  • Wire transfer fees

Recommendation for CoodLoom: Mercury or Novo (free, designed for startups) Process: 1. Go to bank website or branch 2. Select "Business Checking" 3. Upload documents 4. Verify identity (video call or in-person) 5. Make initial deposit 6. Receive account: 1-3 days (online) or same-day (in-person)

Step 15: Set Up Payroll (if paying yourself)

When to Set Up:

  • C-Corp: Not urgent (can start without salary)
  • S-Corp: Required (must pay "reasonable salary")
  • LLC: Not applicable (use owner draws)

Payroll Service Options ($40-150/month):

  • Gusto: Most popular for startups, $40/month + $6/person
  • Rippling: HR + payroll, includes benefits, $8/person
  • ADP: Enterprise-grade, more expensive
  • Paychex: Traditional service
  • JustWorks: PEO service, includes benefits

What It Handles:

  • Calculate payroll taxes
  • Withhold and remit taxes
  • Generate W-2s
  • Handle state unemployment
  • Workers' comp (if included)

Founder Salary Considerations: C-Corp:

  • Can pay zero salary initially (live on savings)
  • Start salary when company can afford it
  • Typical: $0-80K initially, increase as fundraise

S-Corp:

  • Must pay "reasonable salary"
  • IRS guidance: Similar to what you'd earn at another company
  • Too low = IRS audit risk
  • Typical: $60-120K depending on role and location

LLC:

  • No salary, take owner distributions
  • Pay estimated taxes quarterly

Step 16: Get Business Insurance

Types of Insurance: General Liability ($400-1,000/year):

  • Covers: Property damage, bodily injury, advertising injury
  • Required: If have office, customers visit
  • Amount: $1M per occurrence, $2M aggregate

Professional Liability (E&O) ($500-2,000/year):

  • Covers: Errors, omissions, software bugs causing customer loss
  • Required: For software companies selling to businesses
  • Amount: $1M per occurrence
  • For CoodLoom: Important! POS system bugs could cost customers money

Cyber Liability ($1,000-3,000/year):

  • Covers: Data breaches, hacking, loss of customer data
  • Required: If handling payment/customer data
  • Amount: $1M coverage
  • For CoodLoom: Critical if processing payments

Workers' Compensation (varies):

  • Covers: Employee injuries
  • Required: When you have employees (not founders initially)
  • Cost: % of payroll, varies by state and industry

Directors & Officers (D&O) ($1,000-5,000/year):

  • Covers: Directors/officers from lawsuits
  • Required: When raising VC (investors require)
  • Amount: $1-2M coverage

Where to Buy:

  • Embroker: Startup-focused, online
  • Hiscox: Small business policies
  • CoverWallet: Compare quotes
  • Local insurance broker: Shop around

Recommendation for CoodLoom (Year 1):

  • General Liability: $1M ($500/year)
  • Professional Liability: $1M ($1,000/year)
  • Cyber Liability: $1M ($1,500/year)
  • Total: ~$3,000/year

When to Get: After incorporation, before signing first customer

Step 17: File Beneficial Ownership Report (New!)

What: New requirement (2024) - report who owns/controls company Who Must File: All corporations and LLCs When: Within 30 days of formation (new companies) Where: FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network) Information Required:

  • Beneficial owners (25%+ ownership or control)
  • Full legal name
  • Date of birth
  • Address
  • ID number (driver's license or passport)

How to File: 1. Go to fincen.gov/boi 2. Create account 3. File Beneficial Ownership Information Report 4. Cost: Free 5. Time: 20 minutes Penalty for Not Filing: $500/day! Update Required: If ownership changes 25%+

Phase 5: Organize Corporate Records (Week 4)

Step 18: Create Corporate Minute Book

What: Binder (physical or digital) with all corporate documents Contents:

  • Certificate of Incorporation (filed copy with seal)
  • Bylaws
  • Action by Incorporator
  • Board consents/meeting minutes
  • Stock purchase agreements
  • Stock certificates and ledger
  • IP assignment agreements
  • Bylaws amendments
  • Annual reports
  • Tax documents (EIN letter, 2553 if S-Corp)

Physical Binder ($50-150):

  • Buy corporate kit on Amazon
  • Professional look
  • Includes seal, stock certificates

Digital ($0):

  • Create folder structure
  • Use Dropbox, Google Drive
  • Backup regularly
  • Consider: Pulley, Carta (cap table software includes document storage)

Recommendation: Digital with cloud backup (cheaper, accessible, searchable)

Software Options: Free (when small):

  • Pulley: Free for <25 stakeholders
  • Carta: Free for early-stage
  • Excel/Google Sheets

Phase 6: Post-Incorporation Best Practices

Maintain Corporate Formality

Why: To preserve limited liability protection Do's: ✅ Keep business and personal finances separate ✅ Hold required meetings (board, shareholders) ✅ Document all major decisions ✅ File annual reports on time ✅ Pay required taxes and fees ✅ Sign contracts as CEO of company, not personally ✅ Use proper company name on everything ✅ Issue stock properly with certificates ✅ Maintain adequate capitalization Don'ts: ❌ Commingle personal and business funds ❌ Use business account for personal expenses ❌ Make major decisions without board approval ❌ Ignore corporate formalities ❌ Let company become insolvent ❌ Sign contracts personally instead of as officer ❌ Treat company assets as personal property If You Don't: "Piercing the corporate veil" - lose liability protection, personally liable

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Waiting too long to incorporate: Incorporate before:

  • Signing contracts with customers
  • Hiring employees
  • Receiving significant revenue
  • Raising money

❌ Not vesting founder stock: See Chapter 3 ❌ Forgetting IP assignment: Must transfer BEFORE or AT incorporation ❌ Using generic templates without review: Every situation is different ❌ Not maintaining corporate formality: Ruins limited liability ❌ Commingling funds: Death of limited liability ❌ Missing filing deadlines: Penalties and possible dissolution ❌ Not documenting decisions: No board minutes = problems later ❌ Issuing stock without 83(b): Massive tax bill when vesting ❌ Operating without insurance: One lawsuit = bankruptcy ❌ Not filing beneficial ownership report: $500/day fine!
