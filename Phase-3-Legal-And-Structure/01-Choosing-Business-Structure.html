Choosing Business Structure for CoodLoom

Introduction

One of the most critical decisions you'll make is choosing the right legal structure for CoodLoom. This decision affects your taxes, liability, fundraising ability, and future growth. Unlike software architecture, you can't easily "refactor" your legal structure later without significant cost and complexity. This chapter will help you choose between LLC, C-Corp, and S-Corp for your POS system business.

Why This Decision Matters

Your business structure determines:

  • Personal Liability Protection: Can creditors come after your personal assets?
  • Tax Treatment: How much you pay and when
  • Fundraising Ability: Can you raise venture capital?
  • Ownership Flexibility: How easily can you add co-founders or investors?
  • Administrative Burden: How much paperwork and compliance?
  • Exit Strategy: How easily can you sell the business?

Key Insight: For tech startups planning to raise institutional funding, C-Corp is almost always the answer. But let's understand all options.

Option 1: Limited Liability Company (LLC)

What Is It?

An LLC is a flexible business structure that protects your personal assets while offering simple taxation. Think of it as a hybrid between a sole proprietorship and a corporation.

How It Works

Structure:

  • Owned by "members" (not shareholders)
  • Managed by members or appointed managers
  • Operating Agreement governs the LLC
  • Very flexible rules

Taxation:

  • Pass-through taxation: Profits "pass through" to your personal tax return
  • You pay income tax on profits whether or not you distribute them
  • You also pay self-employment tax (15.3%) on net earnings
  • Can elect to be taxed as S-Corp or C-Corp if desired

Pros for CoodLoom

‚úÖ Simple Formation: Easiest and cheapest to set up ($100-500) ‚úÖ Flexible Management: No board of directors required ‚úÖ Flexible Ownership: Easy to split ownership any way you want ‚úÖ Pass-Through Taxation: Avoid double taxation ‚úÖ Less Compliance: Minimal ongoing requirements ‚úÖ Privacy: Some states don't require member names to be public ‚úÖ Flexible Profit Distribution: Don't have to distribute based on ownership %

Cons for CoodLoom

‚ùå Fundraising Nightmare: VCs won't invest in LLCs ‚ùå No Stock Options: Can't offer ISOs to employees ‚ùå Self-Employment Tax: Pay 15.3% SE tax on all profits ‚ùå Less Credible: Some view LLCs as "small business" vs "startup" ‚ùå Harder to Sell: More complex transfer of ownership ‚ùå No IPO Path: Can't go public as an LLC

Tax Example

Scenario: CoodLoom LLC makes $200,000 profit in Year 1

  • Profit: $200,000
  • Your Share (50%): $100,000
  • Income Tax (assume 24% bracket): $24,000
  • Self-Employment Tax (15.3%): $15,300
  • Total Tax: $39,300 (39.3%)

Even if you only took $50,000 as salary, you'd owe tax on $100,000.

Best For

  • Consulting or service businesses: Custom software development
  • Bootstrap startups: No plans for VC funding
  • Lifestyle businesses: Want to take profits out regularly
  • Side projects: Testing an idea part-time
  • Real estate: LLCs are standard for property holding

Red Flags for CoodLoom

üö© Planning to raise venture capital? Don't use LLC üö© Want to grant stock options to employees? Don't use LLC üö© Aiming for acquisition by tech company? Don't use LLC üö© Dream of IPO or unicorn status? Don't use LLC

Option 2: S-Corporation (S-Corp)

What Is It?

An S-Corp is actually a tax designation, not a separate business entity. You form a C-Corp (or LLC) and then elect S-Corp tax treatment with the IRS.

How It Works

Structure:

  • Must be a US corporation
  • Files Form 2553 with IRS to elect S-Corp status
  • Must meet specific requirements (see below)
  • Still issues stock and has shareholders

Taxation:

  • Pass-through taxation like LLC
  • BUT you can split income into:
    • Salary (subject to payroll tax)
    • Distributions (not subject to self-employment tax)
  • Can save on self-employment taxes

S-Corp Requirements

You can only be an S-Corp if you meet ALL of these:

  • ‚úì US corporation
  • ‚úì 100 or fewer shareholders
  • ‚úì Only one class of stock
  • ‚úì Shareholders must be US citizens or residents
  • ‚úì No corporate or partnership shareholders
  • ‚úì Not a financial institution, insurance company, or domestic international sales corporation

Pros for CoodLoom

‚úÖ Tax Savings: Reduce self-employment taxes ‚úÖ Pass-Through Taxation: Avoid double taxation ‚úÖ Credibility: More "serious" than LLC ‚úÖ Stock Structure: Can issue shares to founders ‚úÖ Salary + Distributions: Flexibility in compensation

Cons for CoodLoom

‚ùå No VC Funding: VCs can't invest (not US residents, need multiple share classes) ‚ùå Strict Requirements: Must meet eligibility criteria ‚ùå Limited Stock Options: Can't offer ISOs effectively ‚ùå 100 Shareholder Limit: Restricts growth ‚ùå US-Only Owners: Can't have foreign co-founder or investor ‚ùå One Share Class: Can't have preferred stock for investors ‚ùå Distribution Requirements: Must distribute proportionally to ownership ‚ùå More Compliance: Payroll, reasonable salary requirements

Tax Example

Scenario: CoodLoom S-Corp makes $200,000 profit in Year 1

  • Your Salary: $70,000 (must be "reasonable")
    • Income Tax (24%): $16,800
    • Payroll Tax (7.65% employee + 7.65% employer): $10,710
  • Distribution: $30,000 (your 50% of remaining $60,000 after both salaries)
    • Income Tax (24%): $7,200
    • Payroll Tax: $0 ‚úÖ
  • Total Tax: $34,710 vs $39,300 with LLC = $4,590 savings

Best For

  • Profitable small businesses: Consistent profit to distribute
  • Owner-operated companies: 1-5 owners, all US-based
  • Bootstrap with exit plan: Not VC-funded, but plan to sell to strategic buyer
  • Contractors/consultants: High-earning professionals
  • Businesses with steady revenue: Can afford to pay yourself reasonable salary

When to Convert to S-Corp

Start as LLC, then elect S-Corp status when:

  • Your profit exceeds $60,000-80,000/year
  • You have consistent revenue to pay salary
  • You want to reduce self-employment taxes

Red Flags for CoodLoom

üö© Planning to raise VC funding? Don't use S-Corp üö© Have or want foreign co-founder/investor? Don't use S-Corp üö© Need multiple share classes (common + preferred)? Don't use S-Corp üö© Plan to have 100+ employees with stock? Don't use S-Corp

Option 3: C-Corporation (C-Corp)

What Is It?

A C-Corp is the standard corporation structure. It's a separate legal entity that can raise capital, issue stock options, and go public. Every major tech company (Apple, Google, Facebook) is a C-Corp.

How It Works

Structure:

  • Separate legal entity
  • Owned by shareholders
  • Managed by Board of Directors
  • Officers run day-to-day (CEO, CFO, etc.)
  • Governed by bylaws and stock certificates

Taxation:

  • Double taxation:
    • Corp pays 21% federal tax on profits
    • Shareholders pay tax on dividends (0-20%)
  • But early startups rarely pay dividends, so this is less relevant initially

Pros for CoodLoom

‚úÖ VC-Friendly: Standard structure for institutional funding ‚úÖ Stock Options: Can offer ISOs and RSUs to employees ‚úÖ Multiple Share Classes: Common + Preferred stock for investors ‚úÖ Unlimited Growth: No limit on shareholders ‚úÖ Foreign Investors: Anyone can invest globally ‚úÖ Acquisition-Friendly: Easy for big companies to buy you ‚úÖ IPO Path: Can go public ‚úÖ Credibility: Serious startup structure ‚úÖ Separation of Ownership & Management: Clearer governance ‚úÖ Perpetual Existence: Continues even if founders leave

Cons for CoodLoom

‚ùå Double Taxation: If you pay dividends (but you won't initially) ‚ùå Complex & Expensive: $1,000-3,000 to set up, $2,000-5,000/year to maintain ‚ùå More Compliance: Board meetings, minutes, annual reports ‚ùå State Franchise Tax: Some states (like California) charge minimum tax ‚ùå Less Flexible: Formal structure required ‚ùå Public Records: More information is public

Tax Example

Scenario: CoodLoom C-Corp makes $200,000 profit in Year 1 If you pay yourself salary only:

  • Your Salary: $100,000
    • Income Tax (24%): $24,000
    • Payroll Tax (employee 7.65%): $7,650
    • Employer Payroll Tax: $7,650 (company pays)
  • Company Profit: $92,350 (after salary + employer tax)
    • Corporate Tax (21%): $19,394
    • Retained Earnings: $72,956 (stays in company)

Your Personal Tax: $31,650 Company Pays: $27,044 Total Tax: $58,694 But wait... you don't take distributions initially, so you avoid the second layer of tax until exit! If you DON'T pay yourself (living on savings):

  • Company Profit: $200,000
  • Corporate Tax (21%): $42,000
  • Retained Earnings: $158,000
  • Your personal tax: $0 (this year)

The QSBS Tax Advantage (HUGE!)

Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) - Section 1202: If you hold C-Corp stock for 5+ years:

  • Potentially exclude 100% of gains from federal taxes
  • Up to $10 million or 10x your basis (whichever is greater)
  • This is MASSIVE for founders

Example: You invest $50,000, company is acquired for $10 million after 5 years:

  • Gain: $9,950,000
  • Capital Gains Tax (20%): $0 with QSBS ‚úÖ
  • Savings: ~$2,000,000 in taxes!

Requirements:

  • C-Corp with gross assets under $50M at stock issuance
  • Active business (not passive investments)
  • Hold stock for 5+ years
  • Meet other technical requirements

This alone makes C-Corp attractive for founders planning to build and exit.

Best For

  • VC-funded startups: Raising institutional capital
  • High-growth tech companies: Planning to scale rapidly
  • Employee stock options: Need to recruit with equity
  • Acquisition targets: Plan to be acquired
  • IPO candidates: Dream of going public
  • Global companies: Foreign co-founders or investors

Delaware C-Corp: The Gold Standard

Most tech startups incorporate in Delaware, even if not located there: Why Delaware?: ‚úÖ VC Standard: Investors expect it ‚úÖ Well-Established Law: 200+ years of corporate case law ‚úÖ Court of Chancery: Specialized business court, no juries ‚úÖ Flexible: Easy to amend bylaws, create share classes ‚úÖ Privacy: Directors/officers names not public ‚úÖ Franchise Tax: Reasonable for early startups Cost: ~$89/year franchise tax for early-stage startup Alternative: Your home state (if not raising VC)

Ask Yourself These Questions

1. Fundraising Plans?

"Will you raise venture capital?"

  • Yes or Maybe ‚Üí C-Corp (Delaware)
  • No, bootstrap forever ‚Üí LLC or S-Corp

2. Exit Strategy?

"How do you plan to exit?"

  • Sell to tech company (acquisition) ‚Üí C-Corp

  • Sell to individual/small company ‚Üí Any structure
  • Run forever, take profits ‚Üí LLC or S-Corp

3. Employee Equity?

"Will you hire employees and give them stock options?"

  • Yes, plan to hire 10+ people ‚Üí C-Corp
  • Maybe a few people ‚Üí C-Corp or S-Corp
  • Just founders ‚Üí Any structure

Why: Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) work best in C-Corps.

4. Co-Founder Location?

"Is your co-founder a US citizen/resident?"

  • Yes, both of us are ‚Üí Any structure
  • No, or might add foreign founder ‚Üí C-Corp (S-Corp won't work)

5. International Plans?

"Will you have international customers or investors?"

  • Yes, global business ‚Üí C-Corp
  • US-only ‚Üí Any structure

6. Growth Ambitions?

"How big do you want CoodLoom to be?"

  • $1M-5M revenue, lifestyle business ‚Üí LLC or S-Corp
  • $10M+ revenue, scale nationally ‚Üí C-Corp or S-Corp initially
  • $100M+ revenue, IPO potential ‚Üí C-Corp
  • Empire builder mentality ‚Üí C-Corp

State-Specific Considerations

Where to Incorporate?

Delaware:

  • Best for: VC-funded startups
  • Cost: $89/year + $300 registered agent
  • Pros: Standard, flexible, good law
  • Cons: More expensive, foreign qualification in your state

Your Home State (e.g., California, Texas, New York):

  • Best for: Local small business, LLC/S-Corp
  • Cost: Varies ($50-$800/year)
  • Pros: Simpler, cheaper
  • Cons: Less flexible corporate law

Avoid: Nevada, Wyoming (marketed as "tax havens" but not useful for most startups)

California-Specific Warning

If you live in California:

  • Franchise Tax: $800/year minimum (even with $0 revenue)
  • Applies to: C-Corps, S-Corps, LLCs (except single-member LLCs year 1)
  • Waived: First year for LLCs

LLC in California:

  • $800/year minimum tax
  • Additional tax if revenue > $250K

C-Corp in California:

  • $800/year minimum tax
  • 8.84% corporate tax rate
  • If Delaware corp doing business in CA, must register in CA too

So California founders pay: Delaware fees + California fees

Tax Implications Summary

Effective Tax Rate Comparison (Making $200K profit)

LLC:

  • Federal income tax: 24% = $48,000
  • Self-employment tax: 15.3% = $30,600
  • Total: ~39.3% = $78,600

S-Corp (with $70K salary):

  • Salary taxes: $27,510
  • Distribution taxes: $7,200
  • Total: ~35% = $34,710
  • Savings vs LLC: $4,590/year

C-Corp (no distributions):

  • Corporate tax: 21% = $42,000
  • Personal: $0 (until you take distributions)
  • Total: 21% = $42,000
  • But allows QSBS benefits at exit

Key Insight: S-Corp saves money short-term, C-Corp saves MASSIVE money at exit (via QSBS).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå Picking LLC because it's cheapest: Penny-wise, pound-foolish if you need VC later ‚ùå Delaying incorporation: Not having liability protection is risky ‚ùå Choosing S-Corp with foreign co-founder: Ineligible, will be rejected by IRS ‚ùå Not considering QSBS: Missing out on huge tax savings at exit ‚ùå Incorporating in Nevada/Wyoming for "tax benefits": Doesn't work as advertised ‚ùå Not getting legal advice: $500 lawyer consult saves $50,000 in mistakes ‚ùå Mixing business and personal: Even with LLC/Corp, keep finances separate ‚ùå Forgetting annual compliance: Can lose liability protection

The Conversion Path

You CAN change structures, but it costs money and time:

LLC ‚Üí S-Corp

  • Easy: Just file Form 2553 with IRS
  • Cost: $0 (DIY) or $500 (lawyer)
  • Timeline: 2-15 days

LLC ‚Üí C-Corp

  • Moderate: Form new C-Corp, transfer assets
  • Cost: $1,000-3,000 (legal + filing)
  • Timeline: 2-4 weeks
  • Watch out: Tax implications on asset transfer

S-Corp ‚Üí C-Corp

  • Moderate: Revoke S-Corp election
  • Cost: $500-1,500 (legal)
  • Timeline: 1-2 weeks
  • Timing: Must plan carefully for tax year

C-Corp ‚Üí LLC or S-Corp

  • Difficult: Not recommended, complex tax issues
  • Cost: $5,000-15,000 (legal + accounting)
  • Why avoid: Usually not worth it

Lesson: Easier to start simple and upgrade than to downgrade.
