Pricing Strategy for CoodLoom

Introduction

Pricing is one of the most critical decisions you'll make. Get it right, and CoodLoom becomes a profitable, scalable business. Get it wrong, and you'll struggle to survive. This chapter teaches you how to price your POS system strategically.

Why Pricing Matters

The Impact of Pricing

A 1% improvement in pricing can lead to an 11% increase in profitability (more than any other business lever). Example:

Current: $200/month subscription × 100 customers = $20,000 MRR
                        
Increase to $210/month (+5%) = $21,000 MRR Extra $1,000/month = $12,000/year with same customer count!

Pricing Affects Everything

  • Positioning: Are you premium or budget-friendly?
  • Target Market: Price determines who can afford you
  • Sales Cycle: Higher prices may mean longer sales cycles
  • Customer Quality: Paying customers are more committed
  • Company Valuation: Higher prices = higher revenues = higher valuation

Common Pricing Mistakes

❌ Pricing too low (leaving money on the table) ❌ Copying competitors without understanding your value ❌ Undervaluing your product ❌ Complex pricing that confuses customers ❌ Not testing different price points ❌ Discounting too quickly

Pricing Psychology

1. Perceived Value vs. Cost

Customers don't care what it costs you to build. They care about the value they receive. Cost to Build CoodLoom: $50K development time Value to Customer: Saves $2,000/month in manual processes Your Price: $200/month (10% of value, 100% ROI in 5 months)

2. Price Anchoring

The first price a customer sees sets their expectations. Strategy:

❌ Bad: Show $99/month first
                        
✅ Good: Show $499/month Enterprise plan first Then $299 Pro Then $149 Basic Result: $149 seems like a great deal!

3. The Rule of 100

  • Below $100: Show percentage discount ("Save 25%")
  • Above $100: Show dollar discount ("Save $50")

Example:

  • $80 product: "Save 20%" (sounds bigger than "Save $16")
  • $500 product: "Save $100" (sounds bigger than "Save 20%")

4. Charm Pricing ($99 vs $100)

Does $99 really work better than $100? Research shows:

  • Works for consumer products (B2C)
  • Less effective for B2B/enterprise
  • Use $99 for small businesses
  • Use $100 for professional positioning

CoodLoom Recommendation:

  • Basic plan: $149 (professional, not cheap)
  • Pro plan: $299 (round number shows confidence)
  • Enterprise: Custom (value-based)

5. Decoy Pricing

Add a middle option that makes your preferred option look better. Example:

Basic:      $99/month  (50 transactions/day)
                        
Pro:        $199/month (500 transactions/day) ← Target Premium:    $349/month (unlimited transactions) Most customers pick Pro because:

                        
    
                        
  • Basic feels limiting
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Premium feels like overkill
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Pro is "just right" (Goldilocks effect)

Pricing Models for SaaS/POS Systems

1. Flat-Rate Pricing

Structure: One price for everyone, all features included Pros: ✅ Simple to understand ✅ Easy to sell ✅ Predictable revenue Cons: ❌ Leaves money on the table (power users underpay) ❌ Can't capture different market segments Example: "$299/month for CoodLoom, unlimited everything" Best For: Very early stage, testing product-market fit

2. Tiered Pricing

3. Usage-Based Pricing

Structure: Pay for what you use (per transaction, per user, etc.) Pros: ✅ Aligns cost with value received ✅ Low barrier to entry ✅ Grows revenue as customer grows Cons: ❌ Unpredictable revenue ❌ Can be expensive for heavy users (churn risk) ❌ Complex to track and bill Example: "$99 base + $0.10 per transaction" Best For: When usage varies wildly between customers

4. Freemium

Structure: Free basic version, paid premium features Pros: ✅ Low friction to try ✅ Large user base (marketing advantage) ✅ Viral growth potential Cons: ❌ Most users never convert (<5% typically) ❌ Support costs for free users ❌ Can cannibalize paid plans Example: "Free for up to 10 transactions/day, $149 for unlimited" Best For: Consumer apps, marketplace businesses (NOT recommended for CoodLoom - B2B needs hand-holding)

5. Per-Seat Pricing

Structure: Price per user/location/terminal Pros: ✅ Easy to understand ✅ Scales with customer size ✅ Predictable growth Cons: ❌ Encourages seat sharing (1 login for multiple users) ❌ May limit adoption Example: "$99/month per location" or "$49/month per terminal" CoodLoom Consideration: Could work as an add-on for multi-location businesses

Pricing Tactics and Strategies

1. Anchoring with Annual Plans

Strategy: Offer annual plans at a discount to improve cash flow

STARTER
                        
□ Monthly:   $149/month ⦿ Annual:    $127/month (save $22/month = $264/year = 15% off) Pay $1,521 upfront Benefits: ✅ Customer: Saves money ✅ CoodLoom: 12 months cash upfront (improves cash flow!) ✅ CoodLoom: Lower churn (annual commitment) ✅ CoodLoom: Better CAC payback (get money faster)
Recommendation: Prominently display annual pricing, show monthly equivalent

2. Free Trial vs Free Demo

Free Trial (Freemium-lite):

  • 14-day free trial, no credit card
  • Full access to Starter features
  • Automated emails during trial
  • Requires credit card to continue after trial

Pros: Low friction, high signups Cons: Lots of tire-kickers, low conversion (<10%) Free Demo (Recommended):

  • Book a 30-minute demo call
  • Sales rep shows the product
  • Custom trial based on needs
  • Qualified leads only

Pros: Higher conversion (30-50%), relationship building Cons: Requires sales team, slower scale CoodLoom Strategy:

  • Starter plan: 14-day free trial (self-service)
  • Pro/Enterprise: Book demo (sales-led)

3. Setup Fees

Strategy: Charge for onboarding to cover costs and filter serious customers

Options:
                        
A. No setup fee (easier conversion, but costs you time/money) B. $499 setup fee (filters serious customers, covers onboarding costs) C. $499 setup fee, waived for annual plans (incentivizes commitment)
CoodLoom Recommendation:

  • Option C: $499 setup, waived for annual
  • Includes: Account setup, data migration, 2 hours training
  • Results: Better customers, better cash flow, offsets acquisition costs

4. Discounting Strategy

When to Discount: ✅ Annual plans (15% off is standard) ✅ Early adopters (beta customers - 30% lifetime discount) ✅ Non-profits / education (20-30% off) ✅ Closing enterprise deals (10-20% if needed) When NOT to Discount: ❌ First objection in sales call ❌ Regularly (trains customers to wait for sales) ❌ More than 30% (devalues your product) ❌ Without getting something (annual commit, case study, referrals) Discount Framework:

Customer: "Can you do better on price?"
                        
You: "I can offer 10% off if you commit to an annual plan and provide a testimonial after 60 days. Would that work?"

Handling Pricing Objections

Objection 1: "You're too expensive"

Response Framework:

1. Acknowledge: "I understand price is important."
                        
2. Quantify value: "Let's break down the ROI. You mentioned you're doing $40K/month in revenue. CoodLoom typically helps businesses increase revenue by 5-10% through better upselling and inventory management. That's $2,000-4,000/month in extra revenue. Our $299/month pays for itself in the first week." 3. Reframe: "Is it that $299 is outside your budget, or are you not seeing the value yet?" 4. Offer alternatives: "We also have our Starter plan at $149, or we can structure an annual plan that brings the monthly cost down to $127."

Objection 2: "I need to think about it"

Response:

"Totally understand! Let me make this easier. What specifically are you 
                        
thinking about? Is it: a) Budget / getting approval? b) Comparing with other options? c) Technical concerns? d) Something else? If it's (b), I can show you a comparison chart. If it's (a), I can connect you with a customer in a similar situation who can share their ROI story."

Objection 3: "I'm comparing you with [Competitor]"

Response:

"Great! I'm glad you're doing your research. [Competitor] is a solid choice. 
                        
We hear from customers who switched from them that the main differences are: 1. We offer [unique feature] which saves 5 hours/week 2. Our support response time averages 2 hours vs their 24 hours 3. We don't charge transaction fees if you use your own processor Here's a comparison sheet I can send you. How about we reconnect on Friday after you've had time to compare? I can answer any questions that come up."

Objection 4: "Can you give me a discount?"

Response:

"I appreciate you asking! Our pricing is pretty tight since we focus on 
                        
delivering value rather than negotiating. That said, I can offer: • 15% off if you commit to an annual plan (saves you $537/year on Pro) • Waive the $499 setup fee for annual plans • Throw in a free hardware bundle ($799 value) if you sign by end of week Would any of those work for you?"
