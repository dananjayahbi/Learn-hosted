Domain & Trademark: Securing Your Brand

Introduction

Your brand is one of your most valuable assets. CoodLoom is more than a name - it's your reputation, your promise to customers, and your competitive advantage. This chapter covers how to secure and protect your brand through domain registration, trademark protection, and brand monitoring.

Why Brand Protection Matters

Real Example: Instagram was originally "Burbn". They pivoted and secured instagram.com before launch. What Can Go Wrong:

  • Competitor registers similar domain ‚Üí customer confusion
  • Someone trademarks your name ‚Üí you must rebrand (expensive!)
  • Cybersquatter registers your domain ‚Üí must pay $10K-100K to get it
  • Someone uses your name on social media ‚Üí brand dilution

Prevention is Cheap, Fixing is Expensive:

  • Register domain: $10-15/year
  • File trademark: $250-400 (DIY) or $1K-2K (lawyer)
  • Buy domain from cybersquatter: $10K-100K+
  • Rebrand after trademark conflict: $50K-500K+

Do it early, do it right.

Step 2: Register Your Domain

Domain Registration Basics

What is a Domain?:

  • Your website address (coodloom.com)
  • You don't "own" it, you lease it (annual renewal)
  • Registrar: Company you register through (GoDaddy, Namecheap, Google Domains, etc.)
  • Registrant: You (the person/company leasing the domain)

Cost: $10-15/year for .com

Choosing a Registrar

Reputable Registrars:

  • Namecheap (recommended): Good pricing, free WHOIS privacy, easy management
  • Google Domains: Clean interface, integrated with Google services (now Squarespace)
  • Hover: No upsells, simple, clean
  • Cloudflare: Cheapest (at-cost pricing), requires Cloudflare DNS

Avoid:

  • GoDaddy: Aggressive upsells, expensive renewals (but biggest, so acceptable)
  • Network Solutions: Expensive
  • Any registrar with bad reviews

What to Look For: ‚úì Free WHOIS privacy (hide your personal info) ‚úì Easy domain management ‚úì Reasonable renewal prices (check!) ‚úì Good customer service ‚úì Two-factor authentication (security)

Domain Registration Steps

For CoodLoom: 1. Go to Namecheap.com (or your choice) 2. Search for domain: "coodloom.com" 3. Add to cart: If available 4. Add WHOIS privacy: Free (prevents spam, hides your address) 5. Checkout:

  • Create account
  • Enter payment info
  • Register for 1-10 years (longer = lock in price)

6. Set up auto-renewal:

  • Don't lose your domain!
  • Set reminders (domains expire!)
  • Credit card on file

7. Verify email: Registrar will send verification email (required by ICANN) Total Cost: ~$10-15/year

Which TLD? (.com, .io, .ai, etc.)

TLD = Top-Level Domain (the ".com" part) Best Choice: .com

  • Most recognized and trusted
  • Easiest for customers to remember
  • Best for SEO
  • Worth paying premium for

Acceptable Alternatives:

  • .io: Popular with tech startups (but expensive, $30-60/year)
  • .ai: Trending for AI companies ($70-100/year)
  • .app: For apps ($15-20/year)
  • .co: Short for company ($30/year)

Generally Avoid:

  • .net, .org (less professional for business)
  • .biz, .info (spammy reputation)
  • Obscure TLDs (.xyz, .top, etc.) - not trusted

Country TLDs:

  • .us, .uk, .ca, etc.: Fine if targeting that country
  • May reduce international credibility

Strategy: 1. Get .com if possible (even if you also use .io) 2. Register other TLDs to prevent competitors/squatters 3. Redirect alternatives to main domain

  • CNAME: Aliases (www.coodloom.com ‚Üí coodloom.com)

  • TXT Records: Verification, SPF, DKIM for email

Where to Host DNS:

  • Registrar: Easy, where you registered (Namecheap DNS, etc.)
  • Cloudflare: Free, faster, more features (recommended)
  • Your hosting provider: Convenient if hosting website there

Email Setup:

  • Microsoft 365: $6/user/month, good if using Office
  • Zoho Mail: $1/user/month, budget option
  • Custom email: you@coodloom.com (not you@gmail.com)

SSL Certificate:

  • Let's Encrypt: Free SSL (auto-renews)
  • Most hosting includes SSL free now
  • Essential for https:// (required for credibility and SEO)

Step 3: Trademark Your Brand

What is a Trademark?

Trademark: Legal protection for your brand name, logo, slogan Protects:

  • Brand name (CoodLoom)
  • Logo (your visual mark)
  • Slogan ("Simplify Your Sales" or whatever)
  • Trade dress (distinctive visual appearance)

Benefits:

  • Exclusive right to use name in your industry
  • Prevent others from using confusingly similar names
  • Builds brand value
  • Required for some marketplaces (Amazon Brand Registry)
  • Can sue infringers
  • Adds credibility

What it Doesn't Protect:

  • Generic or descriptive terms ("POS System" - too generic)
  • Domain names (separate, but related)
  • Your actual product/code (that's copyright/patent)

Do You Need a Trademark?

Yes, If: ‚úì Planning to build a brand ‚úì Want to prevent competitors from using your name ‚úì Seeking venture capital (adds legitimacy) ‚úì Expanding nationally/internationally ‚úì Name is distinctive and protectable Maybe Not, If:

  • Name is generic ("Best POS System")
  • Bootstrap with $0 budget (can file later)
  • Not planning to scale
  • Local business only

For CoodLoom: Yes, absolutely

Types of Trademarks

By Strength (stronger = easier to protect): 1. Fanciful (Strongest):

  • Made-up words: Kodak, Xerox, Google
  • CoodLoom = Fanciful (made up)
  • Easiest to trademark

2. Arbitrary:

  • Real words used unrelated to product: Apple (computers), Amazon (e-commerce)
  • Strong protection

3. Suggestive:

  • Suggests benefit: Netflix (net + flicks), Microsoft (micro + software)
  • Moderate protection
  • Requires imagination to link to product

4. Descriptive (Weakest):

  • Describes product: "Fast POS", "Cloud Software"
  • Hard/impossible to trademark
  • Can gain protection if "secondary meaning" (Coca-Cola was descriptive)

5. Generic (Not Protectable):

  • Common name: "POS System", "Software"
  • Cannot trademark

CoodLoom: Fanciful ‚Üí Strong trademark candidate

Trademark Classes

45 Classes of goods/services (international system) Relevant for CoodLoom: Class 9: Computer software (downloadable)

  • "Downloadable computer software for point of sale transactions"
  • Cost: $250-350 per class

Class 42: Software as a service (SaaS)

  • "Software as a service (SaaS) featuring software for point of sale transactions"
  • Cost: $250-350 per class

Strategy:

  • File in both classes: Covers software product and SaaS
  • Total cost: $500-700 (DIY) or $2K-4K (with lawyer)

Other classes (maybe later):

  • Class 35: Business services, retail
  • Class 36: Financial services (if payment processing)

DIY vs. Lawyer

DIY (File Yourself):

  • Cost: $250-350 per class
  • Time: 5-10 hours to prepare
  • Pros: Cheaper, learn the process
  • Cons: Easy to make mistakes, may get rejected, no legal advice
  • Recommended if: Simple mark, clear classes, tight budget

Trademark Lawyer:

  • Cost: $1,000-2,000 per class (total $2K-4K for 2 classes)
  • Time: 1-2 hours of your time
  • Pros: Expert guidance, higher success rate, handles office actions
  • Cons: More expensive
  • Recommended if: Budget allows, complex mark, want peace of mind

Middle Ground:

  • DIY with LegalZoom/Trademark Engine ($100-200 + filing fee)
  • Not as good as real lawyer, but better than pure DIY

For CoodLoom: Lawyer recommended (worth the $2K investment)

How to File a Trademark (DIY)

USPTO (US Trademark Office): Step 1: Search Existing Trademarks

  • USPTO TESS
  • Search: "coodloom" and similar
  • Check: Your classes (9, 42)
  • Look for: Confusingly similar marks

Step 2: Create Account

  • USPTO.gov
  • Create account in USPTO system

Step 3: File Application

  • Use TEAS Plus ($250/class) or TEAS Standard ($350/class)
  • TEAS Plus cheaper but stricter requirements

Step 4: Complete Application Required Info:

  • Applicant: Your legal entity name (CoodLoom Inc. or your name)
  • Address: Business address
  • Mark: "CoodLoom" (or your name)
  • Type: Standard Character Mark (most common) or Design Mark (with logo)
  • Classes: 9 and 42
  • Description: "Downloadable software for point of sale transactions" (Class 9), "Software as a service (SaaS) featuring software for point of sale transactions" (Class 42)
  • Use in Commerce:
    • "Yes" if already selling
    • "Intent to Use" if not yet selling (need to prove use later)
  • Specimen: Proof of use (screenshot of website, product packaging, etc.)
  • Date of First Use: When you first sold product

Step 5: Submit & Pay

  • $250/class (TEAS Plus) or $350/class (TEAS Standard)
  • Total: $500-700 for 2 classes

Step 6: Wait

  • Initial review: 3-4 months
  • Total time: 6-12 months if no issues
  • 3-5 years if complications

After Filing

Timeline: Month 1-3: Waiting for USPTO examiner assignment Month 3-4: Examiner reviews application Possible Outcomes: 1. Approval ‚Üí Publication:

  • Published in Official Gazette (30-day opposition period)
  • Anyone can oppose if they think it infringes their mark
  • If no opposition ‚Üí Registration (2-3 months later)

2. Office Action (Common):

  • Examiner has questions or issues
  • Examples:
    • "Too similar to existing mark"
    • "Description too broad"
    • "Need better specimen"
  • You have 6 months to respond
  • May need lawyer at this point

3. Rejection:

  • Mark refused
  • Can appeal (expensive) or abandon

If Approved:

  • Receive: Trademark Registration Certificate
  • Rights: Exclusive use in your classes
  • Symbol: Can use ¬Æ symbol (before registration, use ‚Ñ¢)
  • Maintenance: File documents at year 5-6 and year 9-10

International Trademarks

USPTO Only Protects US:

  • Want protection in other countries? Must file there.

Options: 1. Madrid Protocol:

  • File one application covering 130+ countries
  • Cost: $500-1,000 + per-country fees
  • Convenient if targeting many countries

2. File Directly in Each Country:

  • Hire local attorney
  • Cost: $1K-3K per country
  • Slower but more control

Priority Countries for CoodLoom:

  • Canada: Close, similar market ($500-1K)
  • EU: Covers all EU countries with one filing (EUIPO, ‚Ç¨850)
  • UK: Post-Brexit, separate from EU (¬£200)
  • Australia: $250-500

When to File International:

  • After US trademark granted
  • When expanding internationally
  • Before someone else registers your mark there

Step 4: Protect Your Brand

Monitoring

Watch for Infringers: Tools:

  • Google Alerts: Free, set alerts for "coodloom" (email when mentioned)
  • Mention: Social media monitoring ($29+/month)
  • Trademark monitoring: USPTO alerts, watch for new filings
  • Domain monitoring: Alerts if similar domains registered

What to Watch For:

  • New trademark filings (similar to yours)
  • Domains: coodloom variants, typos
  • Social media: fake accounts using your name
  • Competitors: using your name in ads or content

Frequency: Check monthly (or use automated tools)

Enforcement

If Someone Infringes: Step 1: Assess:

  • How similar is it?
  • Same industry?
  • Causing confusion?
  • Intentional or accidental?

Step 2: Cease & Desist Letter:

  • Send polite letter asking them to stop
  • Explain your trademark rights
  • Request: Stop using name, transfer domain, etc.
  • Many people comply (didn't know)
  • Cost: $0 (DIY) or $500-1,500 (lawyer)

Step 3: Negotiate:

  • Offer to buy domain
  • License your mark (if not competitor)
  • Co-existence agreement (if different industries)

Step 4: Legal Action (Last Resort):

  • File lawsuit
  • Seek injunction (court order to stop)
  • Damages (if willful infringement)
  • Cost: $50K-500K+ (expensive!)
  • Only if: Serious threat, high value, infringer won't cooperate

UDRP (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy):

  • Faster, cheaper than lawsuit
  • For cybersquatters (bad faith domain registration)
  • File complaint with ICANN arbitration ($1,500-3,000)
  • If you win: Domain transferred to you
  • Timeline: 2-3 months

For CoodLoom:

  • Start with friendly contact
  • Escalate only if necessary
  • Small fish not worth expensive lawsuits

Avoiding Infringement

Don't Infringe Others: Before Launching:

  • [ ] Search existing trademarks
  • [ ] Search domains and social media
  • [ ] Google search
  • [ ] Check competitors

If Conflict Found:

  • Choose different name (easiest)
  • Or: Different spelling, add/remove word
  • Or: Different classes (if truly different industry)

Risk of Infringing:

  • Cease & desist letter (must rebrand)
  • Lawsuit (damages, legal fees)
  • Loss of domain
  • Wasted branding effort

Better safe than sorry.

Setup Steps

Set up MX records: Point email to Google servers 6. Configure: SPF, DKIM, DMARC (for deliverability) 7. Test: Send/receive emails DNS Records (Add at your domain registrar):

MX Records (Email routing):
                        
Priority 1: ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM Priority 5: ALT1.ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM Priority 5: ALT2.ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM Priority 10: ALT3.ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM Priority 10: ALT4.ASPMX.L.GOOGLE.COM TXT Record (SPF - prevents spoofing): v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all TXT Record (DKIM - email signing): (Generated by Google, specific to your account) TXT Record (DMARC - email authentication): v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:admin@coodloom.com
Why These Matter:

  • SPF: Prevents others from sending email "from" your domain
  • DKIM: Cryptographically signs emails (proves authenticity)
  • DMARC: Policy for handling failed authentication (reduces spam)
  • Result: Better email deliverability, less likely to land in spam

Cautionary Tales

Nissan Computer vs. Nissan Motors:

  • Uzi Nissan registered nissan.com in 1994 for his computer business
  • Nissan Motors (car company) sued in 1999
  • Legal battle lasted years, cost millions
  • Nissan Computer kept domain (had it first)
  • Lesson: Check trademarks before launching; first to register domain has rights

Meta (Facebook) vs. MetaX:

  • Facebook rebranded to "Meta"
  • MetaX (Canadian company) had trademark first
  • Facebook had to negotiate/acquire
  • Expensive mistake
  • Lesson: Don't rebrand to name someone else owns

Twitter vs. @music:

  • Twitter wanted @music handle
  • User refused to give up
  • Twitter eventually took it (policy change)
  • Controversial
  • Lesson: Platforms can change rules; secure handles early

International Considerations

Country-Specific Domains

ccTLDs (Country Code TLDs):

  • .uk (United Kingdom)
  • .ca (Canada)
  • .de (Germany)
  • .jp (Japan)
  • etc.

Strategy:

  • Register if targeting that country heavily
  • Example: coodloom.co.uk for UK market
  • Point to localized version of site

Cost: $10-50/year each

International Trademarks

Madrid System:

  • File one application
  • Covers 130+ countries
  • Managed by WIPO (World Intellectual Property Organization)
  • Based on your home country application (US trademark)

Process: 1. File US trademark first 2. After 6 months, file Madrid application 3. Select countries 4. Pay fees (base fee + per-country fees) 5. Each country reviews (may accept or reject) Timeline: 12-18 months Cost: $500-1,000 base + $100-500 per country Worth It If:

  • Expanding to multiple countries
  • Want protection before launch in those markets
  • Competitors might register in other countries
