Founders Agreement: Protecting Your Partnership

Introduction

The #1 reason startups fail isn't lack of funding or bad products—it's co-founder conflict. A Founders Agreement is the prenup of business partnerships. It documents everything you and your co-founder agree on today, so there's no confusion tomorrow. Most importantly, it protects both of you when things don't go as planned. This chapter covers the most critical legal document for CoodLoom: your Founders Agreement.

Why You Absolutely Need This

The Brutal Statistics

  • 65% of startups fail due to co-founder conflict
  • 73% of founding teams split before exit
  • Average time before co-founder split: 18 months

Real-World Horror Stories

Story 1: The 50/50 Deadlock

  • Two founders, 50/50 split, no vesting
  • Disagree on company direction after 6 months
  • Neither can make decisions
  • Company stuck, can't fundraise
  • Result: Shut down after 1 year

Story 2: The Departed Founder

  • Founder leaves after 3 months with 40% equity (no vesting)
  • Remaining founder builds company for 5 years
  • Exit for $10M
  • Departed founder gets $4M for 3 months work
  • Result: Remaining founder bitter, relationship destroyed

Story 3: The Side Project

  • No IP assignment agreement
  • Founder claims they own the code (built before incorporation)
  • VC due diligence finds unclear IP ownership
  • Result: Lost $2M funding round

What a Founders Agreement Prevents

✅ Disputes over equity split ✅ Uncertainty about roles and responsibilities ✅ Founders leaving with too much equity ✅ IP ownership questions ✅ Deadlock in decision-making ✅ Unfair outcomes when someone doesn't pull their weight ✅ Confusion about time commitment ✅ Issues with outside work or competing businesses

Core Components of a Founders Agreement

Every Founders Agreement should cover these 8 critical areas:

1. Equity Split

2. Vesting Schedule

3. Roles & Responsibilities

4. Time Commitment

5. Intellectual Property

6. Decision-Making Process

7. Founder Departure

8. Conflict Resolution

Let's dive deep into each:

1. Equity Split: Who Owns What

The Difficult Conversation

This is often the hardest discussion between co-founders. But it must happen before you incorporate.

Factors to Consider

Equal vs Unequal Split: 50/50 Split:

  • Pros:
    • Simple
    • Shows equal partnership
    • No hurt feelings initially
  • Cons:
    • Deadlock potential
    • May not reflect actual contributions
    • Doesn't account for founder who had the idea
    • VCs don't like it (want clear leader)

Unequal Split (e.g., 60/40, 55/45):

  • Pros:
    • Recognizes different contributions
    • Clear leader for tough decisions
    • More realistic
    • VCs prefer
  • Cons:
    • Can create resentment
    • Junior partner may feel undervalued
    • Hard to determine "right" split

Factors for Determining Split

Consider These: 1. Idea Origin (10-20%):

  • Who had the original idea?
  • How developed is it?
  • Does it matter? (Execution > Idea)

2. Domain Expertise (20-30%):

  • Industry knowledge
  • Technical expertise
  • Business experience

3. Initial Capital (10-30%):

  • Who invested money?
  • How much?
  • Is it a loan or equity?

4. Time Commitment (20-40%):

  • Full-time vs part-time
  • When will each go full-time?
  • Who left their job first?

5. Risk Taken (10-20%):

  • Who has more to lose?
  • Financial obligations
  • Career risk

6. Skills & Network (10-20%):

  • Complementary skills
  • Network value
  • Ability to raise funding

CoodLoom Example

Your Situation:

  • You: Technical founder, built the POS system
  • Co-founder: Business side, has capital

Possible Splits: Option A: 50/50

  • Reasoning: Equal partners, equal risk
  • Best if: Both equally committed, trust is high
  • Watch out: Deadlock risk

Option B: 60/40 (You get 60%)

  • Reasoning: You built the product, technical founder
  • Best if: You're CEO, final decision-maker
  • Watch out: Co-founder must feel valued

Option C: 55/45 (You get 55%)

  • Reasoning: Slight edge for sweat equity
  • Best if: Want near-equality with tiebreaker
  • Watch out: May not be meaningful difference

Option D: 50/50 with Lead Founder

  • Reasoning: Equal equity, but one is CEO with deciding vote
  • Best if: Want equality but need clear leader
  • Watch out: Need strong governance documents

The Dynamic Equity Model

Problem: Hard to predict future contributions Solution: Start with baseline, adjust over time based on contributions How It Works:

  • Track hours, capital, ideas, relationships
  • Quarterly or annual recalculations
  • Complex to manage
  • Software: Slicing Pie calculator

Pros:

  • Fair based on actual contributions
  • Motivates continued effort

Cons:

  • Complex
  • Can create ongoing disputes
  • VCs don't like it
  • Hard to track

Recommendation: Not recommended. Better to use vesting.

  • You built the product
  • You'll be CEO

2. Vesting Schedule: Earning Your Equity

What Is Vesting?

Vesting: Your equity is earned over time, not granted all at once. Why Critical: Protects against a founder leaving early with full equity.

Standard Vesting Terms

4-year vesting with 1-year cliff: 4 years: Total time to earn all equity 1-year cliff: Must stay 1 year to earn anything Monthly after cliff: Earn 1/48 of total equity each month

How It Works

Example: You get 4,000,000 shares (50% of 8,000,000) Timeline:

  • Month 0: 0 shares vested (you own 4M, but they're restricted)
  • Month 6: 0 shares vested (haven't hit cliff)
  • Month 12 (cliff): 1,000,000 shares vest (25%)
  • Month 13: 1,083,333 shares vested (1M + 1/48)
  • Month 24: 2,000,000 shares vested (50%)
  • Month 36: 3,000,000 shares vested (75%)
  • Month 48: 4,000,000 shares vested (100%)

If you leave:

  • Before 12 months: 0 shares (company buys back all)
  • At 18 months: Keep 1.5M shares, company buys back 2.5M
  • After 48 months: Keep all 4M shares

Why the 1-Year Cliff?

Protects against:

  • Founder realizes startup isn't for them
  • Founder underperforms
  • Founder had different expectations
  • Life circumstances change

Gives company time to:

  • Evaluate commitment
  • See if partnership works
  • Replace founder if needed

Without cliff: Founder could leave after 1 month with 1/48 of equity

Variations

Accelerate vesting in certain events: Single-Trigger Acceleration:

  • Vesting accelerates upon acquisition/sale
  • Example: 50% or 100% vests immediately when company sold
  • Pros: Protects founders in acquisition
  • Cons: Acquirer may not want it

Double-Trigger Acceleration:

  • Requires TWO events: (1) Acquisition AND (2) Termination
  • Example: If acquired and you're fired within 1 year, 50% accelerates
  • Pros: Standard for acquisitions
  • Cons: More complex

No Acceleration:

  • Vesting continues as normal
  • Pros: Simplest
  • Cons: Founder might lose unvested in acquisition

Recommendation for CoodLoom:

  • Standard 4-year/1-year cliff
  • Double-trigger acceleration (50% on acquisition + termination)
  • Review after 1 year

The 83(b) Election - CRITICAL!

What: IRS form that changes when you pay taxes on vesting stock Standard Tax Treatment (WITHOUT 83(b)):

  • You pay ordinary income tax on the value of shares as they vest
  • If stock increases in value, huge tax bill

Example WITHOUT 83(b):

  • You get 4M shares at $0.0001/share = $400 value
  • Year 1: 1M vest, stock now worth $1/share = $1M income
  • Tax bill: $370,000 (37% bracket) on paper gains
  • You don't have cash, only stock!

With 83(b) Election:

  • File within 30 days of stock grant
  • Pay tax on $400 (current value) immediately
  • Future gains taxed as capital gains, not income

Example WITH 83(b):

  • You get 4M shares at $0.0001/share = $400 value
  • Tax bill: $96 (24% bracket)
  • 4 years later: Sell for $10M
  • Tax: Capital gains on $10M (preferential rate)

Requirements:

  • File within 30 days of stock grant
  • No extensions, no exceptions
  • Miss it = disaster

How to File: 1. Complete IRS Form 83(b) 2. Mail to IRS (address depends on location) 3. Include copy with your tax return 4. Send copy to company 5. Keep proof of mailing (certified mail) Cost: $0 + postage Recommendation: ALWAYS FILE 83(b) with vesting stock

Reverse Vesting

What: You're given all shares upfront, but company can buy them back if you leave How It Works:

  • Day 1: You own 4M shares
  • If you leave before 1 year: Company buys back all 4M at cost
  • If you leave at 18 months: Company buys back 2.5M at cost

Same economic result as forward vesting, different structure Why Used:

  • Simpler for closely-held companies
  • Some states prefer it

3. Roles & Responsibilities

Why Define Roles

Prevents:

  • "That's not my job" syndrome
  • Duplicate work
  • Important tasks falling through cracks
  • Resentment over unequal workload

Enables:

  • Clear accountability
  • Efficient division of labor
  • Measuring performance
  • Easier to identify when roles need to change

Typical Co-Founder Role Splits

Tech Company Pattern: Founder 1 (Technical):

  • Title: CEO & CTO or CTO
  • Responsibilities:
    • Product development
    • Technology strategy
    • Engineering team (later)
    • Infrastructure/DevOps
    • Security

Founder 2 (Business):

  • Title: CEO & COO or COO
  • Responsibilities:
    • Sales & marketing
    • Finance & operations
    • Customer success
    • Fundraising
    • Legal/compliance

Alternative: Co-CEOs (rare, VCs don't like)

CoodLoom Role Definition Example

You (Technical Founder):

  • Title: CEO or CTO (decide!)
  • Primary: Product & Engineering
  • Responsibilities:
    • CoodLoom POS software development
    • Technical architecture decisions
    • Hiring engineers (later)
    • Product roadmap (jointly with co-founder)
    • Customer technical support (initially)
    • Infrastructure management
    • Security & compliance (technical aspects)

Co-Founder (Business Founder):

  • Title: COO or CFO
  • Primary: Business & Operations
  • Responsibilities:
    • Sales & lead generation
    • Customer relationships
    • Financial management
    • Fundraising & investor relations
    • Marketing strategy & execution
    • Operations & processes
    • Legal & compliance (business aspects)
    • Hiring business team (later)

Shared Responsibilities:

  • Strategic direction
  • Major decisions (see decision-making section)
  • Company culture
  • Board presentations
  • Investor updates
  • Annual planning

The CEO Question

Who should be CEO? Factors to Consider: 1. Who interfaces with investors? (CEO typically) 2. Who is better at big-picture strategy? 3. Who do customers expect to see? 4. Who is more comfortable with public role? 5. Who wants the pressure? For CoodLoom: Option A: You are CEO (common for technical founders)

  • If: You want to be CEO (your stated goal)
  • If: You're good at communication & strategy
  • If: Co-founder is comfortable with COO role

Option B: Co-founder is CEO

  • If: They have more business experience
  • If: They're better at fundraising/sales
  • If: You prefer focusing on product

Option C: Co-CEOs

  • Generally not recommended: VCs dislike, creates confusion
  • Only if: Truly equal partnership, exceptional communication

Recommendation: One person is CEO, the other is COO/CTO. Decide based on:

  • Who's better at external relationships (fundraising, big partnerships)
  • Who wants the title more
  • Who will investors prefer (often technical founder for tech company)

Role Evolution

Expect roles to change:

  • Initially: Everyone does everything
  • Early stage (0-10 employees): Still wearing many hats
  • Growth stage (10-50 employees): Specialization increases
  • Scale stage (50+ employees): Founders become managers

Example Evolution: Year 1:

  • You: 70% coding, 20% sales, 10% ops
  • Co-founder: 70% sales, 20% ops, 10% finance

Year 2:

  • You: 50% coding, 30% product mgmt, 20% hiring
  • Co-founder: 50% sales, 30% ops, 20% fundraising

Year 3:

  • You: 20% coding, 50% product/eng mgmt, 30% strategy
  • Co-founder: 30% sales, 40% ops, 30% finance/fundraising

Year 5:

  • You: 0% coding, 70% strategy/mgmt, 30% vision
  • Co-founder: 20% sales mgmt, 50% ops, 30% finance

Be flexible: Roles should serve the business, not egos

4. Time Commitment

Why Document This

Common Scenario:

  • Founder A: Full-time, 60+ hours/week
  • Founder B: "Full-time" but also consulting on side, 30 hours/week
  • Resentment builds
  • Company suffers

Full-Time vs Part-Time

Full-Time Definition:

  • 40+ hours per week
  • No other employment
  • Primary focus
  • Available during business hours

Part-Time Definition:

  • Specific hours per week (e.g., 20 hours)
  • May have other commitments
  • Clear schedule
  • Not sustainable long-term for equal equity

Transition Plans

Common Situation: Can't both quit jobs immediately Options: Option A: Both Quit Together

  • Pros: Equal commitment from start
  • Cons: Highest risk, need runway

Option B: Staggered Transition

  • Founder A quits now (technical/product)
  • Founder B quits in 3-6 months (when $ needed for sales)
  • Pros: Reduces risk
  • Cons: Unequal effort initially

Option C: Both Part-Time, Then Full-Time

  • Both keep jobs initially
  • Transition when revenue or funding
  • Pros: Lowest risk
  • Cons: Slow progress, maybe not serious

For CoodLoom: If you have runway (6-12 months expenses):

  • Both go full-time immediately
  • Shows commitment
  • Maximizes progress
  • Recommended if possible

If limited runway:

  • Technical founder (you) full-time
  • Business founder part-time (20+ hours) for 3-6 months
  • Transition when milestones hit:
    • $10K MRR, or
    • Closed beta users, or
    • Ready to fundraise

The Side Project Problem

Common Issue: Founder wants to keep side business/project Problems:

  • Time dilution
  • Attention dilution
  • Potential IP conflicts
  • Competitive concerns

Solutions: Option A: No Outside Work (Recommended)

  • Full focus on startup
  • Exception: Angel investing, advisory roles (limited time)

Option B: Allowed with Restrictions

  • Must be disclosed
  • Cannot compete
  • Cannot interfere with duties
  • IP from side project stays with founder

Option C: Wind Down Period

  • 30-90 days to transition out of other commitments
  • Then full focus on startup

For CoodLoom: No outside work if both full-time with equal equity

5. Intellectual Property Assignment

Why This Is Critical

Scenario Without IP Assignment:

  • You built POS software before incorporating
  • Company incorporated
  • You didn't formally assign IP to company
  • Later: Investor due diligence questions who owns software
  • You claim: "I do, I built it"
  • Result: Lost funding round, broken trust, legal battle

With IP Assignment:

  • Crystal clear: Company owns all IP
  • Due diligence: ✓ Clean
  • Funding: ✓ No issues

What IP to Assign

Everything related to the business: Code & Software:

  • All existing CoodLoom POS code
  • Prior versions and prototypes
  • Related tools and scripts
  • Documentation

Intangible Property:

  • Domain names (coodloom.com, etc.)
  • Trademarks and brand names
  • Logos and designs
  • Business methods
  • Customer lists (if any)

Content:

  • Marketing materials
  • Website content
  • Presentations
  • Training materials

Inventions:

  • Any patents or patent applications
  • Trade secrets
  • Proprietary processes

Work Product

All future work created during employment belongs to company:

  • Code written
  • Features developed
  • Designs created
  • Documentation written
  • Ideas and inventions

Standard Rule: If created using company time, resources, or related to company business → Company owns it

Prior Inventions

Problem: You might have other projects/IP you want to keep Solution: List "Prior Inventions" that are excluded Example:

6. Decision-Making Process

The Deadlock Problem

50/50 ownership + Disagreement = Deadlock Example:

  • You want to focus on enterprise customers
  • Co-founder wants to focus on SMB
  • No mechanism to break tie
  • Company can't move forward

Solutions:

A. Designate CEO with Final Say

Structure:

  • Both founders have 50% equity
  • One is CEO
  • CEO has final say on operating decisions
  • But: Major decisions still require both

Pros:

  • Clear decision-maker
  • Can't get stuck
  • CEO has accountability

Cons:

  • Other founder might feel powerless
  • Requires strong trust

Implementation:

One founder shall serve as CEO. In the event of disagreement on 
                        
operating decisions, the CEO shall have final decision-making authority. However, Major Decisions (defined below) require unanimous approval.

B. Tiered Decision Framework

Three tiers of decisions: Tier 1: Individual Authority

  • Daily operational decisions
  • Decisions within founder's domain
  • Spending up to $[5,000]
  • Who Decides: Responsible founder

Tier 2: Joint Founder Approval

  • Spending $[5,000] - $[25,000]
  • Hiring decisions
  • Product roadmap
  • Marketing campaigns
  • Partnership agreements
  • Who Decides: Both founders must agree

Tier 3: Board Approval

  • Major Decisions (see list below)
  • Who Decides: Board of Directors (includes founders + any investors)

C. Major Decisions Requiring Unanimous Approval

Always require both founders (and Board if applicable): Financial:

  • Raising capital (equity or debt)
  • Spending over $[25,000] (adjust based on company size)

  • Change in banking relationships

D. Conflict Resolution Escalation

When founders disagree: Level 1: Discussion (1 week)

  • Founders discuss privately
  • Present arguments and data
  • Try to reach consensus
  • Goal: Understand other's perspective

Level 2: Advisor Input (1 week)

  • Bring in trusted advisor or mentor
  • Present both sides
  • Get outside perspective
  • Non-binding advice

Level 3: Board Decision (1 week)

  • Present to Board of Directors
  • Board votes on resolution
  • Binding decision
  • If no board yet, move to Level 4

Level 4: Mediation (2-4 weeks)

  • Hire professional mediator
  • Structured mediation process
  • Try to reach agreement
  • Cost: $[2,000-5,000]

Level 5: Arbitration (last resort)

  • Binding arbitration
  • Arbitrator makes decision
  • Legally enforceable
  • Cost: $[10,000-50,000]

Timeline: If disagreement not resolved in 6 weeks, arbitration is automatic

E. Voting Control

If unequal equity: 60/40 Split:

  • Majority founder (60%) can make decisions
  • But: Major Decisions still require unanimous or supermajority (75%)
  • Protects minority founder from being steamrolled

Include in Agreement:

Notwithstanding equity ownership percentages, certain Major Decisions 
                        
require unanimous approval of all founders or [75%] supermajority of Board of Directors.

7. Founder Departure

The Uncomfortable Truth

Most co-founder relationships don't last to exit. You need to plan for:

  • Voluntary resignation
  • Termination for cause
  • Termination without cause
  • Death or disability
  • Mutual departure

Reasons Founders Leave

Voluntary:

  • Different vision for company
  • Burned out
  • Health or family issues
  • Better opportunity elsewhere
  • Conflict with co-founder
  • No longer believe in company

Involuntary:

  • Poor performance
  • Ethical violations
  • Breach of agreement
  • Criminal activity
  • Competing with company

Unavoidable:

  • Death
  • Permanent disability
  • Immigration issues

What Happens to Equity?

Vested Shares:

  • Keep them: Founder earned these
  • Exception: Termination for Cause (see below)

Unvested Shares:

  • Forfeited: Returned to company
  • Bought back at original cost (often $0.00001/share = pennies)

Example:

  • Founder has 4M shares, 48-month vesting
  • Leaves at month 20
  • Vested: 1.67M shares (keep)
  • Unvested: 2.33M shares (forfeited)

Termination For Cause

Definition of "Cause":

  • Gross negligence or willful misconduct
  • Breach of fiduciary duty
  • Fraud or embezzlement
  • Criminal conviction
  • Breach of agreement (IP, non-compete, etc.)
  • Competing with company
  • Failure to perform duties after written warning

Consequence:

  • Immediate termination
  • Unvested shares forfeited
  • Possibly: Company can buy back vested shares at cost
  • No severance

High Bar: "Cause" should be serious, not just disagreements

Termination Without Cause

Reasons:

  • Company changing direction
  • Founder not working out
  • Personality conflicts
  • Performance issues (but not egregious)

Consequence:

  • Unvested shares forfeited (per vesting schedule)
  • Vested shares keep
  • Possible severance (if agreed)

Good Leaver vs Bad Leaver

Used in some agreements: Good Leaver:

  • Voluntary resignation with notice
  • Termination without cause
  • Mutual agreement
  • Death or disability
  • Treatment: Keeps vested shares, normal repurchase of unvested

Bad Leaver:

  • Termination for cause
  • Resignation without notice
  • Breach of agreement
  • Competing with company
  • Treatment: May forfeit some or all shares, including vested

Bad Leaver Provisions can be harsh:

  • Company buys back ALL shares (vested + unvested) at cost
  • Means founder leaves with almost nothing

Recommendation: Use "Bad Leaver" only for egregious behavior (cause), not just poor performance

Repurchase Rights

Company Option to Repurchase: When Triggered:

  • Founder leaves (voluntarily or involuntarily)
  • Founder's unvested shares
  • Optionally: Vested shares too

Price:

  • Unvested: Original cost (often $0.00001/share)
  • Vested: Fair market value OR original cost (negotiate)

Timeline:

  • Company has [60-90 days] to exercise option
  • If not exercised, founder keeps shares

Payment:

  • Cash immediately, OR
  • Promissory note over [1-3 years]

Why Important:

  • Keeps equity pool available for new hires/founders
  • Prevents departed founder from blocking decisions

Founder-Friendly Approach:

  • Company buys back unvested at cost (standard)
  • Founder keeps vested shares
  • Optional: Right of first refusal if founder wants to sell

Company-Friendly Approach:

  • Company can buy back ALL shares at FMV
  • Founder must sell if company wants to buy
  • Prevents departed founder from benefiting from future success

Balanced Approach (Recommended):

  • Founder keeps vested shares
  • Company buys unvested at cost
  • Drag-along rights (founder must participate in sale of company)
  • Right of first refusal (company can match any outside offer)

Non-Compete & Non-Solicit

Non-Compete:

  • Founder agrees not to compete with company
  • Typically: [1-2 years] after departure
  • Geography: [Where company operates]
  • Scope: [Specific to company's business]

Example:

For [18 months] following termination, Founder shall not:
                        
    
                        
  • Start a competing POS software company
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Work for a direct competitor
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Solicit Company's customers for competing products

Enforceability: Varies by state

  • California: Non-competes largely unenforceable
  • Other states: Enforceable if reasonable (time, geography, scope)

Recommendation: Keep narrow and reasonable (12-18 months, specific to direct competition) Non-Solicit:

  • Founder agrees not to recruit company's employees or contractors
  • Typically: [1-2 years] after departure
  • More enforceable than non-compete

Example:

For [18 months] following termination, Founder shall not:
                        
    
                        
  • Solicit any Company employee to leave
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Hire any Company employee or contractor
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Encourage customers to stop using Company's services

Recommendation: Include non-solicit (reasonable, enforceable)

Death or Disability

Plan for the worst: Death:

  • Vesting stops
  • Estate keeps vested shares
  • Company has option to repurchase vested shares from estate
  • Life insurance on founders (recommended $500K-1M each)

Disability:

  • Define: "Unable to perform duties for [90 consecutive days] or [120 days in any 12-month period]"
  • Vesting stops after disability period
  • Company can terminate
  • Disability insurance (recommended)

Life Insurance:

  • Key Person Insurance: Company owns policy on founders
  • Benefit: $500K-2M (enough to hire replacement + cushion)
  • Cost: $50-200/month per founder
  • Trigger: Pay to company upon death
  • Use: Hire replacement, fund operations

Departure Process

Notice Period:

  • Departing founder must give [30-60 days] notice
  • Exception: Termination for cause (immediate)

Transition:

  • Departing founder assists with transition
  • Knowledge transfer
  • Introduction to key contacts
  • Documentation

Return of Property:

  • Laptop, phone, equipment
  • Access credentials
  • Confidential information
  • Company credit cards

Non-Disparagement:

  • Neither party will speak negatively about the other
  • Applies to founders and company
  • Protects reputation

8. Conflict Resolution

Preventing Conflicts

Best Practices: Regular Communication:

  • Weekly founder meetings
  • Honest, open dialogue
  • Address issues early
  • No surprises

Clear Expectations:

  • Documented roles
  • Defined metrics
  • Regular check-ins
  • Performance feedback

Shared Vision:

  • Aligned goals
  • Regular strategy discussions
  • Revisit "why we're doing this"

Outside Perspectives:

  • Advisors or mentors
  • Board members
  • Peer founder groups

When Conflict Arises

Step 1: Direct Conversation (Week 1)

  • One-on-one meeting
  • Each person presents perspective
  • Active listening
  • Try to understand root cause
  • Brainstorm solutions

Step 2: Structured Discussion (Week 2)

  • Set agenda
  • Each person writes down:
    • Issue as they see it
    • Proposed solutions
    • Non-negotiables
  • Exchange documents
  • Discuss with goal of resolution

Step 3: Third-Party Advisor (Week 3)

  • Bring in trusted mentor or advisor
  • Both present case
  • Advisor provides perspective
  • Non-binding advice
  • Cost: Usually free (advisor relationship)

Step 4: Professional Mediation (Week 4-5)

  • Hire professional mediator
  • Structured process
  • Goal: Win-win solution
  • Cost: $2,000-5,000
  • Success rate: ~70%

Step 5: Arbitration (Week 6+)

  • Binding arbitration
  • Present case to arbitrator
  • Arbitrator decides
  • Legally enforceable
  • Cost: $10,000-50,000
  • Last resort

Mediation vs Arbitration

Mediation:

  • Mediator helps parties reach agreement
  • Non-binding: Parties must agree to outcome
  • Collaborative: Try to find win-win
  • Cheaper: $2,000-5,000
  • Faster: 1-2 sessions
  • Try first

Arbitration:

  • Arbitrator hears both sides and decides
  • Binding: Must follow decision
  • Win-lose: Someone wins, someone loses
  • Expensive: $10,000-50,000+
  • Slower: Several months
  • Last resort

Irreconcilable Differences

If founders truly can't work together: Option A: One Founder Buys Out the Other

  • Negotiate buyout price
  • Typically based on:
    • Company valuation
    • Vested equity percentage
    • Contributed capital
  • Payment terms: Cash or promissory note
  • Departing founder signs non-compete

Example:

  • Company valued at $1M
  • Each founder has 50%, 50% vested = 25% of company
  • Buyout: $250,000
  • Payment: $50K cash + $200K note over 4 years

Option B: Sell the Company

  • Both founders agree to sell
  • Hire investment banker or broker
  • Split proceeds per equity ownership
  • Clean exit for both

Option C: Shut Down the Company

  • Mutually agree to wind down
  • Pay debts
  • Distribute remaining assets
  • Hardest emotionally but sometimes best

Option D: Russian Roulette Clause

  • One founder offers to buy other's shares at specific price
  • Other founder must either:
    • (A) Accept and sell, OR
    • (B) Buy offering founder's shares at same price
  • Forces fair pricing
  • Nuclear option

Creating Your Founders Agreement

DIY vs Lawyer

DIY (Not Recommended):

  • Cost: $0-100 (templates)
  • Risk: Missing key clauses, unenforceable terms, costly mistakes
  • When: Only if absolutely can't afford lawyer AND using proven template

Lawyer:

  • Cost: $1,500-5,000
  • Value: Customized, enforceable, prevents $100K+ in future disputes
  • When: Always, if possible

Recommendation: Spend the money on a lawyer. This document is too important to DIY.

Finding a Lawyer

Startup/Tech Lawyer:

  • Experience with founders agreements
  • Understands startup dynamics
  • Fixed fee arrangement

Where to Find:

  • Ask other founders for referrals
  • UpCounsel (freelance lawyers)
  • Local bar association
  • Cooley, Wilson Sonsini, Gunderson (top firms, expensive)

Interview Questions:

  • How many founders agreements have you drafted?
  • What are common issues you see?
  • Fixed fee or hourly?
  • Turnaround time?
  • Will you explain each clause?

Cost:

  • Simple agreement: $1,500-2,500
  • Complex agreement: $3,000-5,000
  • Hourly: $300-600/hour

Process

1. Discussion (1 week):

  • You and co-founder discuss all key terms
  • Use this chapter as guide
  • Agree on basics before involving lawyer
  • Write down agreements

2. Engage Lawyer (Week 2):

  • Both founders meet with lawyer together
  • Explain business and relationship
  • Share discussion notes
  • Lawyer drafts agreement

3. Review (Week 3):

  • Lawyer sends draft
  • Both founders review carefully
  • Ask questions about anything unclear
  • Suggest changes

4. Revision (Week 3-4):

  • Lawyer revises based on feedback
  • May take 2-3 rounds
  • Don't rush

5. Signature (Week 4):

  • Both founders sign
  • Company signs (if incorporated)
  • Everyone gets copy
  • Store securely

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ "We'll figure it out later": Founders agreements get harder after conflict starts ❌ Verbal agreements only: Not enforceable, memories differ ❌ 50/50 without tiebreaker: Recipe for deadlock ❌ No vesting: Departed founder keeps full equity ❌ Vesting but no 83(b): Massive tax bill later ❌ No IP assignment: Unclear who owns what ❌ Generic template without lawyer review: May not be enforceable ❌ Signing without understanding: Read and understand every clause ❌ Not revisiting: Should review annually and after major changes ❌ Letting emotions dictate: Make decisions rationally, not emotionally ❌ Unequal terms without reason: Creates resentment
