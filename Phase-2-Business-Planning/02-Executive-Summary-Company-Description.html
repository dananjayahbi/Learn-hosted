Chapter 2: Executive Summary & Company Description

Introduction

The Executive Summary is the most important section of your business plan. Why? Because it's often the only section that gets read. If it doesn't grab attention in 2 minutes, the rest of your plan won't be read at all. For CoodLoom, your Executive Summary needs to immediately communicate: "This is a serious, well-thought-out opportunity worth your time."

Part 1: Executive Summary

What is an Executive Summary?

An Executive Summary is a 1-2 page standalone document that:

  • Captures the essence of your entire business plan
  • Can be read in 3-5 minutes
  • Makes the reader want to learn more
  • Works independently (reader doesn't need to read anything else)

Think of it as your "business plan in miniature."

Why It's Written LAST

Critical Rule: Write your Executive Summary AFTER completing all other sections. Why?

  • You need clarity on every aspect of your business first
  • It's easier to summarize what you've already written
  • Your understanding will evolve as you write other sections
  • Financial numbers must be finalized first

Process: 1. Write Chapters 3-12 first (8-10 weeks) 2. THEN write Executive Summary (1 week) 3. Polish it until perfect

Key Components of an Executive Summary

Your Executive Summary must include these 7 elements:

1. The Hook (2-3 sentences)

Start with the problem or opportunity Bad Example: > "CoodLoom is a technology company that provides point of sale solutions to small businesses." Good Example: > "Over 2.5 million small restaurants in the U.S. lose an average of $15,000 annually due to inefficient POS systems that are slow, inflexible, and difficult to use. CoodLoom solves this with a modern, lightning-fast POS system designed specifically for quick-service restaurants and coffee shops, reducing transaction time by 40% and increasing revenue through intelligent upselling features."

2. Company Overview (2-3 sentences)

Who you are and what you do Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom is a SaaS-based point of sale platform founded in 2025 by [Your Name] and [Co-founder Name], experienced software developers who previously worked at [Company]. Based in [City], we currently serve 15 paying customers across [State/Region], processing over $50,000 in monthly transactions. Our mission is to become the go-to POS solution for modern, growth-oriented food service businesses."

3. Products/Services (3-4 sentences)

What you're selling and key differentiators Example for CoodLoom: > "Our cloud-based POS system offers: > - Lightning-fast transactions: 40% faster checkout than Square or Toast > - Intelligent inventory management: Automatic reordering when stock runs low > - Built-in customer loyalty: Increase repeat visits by 25% > - Real-time analytics: Know your best sellers and profit margins instantly > - Mobile-first design: Works seamlessly on tablets and phones > > Unlike legacy systems, CoodLoom is intuitive enough that new staff can be trained in under 15 minutes, reducing onboarding costs and eliminating errors."

4. Market Opportunity (3-4 sentences)

Market size and why now Example for CoodLoom: > "The U.S. POS software market is projected to reach $42 billion by 2028, growing at 8.3% annually. The restaurant and food service segment represents $12 billion of this market, with 2.5 million potential customers. Post-pandemic, businesses are rapidly digitizing, creating an unprecedented opportunity to capture market share from outdated systems. Our initial focus is coffee shops and quick-service restaurants with 1-3 locations ($500K-$2M annual revenue), representing a $2.8 billion addressable market."

5. Competitive Advantage (2-3 sentences)

Why you'll win Example for CoodLoom: > "While Square and Toast dominate the POS market, they suffer from feature bloat, slow support, and one-size-fits-all design. CoodLoom focuses exclusively on quick-service restaurants and coffee shops, offering specialized features these businesses need (like rapid order customization and mobile ordering integration) without the complexity of features they don't. Our white-glove onboarding and same-day support response time create customer loyalty that larger competitors can't match."

6. Business Model & Financials (4-5 sentences)

How you make money and key financial highlights Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom operates on a SaaS subscription model: > - Software: $199/month per location > - Hardware: $1,200 one-time (optional) > - Transaction fees: 1.5% + $0.10 per transaction > > With our current 15 customers, we're generating $3,500 in monthly recurring revenue (MRR). Our 5-year projections show: > - Year 1: 50 customers, $120K revenue > - Year 3: 500 customers, $1.2M revenue > - Year 5: 2,000 customers, $4.8M revenue > > We project profitability by Month 18 with 75 customers. Customer acquisition cost is $800, with lifetime value of $7,200 (3-year retention), yielding a 9:1 LTV:CAC ratio."

7. Funding Request (2-3 sentences) [Optional if not fundraising]

How much you need and what you'll do with it Example if fundraising: > "We are seeking $500K in seed funding to accelerate growth over the next 18 months. Funds will be allocated to: product development (30%), sales and marketing (40%), customer success team (20%), and working capital (10%). This capital will enable us to grow from 15 to 200 customers, achieving $50K MRR and positioning us for a Series A round in Q4 2027." Example if bootstrapping: > "CoodLoom is currently bootstrapped with $150K in founder capital. We are not actively seeking outside investment at this time, choosing instead to grow organically through revenue. This approach allows us to maintain full control and optimize product-market fit before considering external funding."

8. The Ask/Next Steps (1-2 sentences)

What you want the reader to do Examples:

  • "We invite you to review the full business plan and schedule a meeting to discuss investment terms."
  • "We welcome the opportunity to demonstrate CoodLoom and discuss a strategic partnership."
  • "This plan will guide our next 18 months of execution toward profitability and sustainable growth."

Executive Summary Writing Tips

✅ DO:

  • Write in present tense: "We serve customers" not "We will serve"
  • Use concrete numbers: "40% faster" not "much faster"
  • Be confident: "We will capture" not "We hope to"
  • Make it scannable: Use bullets and short paragraphs
  • Tell a story: Problem → Solution → Success
  • Be specific: Names, dates, numbers
  • Make it standalone: Don't reference other sections

❌ DON'T:

  • Use jargon or buzzwords ("synergistic ecosystem")
  • Include too much technical detail
  • Make unsubstantiated claims ("We'll dominate the market")
  • Be vague ("substantial revenue growth")
  • Write in passive voice
  • Exceed 2 pages
  • Include footnotes or appendices

Executive Summary Quality Checklist

Review your Executive Summary against these criteria: ✓ Can be understood in 3 minutes by someone unfamiliar with your business ✓ Makes the reader want to learn more ✓ Includes specific numbers and data ✓ Clearly states what problem you solve ✓ Demonstrates traction (customers, revenue) ✓ Shows realistic financial projections ✓ Explains competitive advantage clearly ✓ Has zero typos or grammatical errors ✓ Fits on 1-2 pages maximum ✓ Works as a standalone document

Common Executive Summary Mistakes

❌ Mistake #1: Too Long

Problem: 3-4 pages of dense text Fix: Ruthlessly cut to 1-2 pages. Every word must earn its place.

❌ Mistake #2: Too Technical

Problem: "Our proprietary REST API enables seamless integration..." Fix: "Our system connects with any existing software in minutes."

❌ Mistake #3: No Hook

Problem: Starting with "XYZ Corp was founded in..." Fix: Start with the problem: "Restaurants lose $15,000 annually due to..."

❌ Mistake #4: Vague Claims

Problem: "Significant market opportunity" Fix: "$2.8 billion addressable market with 250,000 potential customers"

❌ Mistake #5: No Traction

Problem: All future-focused, no present proof Fix: "Currently serving 15 customers processing $50K monthly"

Part 2: Company Description

What is a Company Description?

The Company Description section provides detailed information about your business that doesn't fit in the Executive Summary. It's typically 2-4 pages and covers:

  • Company history and formation
  • Legal structure and ownership
  • Location and facilities
  • Mission, vision, and values
  • Core competencies
  • Key success factors
  • Current stage and milestones

Why This Section Matters

The Company Description:

  • Establishes credibility and legitimacy
  • Shows your business is real and operating
  • Communicates your values and culture
  • Demonstrates thoughtful planning
  • Provides context for the rest of the plan

Key Components of Company Description

1. Company Overview & History

Tell your origin story. How did CoodLoom come to be? Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom was founded in January 2025 by [Your Name] and [Co-founder Name], software developers who spent a combined 12 years building enterprise applications. The idea emerged from a frustrating experience at a local coffee shop where the outdated POS system caused a 10-minute wait in line. We realized that while consumer technology has advanced dramatically, small business POS systems have stagnated. > > After interviewing 50 restaurant and coffee shop owners, we discovered this wasn't an isolated issue—business owners universally complained about slow, inflexible, and expensive POS systems. In March 2025, we began development of CoodLoom, building a modern, cloud-based POS system designed specifically for quick-service food businesses. > > Our MVP launched in July 2025 with 3 pilot customers. By October 2025, we had grown to 15 paying customers processing over $50,000 in monthly transactions. We're now positioned to scale operations and expand our market presence."

2. Legal Structure & Ownership

Be transparent about your business structure. Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom Inc. is a Delaware C-Corporation registered in [State] in February 2025. The company is owned by: > - [Your Name]: 50% equity, CEO and Co-Founder > - [Co-founder Name]: 50% equity, CTO and Co-Founder > > Both founders have 4-year vesting schedules with 1-year cliffs to ensure long-term commitment. The company has issued 10,000,000 authorized shares, with 7,000,000 shares issued to founders and 3,000,000 shares reserved for future employee stock options and investor equity. > > We chose the C-Corp structure to enable future equity fundraising and to attract top talent through stock options." Note: Include cap table in appendix if there are multiple shareholders.

3. Location & Facilities

Where you operate from matters. Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom's headquarters is located in [City, State], chosen for its thriving tech ecosystem, access to talent, and proximity to our target customer base. We currently operate from a shared coworking space at [Address], which provides: > - Dedicated office space for 2-4 people > - High-speed internet and infrastructure > - Meeting rooms for customer demos > - Collaborative environment with other startups > - Professional mailing address > > As we grow to 5+ team members, we plan to secure our own office space (budgeted for Month 12). Our cloud-based infrastructure means our team can work remotely when needed, though we value in-person collaboration for core working hours."

4. Mission, Vision, and Values

This is your "why" and your guiding principles. Mission Statement (What you do today): > "To empower small food service businesses with powerful, intuitive point-of-sale technology that increases efficiency, grows revenue, and delights customers." Vision Statement (Where you're going): > "To become the leading POS and business management platform for modern, growth-oriented food service businesses, evolving from point of sale to a comprehensive business operating system." Core Values: > 1. Customer Obsession: We succeed when our customers succeed. Every decision prioritizes customer value. > > 2. Move Fast, Build Quality: We ship quickly but never compromise on reliability. Our customers' businesses depend on us. > > 3. Transparency: We communicate honestly with customers, partners, and each other. No hidden fees, no surprises. > > 4. Continuous Learning: We're first-time founders building an empire. We embrace learning, admit mistakes, and iterate rapidly. > > 5. Sustainable Growth: We build for the long term, not quick wins. Profitability and customer retention matter more than vanity metrics.

5. Core Competencies

What you're exceptionally good at. Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom's competitive advantage stems from three core competencies: > > 1. Technical Excellence > Both founders are experienced software engineers with deep expertise in cloud architecture, real-time systems, and mobile development. We build reliable, fast, scalable software that 'just works.' > > 2. Customer Empathy > We've spent 100+ hours in restaurants and coffee shops observing workflows, interviewing staff, and understanding pain points. We design for real users, not what we think they need. > > 3. Rapid Iteration > As a small, focused team, we can ship features in weeks that take competitors months. When a customer requests a feature on Monday, we can deliver it by Friday. This agility is our superpower against larger, slower competitors."

6. Key Success Factors

What needs to be true for CoodLoom to succeed? Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom's success depends on five critical factors: > > 1. Product Reliability (Non-Negotiable) > Our POS system must work 99.9% of the time. A single hour of downtime could cost a restaurant thousands of dollars. We invest heavily in redundancy, monitoring, and rapid incident response. > > 2. Customer Acquisition Efficiency > We must maintain CAC under $1,000 and achieve at least a 6:1 LTV:CAC ratio to be capital efficient. This requires effective marketing channels and high conversion rates. > > 3. Customer Retention > Monthly churn must stay below 3%. Since switching POS systems is painful, we focus on onboarding excellence and proactive support to lock in long-term customers. > > 4. Team Quality > As we hire, every person must be exceptional. One bad hire in a 5-person team is devastating. We'll hire slowly and deliberately, prioritizing culture fit and competence. > > 5. Cash Flow Management > We must reach profitability before running out of capital. This means disciplined spending, tracking unit economics religiously, and making data-driven decisions about when to hire and invest."

7. Current Stage & Milestones

Where you are now and what's next. Example for CoodLoom: > "As of October 2025, CoodLoom is in the early growth stage: > > Completed Milestones: > ✓ MVP developed and launched (July 2025) > ✓ First 3 pilot customers acquired (July 2025) > ✓ Reached 15 paying customers (October 2025) > ✓ Processed first $50K in monthly transactions (October 2025) > ✓ Achieved product-market fit validation (August-September 2025) > ✓ Incorporated as Delaware C-Corp (February 2025) > ✓ Raised initial $150K in founder capital > > Upcoming Milestones (Next 6 Months): > ☐ Reach 30 customers (March 2026) > ☐ Launch mobile ordering integration (December 2025) > ☐ Hire first sales representative (February 2026) > ☐ Achieve $10K MRR (March 2026) > ☐ Expand to second geographic market (April 2026) > > 12-Month Goals: > ☐ Grow to 50 customers > ☐ $20K MRR > ☐ Build team to 5 people > ☐ Launch loyalty program feature > ☐ Break-even on operations (excluding founder salaries)"

8. Products & Services Overview

Brief overview (detailed section comes later in plan). Example for CoodLoom: > "CoodLoom offers a cloud-based POS system specifically designed for quick-service restaurants and coffee shops. Our platform includes: > > Core POS Features: > - Fast order entry with customizable menu layouts > - Split payments and tip management > - Cash drawer integration > - Receipt printing and email > - Multi-location support > > Business Management: > - Real-time inventory tracking > - Sales analytics and reporting > - Employee shift management > - Customer database and history > > Advanced Features: > - Mobile ordering integration > - Loyalty program management > - Automatic sales tax calculation > - Integration with accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero) > > Hardware: > - iPad/Android tablet POS terminal > - Receipt printer > - Cash drawer > - Payment terminal (integrated with Stripe) > > Pricing starts at $199/month per location plus 1.5% transaction fees, with hardware available for $1,200 one-time purchase."

Company Description Writing Tips

✅ DO:

  • Be specific: Names, dates, numbers
  • Show progress: What you've accomplished so far
  • Be honest: Acknowledge your stage (early, growth, etc.)
  • Use professional language: But not overly formal
  • Demonstrate thought: This isn't just an idea; it's a real business
  • Include verifiable facts: Incorporation date, location, etc.

❌ DON'T:

  • Exaggerate your stage or accomplishments
  • Use vague language ("significant progress")
  • Hide important information (like founders' inexperience)
  • Make it too long (2-4 pages max)
  • Duplicate information from Executive Summary verbatim

Integrating Executive Summary & Company Description

Flow and Positioning

In Your Business Plan: 1. Cover Page 2. Table of Contents 3. Executive Summary (pages 1-2) 4. Company Description (pages 3-6) 5. Market Analysis (continue...)

Avoiding Redundancy

The Executive Summary and Company Description will have some overlap. Here's how to handle it: Executive Summary: High-level, compelling, forward-looking Company Description: Detailed, factual, provides context Example: Executive Summary: > "Founded by experienced software developers in 2025, CoodLoom now serves 15 customers processing $50K monthly." Company Description: > "CoodLoom was founded in January 2025 by [Name] and [Name], who bring 12 combined years of software development experience from [Companies]. After conducting 50 customer interviews and building an MVP over 4 months, we launched in July 2025. Our first 3 pilot customers validated product-market fit, leading to organic growth to 15 paying customers by October 2025. These customers now process over $50,000 in monthly transactions through CoodLoom, with an average transaction value of $18.50 and 95% uptime." See the difference? Executive Summary is brief; Company Description adds depth.

Final Checklist

Before moving to the next chapter, ensure: ✅ Executive Summary:

  • [ ] 1-2 pages maximum
  • [ ] Includes all 7 key components
  • [ ] Can stand alone (doesn't reference other sections)
  • [ ] Written AFTER all other sections
  • [ ] No typos or errors
  • [ ] Compelling and concise
  • [ ] Includes specific numbers and data

✅ Company Description:

  • [ ] 2-4 pages
  • [ ] Covers all 8 components
  • [ ] Provides legal and structural details
  • [ ] Clearly states mission, vision, values
  • [ ] Shows current stage and progress

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Executive Summary is THE most important section—write it last
  • ✓ It must work as a standalone document in 1-2 pages
  • ✓ Company Description provides context and credibility
  • ✓ Be specific with numbers, dates, and facts
  • ✓ Show what you've accomplished, not just what you plan to do
  • ✓ Mission/vision/values matter—they guide your culture
  • ✓ Avoid jargon and vague language
  • ✓ Make every word count—ruthlessly edit for clarity

Your Executive Summary determines if anyone reads the rest. Make it count! 🎯
