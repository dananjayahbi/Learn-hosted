Fundraising for CoodLoom

Introduction

Should you raise money? When? How much? From whom? Fundraising is a critical decision that can accelerate your growth or distract you from building. This chapter helps you navigate the complex world of startup fundraising.

The Bootstrap vs Fundraise Decision

Should CoodLoom Raise Money?

Arguments for Bootstrapping: ✅ Keep 100% ownership and control ✅ Build sustainable business fundamentals ✅ Avoid investor pressure and timelines ✅ Learn to be capital-efficient ✅ No dilution, full upside is yours ✅ CoodLoom can be profitable Year 1 (rare!) Arguments for Fundraising: ✅ Accelerate growth (hire faster, market more) ✅ Capture market before competitors ✅ Build features faster ✅ Expand to new markets/verticals ✅ Runway to experiment and fail ✅ Access to investor networks and expertise

The CoodLoom Recommendation

Phase 1: Bootstrap to Product-Market Fit (Months 1-12)

  • Goal: 170+ customers, $400K+ ARR
  • Prove the model works
  • Understand unit economics
  • Build strong foundation
  • Stay lean and focused

Phase 2: Raise Seed Round (Months 13-18)

  • After proven traction
  • $500K-$1M seed round
  • Use for: Hiring (sales, dev), marketing scale
  • Goal: $2M ARR by end of Year 2

Phase 3: Series A (Months 24-30)

  • After achieving strong growth
  • $3M-$5M Series A
  • Use for: National expansion, enterprise features
  • Goal: $10M ARR by end of Year 3

Why This Path?:

  • Bootstrap first → Better valuation later
  • Proven traction → More investor interest
  • Understanding of business → Smarter capital deployment
  • Strong position → Negotiate better terms

Types of Funding

1. Self-Funding (Bootstrapping)

Sources:

  • Personal savings
  • Income from current job (side hustle initially)
  • Revenue from early customers
  • Credit cards (risky, use carefully)

CoodLoom Example:

You + Co-founder: $50K initial investment ($25K each)
                        
First 6 months revenue: $50K Total available: $100K → Enough to reach $400K ARR
Pros: ✅ Full control ✅ No dilution ✅ Forces discipline Cons: ❌ Personal financial risk ❌ Slower growth ❌ Limited resources

2. Friends & Family Round

Typical Size: $50K-$250K Timing: Pre-product or early traction Terms: Usually simple (convertible note or SAFE) CoodLoom Scenario:

Raise $100K from friends & family:
                        
    
                        
  • Uncle: $25K
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Former colleague: $25K
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • College friend: $25K
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Family friend: $25K
    • 
                        
                        

                        
Terms: SAFE at $2M valuation cap Converts to equity when you raise Series A
Pros: ✅ Quick to close ✅ Supportive investors ✅ Flexible terms Cons: ❌ Mixing personal relationships with business ❌ May not have startup experience ❌ Limited capital available Best Practices:

  • Treat it professionally (written agreements)
  • Set clear expectations (high risk, could lose money)
  • Don't take money from people who can't afford to lose it
  • Keep them updated regularly

3. Angel Investors

Typical Size: $25K-$250K per angel Timing: After early traction ($50K+ ARR) Who: Wealthy individuals, often former founders CoodLoom Scenario:

Raise $500K from angels:
                        
    
                        
  • 5 angels × $100K each
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Priced round: 20% equity at $2.5M valuation
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Or SAFE/Convertible Note: $3M cap, 20% discount

What Angels Look For:

  • Strong founding team
  • Clear market opportunity
  • Early traction/validation
  • Reasonable valuation
  • Personal connection/interest in space

Finding Angels:

  • AngelList
  • Local angel groups
  • Industry events/conferences
  • LinkedIn (2nd degree connections)
  • Introductions from other founders

Pros: ✅ Smart money (often have startup experience) ✅ Can provide advice and connections ✅ Faster decisions than VCs Cons: ❌ Time-consuming (many individual conversations) ❌ Need multiple angels to raise meaningful amount ❌ Less follow-on capital available

4. Venture Capital (VC)

Stages:

Pre-Seed:    $500K-$2M    (idea to early traction)
                        
Seed:        $1M-$5M      (product-market fit) Series A:    $3M-$15M     (scaling proven model) Series B:    $10M-$50M    (aggressive expansion) Series C+:   $25M+        (market leader, pre-IPO)
CoodLoom Path:

Month 13: Seed Round - $750K at $4M post-money (18.75% dilution)
                        
Month 24: Series A - $3M at $15M post-money (20% dilution) Month 36: Series B - $10M at $50M post-money (20% dilution) Your ownership: Start:      100% Post-Seed:  81.25% Post-A:     65% (81.25% × 80%) Post-B:     52% (65% × 80%)
What VCs Look For:

  • Large market opportunity ($1B+ TAM)
  • Strong team (domain expertise + execution ability)
  • Traction (growing revenue, strong retention)
  • Defensibility (network effects, switching costs, IP)
  • Path to $100M+ revenue (venture scale returns)
  • 10x potential return on investment

Finding VCs:

  • Warm introductions (other founders, angels, advisors)
  • Industry-specific funds (SaaS-focused, B2B, etc.)
  • Micro VCs for seed stage
  • Research: Crunchbase, PitchBook, firm websites

Pros: ✅ Large capital (scale quickly) ✅ Strategic support and connections ✅ Credibility and press ✅ Follow-on funding for future rounds Cons: ❌ Significant dilution ❌ Loss of control (board seats) ❌ Pressure for aggressive growth ❌ Long, difficult process ❌ Not compatible with "lifestyle business"

5. Alternative Funding

Revenue-Based Financing:

  • Borrow money, repay with % of monthly revenue
  • No equity dilution
  • Good for profitable SaaS with predictable revenue
  • Example: Pipe, Clearco, Lighter Capital

Bank Loans:

  • Traditional SBA loans
  • Requires personal guarantee usually
  • Good for hardware/inventory financing
  • Not common for early-stage software

Grants:

  • SBIR/STTR (government grants for R&D)
  • State/local economic development programs
  • Specific to certain industries/demographics

Accelerators:

  • Y Combinator ($500K for ~7%)
  • Techstars ($120K for 6%)
  • Industry-specific accelerators
  • Provide capital + mentorship + connections

Step 7: Negotiating Terms

Key Terms to Understand: Valuation:

Pre-Money Valuation: Company value before investment
                        
Post-Money Valuation: Company value after investment Example: Pre-money: $3M Investment: $750K Post-money: $3.75M Investor ownership: $750K / $3.75M = 20% Founder ownership: 80% Alternative (more common to quote): "We're raising $750K at $4M post-money" → Investor gets $750K / $4M = 18.75% → Pre-money = $4M - $750K = $3.25M
Liquidation Preference:

1x non-participating: Most common, fair
                        
    
                        
  • Investor gets their money back first
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Then everyone shares remaining pro-rata
    • 
                        
                        

                        
Example: Sell company for $10M

                        
    
                        
  • Investor invested $750K, owns 20%
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Investor gets: $750K back + ($10M-$750K) × 20% = $2.6M
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Founders get: $7.4M
    • 
                        
                        

                        
1x participating: Investor-friendly (try to avoid)

                        
    
                        
  • Investor gets money back PLUS pro-rata share
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Investor gets: $750K + ($10M × 20%) = $2.75M
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • Founders get: $7.25M
    • 
                        
                        

                        
⚠️ Watch out for multiples: 2x, 3x (very founder-unfriendly)
Board Composition:

Common structure:
                        
    
                        
  • 2 founder seats
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • 1-2 investor seats
    • 
                        
                        

                        
    
                        
  • 1 independent seat
    • 
                        
                        

                        
Seed stage: Usually no board seat (just observer rights) Series A: Usually 1 board seat Series B+: May add another investor seat
Pro-Rata Rights:

Right to invest in future rounds to maintain ownership %
                        
Example: Seed investor owns 20% Series A: $3M raise Seed investor has right to invest $600K to maintain 20% This is standard and founder-friendly (good investors help in future rounds)
Anti-Dilution Protection:

Full-ratchet: Very investor-friendly (avoid if possible)
                        
    
                        
  • If down round, investor's price adjusts to new lower price
    • 
                        
                        

                        
Weighted-average: Standard, more balanced

                        
    
                        
  • Adjustment based on amount raised and price difference
    • 
                        
                        

                        
Seed stage: Often "no anti-dilution" or "weighted-average"

Step 8: Closing the Round

Steps to Close:

1. Term sheet signed (non-binding, typically)
                        
2. Due diligence (2-6 weeks) 3. Legal docs drafted (1-2 weeks) 4. Final documents signed 5. Money wires (usually within 1-3 days) 6. Celebrate! 🎉 Total timeline: 6-12 weeks from term sheet to close
Legal Documents:

  • Stock Purchase Agreement (SPA)
  • Investors' Rights Agreement
  • Voting Agreement
  • Right of First Refusal Agreement
  • Amended & Restated Certificate of Incorporation

Legal Costs: $15K-$40K

  • Use startup-friendly lawyers
  • Sometimes can negotiate for investor to pay
  • Don't skimp here (bad docs cause problems later)

Common Fundraising Mistakes

Mistake 1: Raising Too Early

❌ Wrong: Raising before any traction ✅ Right: Bootstrap to prove model, then raise to scale

Mistake 2: Raising Too Much

❌ Wrong: "We'll raise $5M just in case" ✅ Right: Raise 18-24 months of runway to next milestone

Mistake 3: Optimizing for Valuation

❌ Wrong: Taking highest valuation offer ✅ Right: Taking best investor even if lower valuation

Mistake 4: Not Negotiating Terms

❌ Wrong: Accepting first term sheet as-is ✅ Right: Understanding all terms, negotiating what matters

Mistake 5: Stopping Work on Business

❌ Wrong: Fundraising becomes full-time job for 6 months ✅ Right: Co-founder split: One fundraises, one runs business

Mistake 6: Taking Money from Anyone

❌ Wrong: "An investor wants to invest, I'll take it!" ✅ Right: Choose investors as carefully as co-founders

Mistake 7: Not Managing the Process

❌ Wrong: Talking to investors one at a time over 6 months ✅ Right: Compressed timeline, create competition and urgency

Mistake 8: Being Unprepared

❌ Wrong: "I'll figure it out as I go" ✅ Right: Polished deck, practiced pitch, data room ready

Key Takeaways

✓ Bootstrap first if possible - Better valuation, stronger position ✓ Raise when ready to scale - After product-market fit, not before ✓ Choose investors carefully - They're partners for 5-10 years ✓ Understand all terms - Valuation isn't everything ✓ Create competition - Multiple offers give you negotiating power ✓ Compress timeline - 6-8 weeks for process, not 6 months ✓ Be transparent - Hiding problems always backfires ✓ Stay focused on business - Don't let fundraising kill momentum ✓ Learn to say no - Wrong investor is worse than no investor ✓ Communicate regularly - Monthly updates minimum

Exercises

Exercise 1: Should You Raise?

Answer these questions: 1. What can't you achieve without funding? 2. What would you use $750K for specifically? 3. What milestones would that achieve? 4. What's the alternative (bootstrap longer)? 5. Final decision: Raise or bootstrap? Why?

Exercise 2: Build Your Pitch Deck

Create a 12-slide deck for CoodLoom:

  • Follow structure outlined above
  • Use real data where possible
  • Projections where needed
  • Practice presenting it (15 minutes max)

Exercise 3: Investor Research

Find 20 potential investors:

  • 5 angels in your network
  • 10 micro VCs/seed funds
  • 5 traditional VCs (Series A stage)

Research each: thesis, recent investments, fund size

Exercise 4: Financial Model for Fundraising

Build a model showing:

  • Base case: Bootstrap path
  • Funded case: With $750K seed round
  • Show difference in outcomes
  • Justify the round

Exercise 5: Practice Pitch

Record yourself pitching:

  • Watch it back (painful but valuable)
  • Note: Filler words, pace, clarity
  • Practice until you can do it smoothly
  • Get feedback from others

Move to Chapter 6: Unit Economics to master the metrics that investors care about most.

Pro Tip: The best fundraising pitch isn't a pitch at all - it's a conversation. Know your business so well that you can discuss it naturally, answer any question, and get investors excited about the vision.
